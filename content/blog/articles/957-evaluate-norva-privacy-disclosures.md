# How to Review Norva Privacy Disclosures Before Deciding

> **In short:** Read the current Norva privacy policy, terms, and legal notice together. Map each relevant data category to its purpose, service-provider role, transfer statement, retention wording, available control, deletion route, and security description. Separate Norva's cloud practices from device, source, payment, and household behavior. Mark unanswered material questions explicitly and use official support before accepting a privacy trade-off.

A privacy policy is not a score by itself. It is evidence for a decision about specific data and workflows. The goal is to understand what is stated, what is conditional, what belongs to another provider, and which unanswered point matters enough to pause.

## Start with your planned use

List the Norva functions you expect to use: account creation, profiles, connected source, catalog organization, progress, history, favorites, preferences, support, payment, and conditional offline access. Exclude features you do not plan to use so the review stays relevant.

Place the resulting privacy requirements in the [Norva evaluation framework](/blog/norva-evaluation-framework/) as observable criteria, not a vague trust score.

## Identify data categories

For each planned function, note the categories described in the current policy: account and contact information, profile labels, usage or viewing-related state, device or technical data, support communications, payment-related handling, and any other disclosed category. Use the policy's current wording rather than inventing a complete data inventory.

Do not write actual email addresses, source details, or viewing history in the review sheet.

## Map purpose and provider roles

Record why each category is described as being used and whether a technical, authentication, hosting, or payment provider is involved. “Provider involved” does not automatically mean the same data is sent to every provider. Mark the exact statement and any uncertainty.

The terms and legal notice help distinguish the service operator from other parties in the workflow.

## Review transfer and location wording

Norva's public policy states that some data may be processed in other countries with appropriate safeguards. That is different from claiming that all data stays in one region or that every transfer has the same mechanism. Record the language that applies to your concern and ask support when a material detail is unclear.

Avoid drawing legal conclusions beyond the published text without qualified advice.

## Read retention and deletion together

Map each relevant retention statement to the available account or deletion control. Norva states that users can request account deletion in the application or through the designated page. Deletion, subscription cancellation, payment records, source-side data, and device-local copies are separate actions.

The [account closure evidence guide](/blog/close-account-keep-cancellation-proof/) helps preserve legitimate cancellation proof without retaining unnecessary personal data.

## Interpret security wording precisely

Norva states that traffic to its cloud is protected in transit by HTTPS and passwords are stored in hashed form by the authentication provider. Do not rewrite that as end-to-end encryption, absolute security, or protection for every local note and connected source.

Use the [new-account security guide](/blog/secure-new-norva-account/) to address password reuse, phishing, and shared-device risks that a privacy policy cannot remove.

## Separate the four privacy layers

Keep four columns:

1. Norva account and cloud practices.
2. Connected-source practices and rights.
3. Device, browser, operating-system, and local-storage behavior.
4. Household sharing, screenshots, notes, and administrator choices.

A concern in one layer should not be attributed automatically to another. Payment-provider terms may also create a separate billing layer.

## Check your practical controls

Confirm which controls are visible and documented now: account details, profile labels, support contact, sign-out, subscription management through the original provider, and deletion route. Do not assume a dashboard, export, or security feature that is not documented.

If a password change follows a concern, use the [trusted-device audit](/blog/trusted-device-audit-after-password-change/) rather than assuming the change ends every session.

## Set decision thresholds

Mark each question acceptable, conditional, unresolved, or unacceptable. A material unresolved question about a planned essential function should pause the decision. Minor unknowns can have an owner and review date. Record the policy URL and access date because disclosures can change.

This is a product decision method, not legal advice or a claim of regulatory certification.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Reading only a summary instead of current official pages.
- Treating provider involvement as identical data sharing.
- Converting HTTPS into an absolute security claim.
- Confusing account deletion with subscription cancellation.
- Ignoring device, source, and household privacy layers.
- Treating this worksheet as legal advice.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does Norva say it sells personal information?

Norva states that it does not sell personal information or use it for third-party advertising. Verify the current policy at the time of decision.

### Does the policy promise all data stays in one region?

No such universal claim should be inferred. The policy describes possible processing in other countries with appropriate safeguards.

### Is deleting an account the same as canceling a subscription?

No. Treat deletion, cancellation with the original provider, source-side data, and device-local copies as separate workflows.
