# What to Check Before Viewing on a Shared Phone

> **In short:** Obtain the owner's permission, confirm the intended service account and profile, and decide whether your activity may affect history, favorites, recommendations, or downloads. Hide sensitive notification previews, verify private audio, avoid saving credentials, and know how to sign out before starting. Return the phone locked and in the agreed state.

A “shared phone” can mean a family device, a borrowed phone, or a personal device temporarily handed to someone else. Those are not the same trust situation. The safest workflow starts with clear permission and uses only authorized accounts, sources, and content.

Add this check to the [mobile session preflight](/blog/one-minute-mobile-session-prep/) whenever the owner, user, or location changes.

## Agree on the boundary first

Ask the owner what is permitted: browsing only, playback, saving an item, changing a subtitle, or downloading when a supported option exists. Do not add accounts, accept new permissions, alter parental controls, change device-wide accessibility settings, or store payment details without explicit approval.

Decide who should remain signed in afterward. If the session uses your account, plan to sign out and remove any temporary credentials. If it uses the owner's account, choose the intended profile and avoid exposing your own personal information through search or autofill.

## Verify account, profile, and source

Read the visible profile name or avatar rather than assuming the last-used state is correct. Confirm the authorized connected source and exact item before playback. On a family profile, check age or content restrictions without trying to bypass them.

Consider how the session may change visible state:

- watch or progress history;
- favorites or saved lists;
- recommendations;
- search history;
- downloaded files or offline data;
- recently used audio and subtitle choices.

If those effects are not acceptable and the current client offers no suitable profile or private mode, use another approved device. Do not promise that local or account history will be isolated unless the service documents it.

## Protect notifications and audio

Incoming banners can reveal messages, appointment details, codes, or names. Android and Apple both provide controls for notification previews, though paths vary by version and device. Review the phone's current settings with the owner; do not silence emergency, alarm, or accessibility alerts that must remain active.

Start playback at low volume and confirm the intended output. Headphones can reconnect to another device or drop back to the speaker. Use the [headphone audio-route check](/blog/verify-headphone-audio-route/) and avoid raising volume until the route is proven.

Keep the screen angled away from bystanders. A physical privacy measure does not replace account controls, and headphones do not protect what remains visible on screen.

## Use device restriction features carefully

Android's app pinning can keep one app in view until it is unpinned; Apple Guided Access can temporarily restrict a device to one app and control certain inputs. When supported and already understood by the owner, these tools can reduce accidental movement into other apps.

They do not create a separate service profile, erase history, guarantee that notifications are hidden, or sign the account out. Configure the exit passcode or biometric with the owner, test how to end the restricted session, and never set a code the owner does not know.

## Avoid persistent local traces

Do not choose “remember me,” save a password, or enable automatic sign-in on someone else's phone. If a supported download is permitted, agree on storage use and deletion before starting. Do not clear the whole app's data or browser history; that can remove the owner's unrelated information.

Instead, remove only the temporary item or account data you are authorized to remove. If the current client provides a sign-out action, use it and then verify that protected details require authentication. If sign-out is not available or does not complete, stop and consult current support rather than deleting broad device data.

## Close and hand back deliberately

At the end:

1. stop playback and close any item details that expose personal choices;
2. remove temporary downloads or saves as agreed;
3. sign out of your account when required;
4. confirm no credential prompt remains filled;
5. restore only settings you changed with permission;
6. close the app if appropriate;
7. lock the phone and return it directly to the owner.

For a public environment, combine this with the [public mobile privacy workflow](/blog/protect-mobile-session-in-public/). Record unresolved sign-out or profile behavior without including passwords, tokens, or sensitive screenshots.

## FAQ

### Is app pinning or Guided Access the same as a private profile?

No. It limits navigation on a device, but it does not necessarily isolate service history, saved items, account data, or notifications.

### Should I clear app data after using a borrowed phone?

Not without the owner's informed approval. Broad clearing can delete unrelated settings or content. Prefer the documented sign-out flow and remove only your authorized temporary data.

### Can I use the owner's profile if they gave me the phone?

Only within the permission and household rules they specified. Confirm the profile, content restrictions, and whether your activity may change their history or recommendations.
