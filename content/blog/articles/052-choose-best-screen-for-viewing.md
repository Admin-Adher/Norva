# How to Choose the Best Screen for Different Viewing Situations

> **In short:** Choose the screen that best fits the moment, not the one with the largest display. A television usually suits shared, focused viewing; a phone suits private and flexible sessions; and a browser is useful when you are already at a computer. Comfort, control, accessibility, connection quality, and likely interruptions should decide.

The “best” screen changes with the room, the people present, and what you need to do while watching. Norva is available on the Web, mobile and TV, with progress and preferences associated with the same account on supported devices. That makes screen choice a practical decision rather than a permanent commitment.

## Match the screen to the social setting

Start with one question: **who is the session for?**

- **One person, frequent movement:** a phone can keep controls close and avoid occupying a shared display.
- **One person already working at a desk:** a browser may be the lowest-friction option because it requires no installation in a compatible browser.
- **Several people in one room:** a television usually gives everyone a common sightline and shared controls.
- **A short preview before a longer session:** use the nearest personal screen, then continue on the main screen when you are ready.

For a direct product-oriented comparison, see [Mobile, Web, or TV: Which Norva Experience Fits Each Moment?](https://norva.tv/blog/norva-mobile-web-tv-comparison/).

## Consider attention and interruption cost

A screen can be physically convenient but mentally disruptive. Phones receive messages and calls. Computers often sit beside work tabs. A television may be in a room where other people need quiet or control of the display.

Estimate the likely session length and interruption rate:

- For a five-minute check, quick access may matter most.
- For a focused session, distance, seating, and a stable control method matter more.
- For stop-and-start viewing, choose the screen that makes pausing and resuming least disruptive.

Cross-device progress is useful here because it lets you treat an interruption as a handoff rather than a restart on supported devices.

## Compare control, distance, and readability

Control should remain predictable from the position where you will sit. On TV, that means remote-friendly navigation and readable focus states. On mobile, it means comfortable touch targets and a screen position you can hold without strain. On Web, it means a keyboard, pointer, or other input method that suits your setup.

Distance changes what is legible. A large screen does not automatically solve small captions, weak contrast, or a cluttered interface. The W3C’s media accessibility guidance emphasises captions, descriptions, transcripts, and usable media players as separate parts of an accessible experience.

If the room makes TV text difficult to read, start with [why sofa readability matters](https://norva.tv/blog/tv-app-sofa-readability/) before changing devices.

## Check the connection and device constraints

Do not judge only by a headline connection speed. Consider where the device sits, whether its connection is stable, and whether other activity competes for the same network. A portable device may move between strong and weak coverage. A fixed screen may have a more predictable position but still depend on local network conditions.

Also consider:

- available battery power;
- whether headphones or speakers are appropriate;
- whether the device supports the media configuration supplied by your authorised source;
- whether offline access is available for that item, device, source, and set of rights.

Norva does not promise universal device, format, or offline compatibility, so verify the actual setup rather than assuming.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Choosing by display size alone ignores distance, sound, controls, and interruptions.
- Treating every mobile, browser, or TV device as equivalent ignores hardware and software differences.
- Assuming a language track, subtitle option, or offline copy exists without checking the source can derail the session.
- Using a work computer for leisure may conflict with organisational policies.
- A scorecard cannot assess an individual’s comfort or access needs; those always take priority.

When the browser appears to be the right option, the guide to [browser-based viewing situations](https://norva.tv/blog/when-to-use-browser-media-player/) adds more context.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is TV always best for a group?

No. It is often convenient for shared sightlines, but room layout, hearing needs, captions, control access, and the preferences of everyone present still matter.

### Should I avoid a phone for longer sessions?

Not as a universal rule. Consider posture, battery, interruptions, display comfort, and personal access needs. Change screens if discomfort or distraction grows.

### Can I choose one default screen for everything?

You can have a favourite, but a situational rule is more useful: personal and mobile, desk-based and interactive, or shared and room-focused. Cross-screen continuity reduces the cost of changing your mind.
