# The Complete Guide to Household Media Profiles

> **In short:** Build profiles around viewing contexts that need separate progress, history, favourites, or preferences. Name them clearly without exposing unnecessary personal data, confirm the active profile before playback, and agree on shared-screen and guest routines. Keep the structure as small as practical, review it after household changes, and never treat a profile as an account password or parental-control boundary.

A good profile system answers one question at a glance: “Whose viewing context will this action change?” It should prevent accidental progress, simplify favourites, and preserve language preferences without creating a maze of near-duplicate choices.

## Understand what a profile does

Norva can preserve progress, history, favourites, and preferences under the same account across supported devices. Profiles provide separate viewing contexts when included in the current plan.

A profile does not automatically:

- secure the account from someone who can access the signed-in device;
- define the number of devices or simultaneous sessions;
- provide parental controls or content classification;
- change rights to the connected media source;
- guarantee that every audio or subtitle track exists;
- hide personal data on a shared screen.

Check current profile capacity and plan terms on the official pricing page rather than relying on an old screenshot.

## Decide what deserves separation

Create a separate context when two viewers regularly need different progress, history, favourites, or language preferences. A shared context may fit when people genuinely watch together and want one combined state.

Use [the separate-versus-shared decision framework](/blog/separate-profiles-or-one-shared-profile-a-decision-framework/) to compare the trade-offs. Do not create a new profile for every occasional visitor or one-time session without checking capacity and cleanup implications.

## Count contexts, not just people

A person who always watches with the household may not need a separate context. Two people sharing a device but watching independently often do. A recurring group may need one shared context if everyone agrees.

The [household profile count worksheet](/blog/how-many-household-profiles-do-you-actually-need/) turns viewing patterns into a minimum viable structure. Plan capacity is a constraint, not a reason to invent unlimited profiles.

## Name profiles for recognition and privacy

Use short, distinguishable labels that make sense from across a shared TV. Avoid email addresses, birthdays, account roles, medical information, or other unnecessary personal details.

Colour and avatar can support recognition, but the text label should carry the meaning. Similar icons may be hard to distinguish under remote focus or on a small display.

## Establish the playback preflight

Before starting or resuming media:

1. confirm the signed-in account;
2. read the active profile label;
3. confirm the item or episode;
4. verify version and approximate progress;
5. check audio and subtitles;
6. play once.

The [active-profile check](/blog/how-to-confirm-the-active-profile-before-playback/) prevents most wrong-context progress before it begins.

## Handle shared screens deliberately

A household TV is not automatically private. Decide who may enter account settings, whether the account remains signed in, and what the next user should see. At handoff, say “screen, profile, item” so the profile becomes part of the request.

For guests, use an available profile only when the current plan and household agreement support it. Do not claim a dedicated guest mode unless the current product visibly provides one.

## Review favourites and preferences per profile

Favourites should represent the profile's actual intent. Language and subtitle preferences may follow the profile, but available tracks still depend on the source and media.

When several people share one profile, agree before removing a favourite or changing a persistent preference. A shared profile is shared ownership, not a shortcut around communication.

## Recover from mistakes without causing another one

If playback begins under the wrong profile, pause immediately and record the item, version, and position. Do not delete another person's full history or change their profile to conceal the mistake. Use visible correction controls only when verified; otherwise contact support.

A wrong favourite or language preference should be investigated as a specific state change, not a reason to reset the whole account.

## Common mistakes and limitations

Avoid one profile per device, names that reveal sensitive data, identical labels, treating icons as identity, confusing profile count with concurrent use, and creating a profile without an owner or purpose.

Profiles depend on current plan capacity and product controls. Source metadata, tracks, rights, and device support remain separate. A profile structure cannot guarantee privacy on a device controlled by someone else.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does every household member need a profile?

No. Create one when a persistent viewing context needs separation. Shared viewing may justify one agreed shared profile.

### Can a profile protect account settings?

Do not treat it as a password or security boundary. Device access and account authentication remain separate.

### Should profile names be real names?

They can be, but use only enough information for clear recognition. A nickname or role may be more privacy-conscious on a shared screen.

### How often should profiles be reviewed?

Use real triggers such as a changed household, viewing pattern, privacy need, or plan. No universal calendar is necessary.
