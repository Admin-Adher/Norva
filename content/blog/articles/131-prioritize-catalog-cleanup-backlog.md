# How to Prioritize a Media Catalog Cleanup Backlog

> **In short:** Prioritise cleanup that restores an important viewing task, prevents further disorder, or unblocks other work. Do not rank items by annoyance alone. For every candidate, record impact, dependency risk, evidence quality, reversibility, effort, and blocked work. Handle high-impact, well-understood, reversible changes first; investigate destructive or poorly evidenced changes before scheduling them.

A backlog can mix harmless naming inconsistencies with broken series relationships, failed imports, duplicates, and obsolete categories. Treating all of them as equal turns cleanup into an endless cosmetic project.

## Start with a bounded inventory

Build the backlog from [a pre-cleanup audit](/blog/audit-library-before-cleanup/), support notes, failed retrieval tasks, and recent source changes. Write one observable problem per card. “Catalogue is messy” is not actionable; “search returns two indistinguishable versions of 14 titles” is.

Give each card:

- a stable ID;
- affected section, source, or relationship;
- one example and an estimated scope;
- the viewer task that fails;
- evidence location;
- proposed action and rollback route;
- owner and next review date.

Separate discovery from action. A suspected issue belongs in an investigation queue until evidence supports a change.

## Use the six-factor priority card

Rate each factor low, medium, or high and add a short reason:

| Factor | Question | A high rating means |
|---|---|---|
| Viewer impact | Which real task is affected? | Search, browsing, playback choice, or continuity regularly fails |
| Dependency risk | Can the issue spread or block later work? | Imports, mappings, or relationships may compound the problem |
| Evidence quality | Do we know the cause and scope? | Samples, logs, counts, and ownership agree |
| Reversibility | Can the change be undone? | Baseline, mapping, and tested rollback exist |
| Effort | How large is the safe batch? | Many records or manual decisions are required |
| Blocked work | What becomes possible afterwards? | Several approved tasks depend on this one |

This is a decision aid, not arithmetic. High impact plus high evidence and high reversibility is a strong early candidate. High impact with weak evidence should trigger investigation. High effort is not automatically low priority; it may need decomposition through [the batch-cleaning method](/blog/clean-large-catalog-in-batches/).

## Apply vetoes before ranking

Pause a card when any of these is true:

- source ownership or authorisation is unclear;
- the proposed action would remove data without a recovery path;
- affected personal context has not been mapped;
- the scope cannot be counted or sampled;
- a migration or import is still running;
- success cannot be tested.

A veto does not mean “never.” It defines the missing work needed before prioritisation can be trusted.

## Build four practical queues

Use these queues instead of one long numbered list:

1. **Stabilise now:** active failures that can worsen, such as a partial import or broken mapping.
2. **Repair next:** high-impact, understood issues with reversible solutions.
3. **Investigate:** suspected causes, destructive proposals, or conflicting evidence.
4. **Improve later:** cosmetic consistency and low-impact refinements.

Limit active work. Complete and validate one batch before opening another. The National Archives recommends inventory information as a foundation for understanding records; the same principle makes a personal catalogue backlog evidence-led rather than impression-led.

## Define completion before work begins

For each selected card, record a measurable finish line: expected count, sample, retrieval test, affected devices, rollback test, and observation period. Then follow [the reversible cleanup process](/blog/make-catalog-cleanup-reversible/) and add the result to [the quality checklist](/blog/catalog-cleanup-quality-checklist/).

Re-rank after each batch. A resolved dependency may lower several cards, while new evidence may split one card into different causes.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Prioritising the most visible problem rather than the most consequential one.
- Adding urgency labels without reasons.
- Combining several causes on one card.
- Using a numeric total that hides a destructive action.
- Keeping hundreds of tasks “in progress.”
- Measuring output by records changed instead of tasks restored.

## Frequently asked questions

### Should quick wins always come first?

No. A quick, reversible improvement can build confidence, but it should not distract from an active issue that compounds damage or blocks essential work.

### How often should the backlog be reviewed?

Review it after every completed batch and after a source, mapping, or household workflow changes. A lightweight monthly review is usually easier than a major rescue.

### Can several people score the same backlog?

Yes. Ask them to write reasons independently, then discuss disagreements. The useful output is the evidence behind the rating, not consensus around a number.
