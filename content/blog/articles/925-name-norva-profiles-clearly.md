# How to Name Norva Profiles Clearly and Privately

> **In short:** Name each profile for a recurring viewing context rather than a complete identity. Use a short neutral role, time, or preference cue that is unique inside the household. Exclude full names, emails, birth dates, precise locations, health or belief details, account identifiers, and source information. Check the label on a shared screen, document its owner and purpose, and review it when the context changes.

A profile name appears where household members may see it. It should help people select the right context without becoming a miniature identity record. The safest useful label says enough for the household and very little to anyone else.

## Begin with purpose

Write the profile's recurring purpose in a private worksheet: personal viewing, shared evening viewing, or a recurring language preference where supported. Then reduce that description to the minimum useful label.

Create the purpose itself with the [first household profile guide](/blog/create-first-household-profile/) before choosing a name. Naming cannot repair a profile whose role is unclear.

## Prefer neutral naming patterns

Useful patterns include a household role, a shared context, a broad time cue, or a non-sensitive preference. Examples such as “Shared,” “Guest,” “Evening,” or “French Audio” may work when they are accurate, understandable, and not sensitive in that household.

Avoid using an example mechanically. “Guest” is poor if several recurring guest contexts exist, and a language cue is poor when it reveals more than the household wishes to show.

## Exclude direct identifiers

Do not use a full legal name, email address, phone number, account ID, birth date, school, workplace, or precise location. Initials may still identify someone in a small household or on a photographed screen, so assess context rather than treating initials as automatically private.

The [privacy-safe display-name guide](/blog/choose-privacy-safe-display-name/) offers a broader account-level comparison.

## Exclude sensitive traits

Do not build names from health conditions, disability, religion, politics, sexual orientation, medication, exact age, or another sensitive characteristic. Even when the profile owner suggested it, the label may appear on a shared screen or in support evidence later.

Use an operational cue instead. A neutral time or shared-use label often communicates enough without stating why the context exists.

## Keep source and access details out

Never place a source address, provider login, username, password hint, subscription identifier, or authorization claim in the profile name. A profile does not prove source rights and should not be used as an access-control label.

Source administration belongs in a protected process such as the [household source handoff](/blog/household-admin-source-handoff/), not in the visible profile picker.

## Make names unique within the household

Two profiles named “Shared” create selection errors. Add a neutral differentiator tied to purpose, such as a broad context or preference, without adding identity data. Keep the set visually distinct at a glance.

Do not solve a collision by appending a birth year, room number, email fragment, or source name.

## Check shared-screen readability

View the name through the current Norva interface on the supported shared screen. Check truncation, confusing abbreviations, similar labels, and characters that may be hard to distinguish from a distance. Record the visible result without photographing other private profile names.

The TV experience is designed for remote navigation, but this does not guarantee that every long label will fit every presentation. Favor concise names.

## Assign ownership and a review trigger

Record who may rename or delete the profile and when the label should be reviewed. Useful triggers include a purpose change, household administration handoff, duplicate name, unwanted disclosure, or profile retirement.

The [account security guide](/blog/secure-new-norva-account/) helps keep naming records separate from credentials.

## Rename cautiously

Before renaming, confirm that the intended profile is active and record its purpose, not its private history. After the change, check one supported screen and ask the affected household members whether the label remains clear. Do not create a duplicate profile merely to correct a name unless current official guidance requires it.

## Quick naming test

Before saving, ask five questions:

1. Does the label identify the correct recurring context?
2. Would it remain acceptable if visible in a household photo?
3. Does it avoid contact, location, age, health, belief, and source details?
4. Is it distinct from every other profile?
5. Can it be read and understood on the shared screen?

If any answer is no or uncertain, shorten or generalize the label and test again.

## Common naming mistakes

- Copying a full name or email address.
- Adding a birth year or exact room location to resolve duplicates.
- Encoding a medical, religious, or other sensitive trait.
- Including source or account details.
- Choosing labels that look identical when truncated.
- Keeping an obsolete name after the profile purpose changes.

## Frequently asked questions

### Are initials always privacy-safe?

No. Initials may identify someone in a small household or combined context. Use the least identifying label that still prevents selection errors.

### Can a profile name describe a language preference?

It can when the cue is useful and acceptable to the household. Actual languages and subtitles still depend on the source and media.

### Should profile names match device or room names?

Usually not. Profiles represent viewing contexts, not devices. A precise room label can also reveal location information without improving the profile's purpose.
