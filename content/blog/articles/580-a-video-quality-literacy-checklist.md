# A Video Quality Literacy Checklist

> **In short:** Name the visible symptom and exact scene, then trace source, encode, delivery, decoding, output, display, and room in order. Record verified resolution, bitrate context, codec, frame rate, color and dynamic range without treating any one value as a score. Change one layer, repeat, preserve unknowns, and report rights-safe evidence.

Use this checklist to route a question, not to certify every source or device. [The complete video-quality guide](/blog/the-complete-guide-to-understanding-video-quality/) explains the chain behind each step.

## 1. Define the task and symptom

- Name the viewer goal: compare versions, diagnose a stall, read a badge, or fix a path.
- Record title and version without private source addresses.
- Capture exact scene and timecode.
- Describe blocking, banding, softness, tint, crop, crushed shadows, raised blacks, stutter, smearing, or another observable pattern.
- Separate perception, diagnostic value, and hypothesis.

## 2. Verify the source and encode

- Record source lineage only when known.
- Capture dimensions, codec and profile, bitrate definition, frame rate, aspect ratio, bit depth, color, and dynamic range where exposed.
- Mark unavailable properties unknown.
- Do not infer native detail, capture rate, or creative intent from a filename or badge.
- Check whether prior scaling or compression may already limit the source.

## 3. Inspect scene complexity

- Include fine texture, gradients, dark detail, highlights, color, faces, slow pans, fast motion, grain, smoke or water, graphics, and credits.
- Observe normal speed before paused inspection.
- Use multiple scenes; one shot can favor a particular grade or encode.

## 4. Check delivery and adaptation

- Record local, buffered, or network playback context.
- Note startup delay, buffer events, visible quality changes, selected representation, and safe throughput evidence where exposed.
- Do not use a generic speed test as proof of the player's full path.
- Use [the network-versus-encode guide](/blog/network-problem-or-encoded-quality-separate-the-symptoms/) for a symptom timeline.

## 5. Check decoding

- Record device, operating system, app or browser version, and media configuration.
- Separate codec support from profile, dimensions, rate, bit depth, protection, and smoothness.
- Capture errors, fallback, dropped frames, and hardware-acceleration status only through official diagnostics.
- Do not weaken security controls to make a file play.

## 6. Map output and intermediate devices

- Record output resolution, refresh context, color and dynamic-range state where verified.
- Include receiver, switch, adapter, and display input.
- Bypass one layer only when safe and authorised, then restore it.
- Disclose coupled changes.

## 7. Check display processing

- Record display model, input, picture mode, aspect, overscan, scaling, sharpness, noise reduction, motion processing, tone mapping, and automatic adjustments.
- Preserve original values.
- Change only one understood control.
- Retest at ordinary distance after close inspection.

## 8. Include the viewing environment

- Record seat, distance, angle, room light, and reflections when relevant.
- Keep the room fixed during technical comparisons.
- Do not prescribe universal darkness or brightness.
- Keep viewer feedback separate rather than averaging disagreement.

## 9. Read badges cautiously

- Find the official current definition.
- Separate available media, active selection, decode, output, display, and visible result.
- Do not treat resolution as bitrate or HDR as resolution.
- Confirm current Norva labels through official information.

## 10. Compare responsibly

- Match edit, scene, timecode, device, path, display, and room.
- Conceal labels or vary order where practical.
- Report detail, gradients, darks, color, motion, framing, and artifacts separately.
- Repeat meaningful differences.
- Use [the responsible comparison protocol](/blog/how-to-compare-two-video-versions-responsibly/) before declaring a preference.

## 11. Protect rights and privacy

- Use only sources the viewer owns or is authorised to use.
- Remove credentials, source addresses, tokens, account identifiers, viewing history, and faces.
- Do not share copyrighted clips without permission.
- Use timecodes and safe descriptions when captures are inappropriate.

## 12. Report limits

- State exactly what was tested.
- Mark unknown metadata and uncontrolled differences.
- Avoid universal codec, bitrate, display, or network claims.
- Distinguish informal household observation from ITU-style structured assessment.
- Norva is software for organising and playing compatible authorised sources, not a catalogue provider or source-quality certifier.

## Frequently asked questions

### Which number best represents video quality?

No single number captures the whole source-to-viewer chain. Use the properties relevant to the observed task.

### Should display settings be changed first?

First preserve the baseline and locate the symptom. Change one understood layer only after the path is mapped.

### Can this checklist prove formal quality compliance?

No. It supports reproducible reasoning; formal evaluation needs an applicable standard, controlled scope, and qualified method.
