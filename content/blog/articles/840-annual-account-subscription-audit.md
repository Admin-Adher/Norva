# An Annual Account and Subscription Audit

> **In short:** Once a year, verify the account owner, sign-in email, recovery security, profiles, supported devices, connected-source authorization, original billing provider, plan, renewal status, displayed price and tax, receipts, privacy controls, current notices, local downloads, and open support cases. Record evidence dates and action owners, never credentials. Remove obsolete access, fix discrepancies, and confirm closure readiness. Repeat sooner after security, household, device, source, provider, plan, or policy changes.

An annual audit is a minimum structured checkpoint, not a reason to ignore urgent changes during the year. It gives the household one complete view across separate systems.

## Confirm ownership and recovery

Verify who administers the Norva account, who controls the sign-in inbox, and whether recovery details remain current. Use unique passwords and stronger authentication where supported. Do not copy secrets into the audit.

If administration must change, use the [household handoff guide](/blog/transfer-household-account-administration/) instead of sharing credentials.

## Review profiles and devices

List recurring profile users and remove obsolete profiles only after preserving needed state. Inventory televisions, mobile devices, browsers, and local downloads. Sign out or revoke devices that no longer belong to authorized household users where current controls allow.

Check operating-system and application updates and screen-lock settings.

## Reconfirm source authorization

Verify that the connected source remains compatible and that the account owner still owns it or is authorized to use it. Review source security and recovery separately from Norva.

Remove stale source settings without recording credentials in the audit register.

## Verify billing at the provider

Open the original provider's subscription page and record exact plan, status, next renewal or expiry, price, interval, currency, and tax wording. The [renewal-date guide](/blog/document-next-renewal-date/) provides a provenance record.

Do not rely solely on an in-app entitlement display for billing facts.

## Reconcile receipts and reminders

Confirm that receipts correspond to provider transactions and that the renewal reminder points to the current date. The [receipt-organization guide](/blog/organize-subscription-receipts/) minimizes payment data while preserving useful evidence.

Remove redundant copies and former administrators from archive access.

## Review privacy controls and notices

Compare current policy date, categories, purposes, providers, retention, security claims, transfer wording, and account controls with the prior audit. Use the [privacy-control routine](/blog/privacy-controls-review-routine/) for event-driven checks between annual reviews.

Record changed wording as a question until product behavior or official support confirms its effect.

## Close support and security gaps

Review open cases, recent lockouts, suspicious messages, email changes, and entitlement incidents. Confirm that case evidence is redacted and that resolved files no longer need broad access.

CISA recommends strong unique passwords, multi-factor authentication, phishing recognition, and software updates.

## Test closure readiness

Know where to cancel with the original provider, how to preserve proof, how to disconnect the source, which devices contain local media, and how to request account closure. Readiness does not mean closing; it prevents a rushed exit later.

## Compare with the previous audit

Mark what changed, why it changed, and whether the change was expected. A new device or provider should have an owner and evidence; a missing profile or receipt needs investigation. Keep trend notes concise and non-sensitive. The value of an annual cycle comes from detecting drift, not merely checking the same boxes again.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Treating an annual audit as sufficient after an incident.
- Storing passwords or receipt secrets in the register.
- Reviewing Norva but not the source or provider.
- Keeping obsolete devices and profiles.
- Copying last year's renewal date.
- Ignoring policy and processor changes.
- Finding problems without assigning owners and dates.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is once a year enough?

Use the annual audit as a baseline and repeat relevant checks after material security, household, device, source, provider, plan, or policy events.

### Should the audit include passwords?

No. Record that controls were verified, along with date and owner, but never place passwords, codes, or tokens in the register.

### Why test closure readiness if I am staying?

Knowing cancellation, evidence, device, source, and deletion paths prevents confusion during a future urgent change.
