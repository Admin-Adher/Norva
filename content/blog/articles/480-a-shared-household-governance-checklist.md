# A Shared Household Governance Checklist

> **In short:** A complete household checklist names an account coordinator and backup, confirms source ownership and authorisation, inventories trusted devices, assigns profile owners, separates credentials, defines privacy and accessibility practices, controls disruptive changes, prepares incident handoffs, and covers onboarding, offboarding, device disposal, and periodic review. Every exception needs an owner and date.

Use this checklist to test whether the household can operate and recover safely. It is not a license to inspect personal viewing or a substitute for source terms, device guidance, or Norva's current documentation.

## Choose a simple status system

Mark each line:

- **confirmed:** owner and current process are known;
- **needs action:** a specific gap exists;
- **not applicable:** the household does not use that context;
- **control unverified:** the plan depends on a feature or setting that must be checked.

Add an action owner and due date to every gap. “Someone should fix this” is not a completion state.

## Account and billing roles

- [ ] Account coordinator and backup are named.
- [ ] Billing contact knows where to verify current plan terms.
- [ ] Permanent account changes require the appropriate owner's consent.
- [ ] A continuity note exists without copied passwords.
- [ ] Support contact and identity-verification routes are known.

The coordinator maintains process; they do not automatically own every source, profile, device, or private record.

## Connected sources and credentials

- [ ] Each source has a named owner.
- [ ] The owner confirms they may connect and use it.
- [ ] Credentials stay in an owner-controlled process.
- [ ] Reconnection responsibility is documented without source addresses.
- [ ] Removal has an approval and rollback plan where available.

Norva is a media player and organiser for compatible sources users connect; it is not a catalogue of media. Household governance cannot create rights the source owner does not hold.

## Devices and screens

- [ ] Trusted devices have custodian, location, and purpose.
- [ ] Guest and borrowed screens have a sign-out and reopen check.
- [ ] Sold, returned, or repaired devices follow manufacturer guidance.
- [ ] Unknown-device evidence is preserved before changes.
- [ ] No unsupported session-control capability is assumed.

## Profiles and viewing context

- [ ] Every profile has a current owner or defined shared purpose.
- [ ] Personal and shared profiles are distinguishable.
- [ ] Favorites, history, and progress are not used for surveillance.
- [ ] Accessibility preferences are recorded minimally and consensually.
- [ ] Profile onboarding and offboarding have owners.

Profiles organise context. They are not passwords, parental controls, concurrency limits, content rights, or guaranteed privacy boundaries.

## Changes and troubleshooting

- [ ] One coordinator owns each incident.
- [ ] Initial state is captured before changes.
- [ ] One variable changes at a time.
- [ ] Disruptive actions require approval and a safe rationale.
- [ ] Results and rollback are recorded.
- [ ] Identity, privacy, rights, or rollback uncertainty triggers a stop.

Use the [one-page household media agreement](/blog/create-a-one-page-household-media-agreement/) to make these rules visible before an incident.

## Original evidence: readiness board

Create a board with columns for checklist item, status, evidence location, owner, due date, and next verification. Evidence location should point to an authorised record, not duplicate credentials or private screenshots.

Test the board with one routine scenario: a new shared TV, a profile handoff, or a source-owner absence. If the household cannot identify the decision owner and stop condition, the item is not confirmed.

## Lifecycle and review

- [ ] New members receive role and privacy guidance.
- [ ] Departing members receive profile and account offboarding.
- [ ] Device disposal has custody confirmation.
- [ ] Major changes enter a minimal change log.
- [ ] Open support issues have a handoff packet.
- [ ] A periodic review checks exceptions, not personal activity.

Run the [quarterly household account review](/blog/how-to-run-a-quarterly-household-account-review/) or choose another explicit cadence. Review sooner after moves, source-owner changes, unknown-device incidents, or device disposal.

## Interpret the result

Count unresolved actions only to manage work, not to score people. Prioritise credential exposure, unknown access, unclear source authority, and irreversible changes. Then handle organisation and convenience gaps.

For the principles behind each line, use the [complete household governance guide](/blog/the-complete-guide-to-shared-household-media-governance/).

## Common mistakes and limitations

Avoid checking boxes without owner confirmation, storing passwords as evidence, hardcoding plan limits, treating profiles as security boundaries, and collecting histories to prove “usage.”

The checklist verifies household readiness at a point in time. Current product, source, device, and plan behavior still needs official confirmation.

## Frequently asked questions

### Must every household use every checklist item?

No. Mark genuinely irrelevant contexts not applicable, but document why high-risk areas such as credentials or device disposal do not apply.

### Should the checklist contain account passwords?

No. Record ownership and recovery routes, never the secret itself.

### When is the checklist complete?

When every applicable item is confirmed or has a named action owner and date, and unverified controls are not being treated as facts.
