# Run an Annual Media Account Security Audit

> **In short:** Annually—and after major changes—review current Norva terms and privacy information, recovery email, unique credentials, devices and sessions, compatible authorized sources, household roles, support evidence, and incident readiness. Verify each control through an official destination, record gaps without secrets, assign an owner and due date, and require evidence before closure. An annual review is a baseline, not a reason to wait when a loss or suspicious event occurs.

A periodic audit catches quiet drift: retired devices remain listed, a recovery inbox changed owner, a guest still has vault access, or a source authorization is no longer current. The review should produce decisions, not merely a checked box.

## Freeze the scope and evidence date

List the Norva account, recovery email, password manager, device-platform accounts, all supported viewing devices, and each compatible source the account owner owns or is authorized to use. Record the date and official documentation consulted because interfaces and policies change.

Start from the [complete media-player security checklist](/blog/media-player-security-checklist/). Add any household-specific account, device, travel, repair, or accessibility requirements.

## Reconfirm terms, privacy, and authorization

Read current Norva terms, privacy information, and support guidance. Confirm that account ownership and connected-source use still match those rules. Norva is software for organizing and playing compatible media; it does not supply the user's media.

Do not infer compatibility or a source entitlement from past success. Verify current authorization with the source owner and source's official documentation.

## Audit recovery and credentials

Review the recovery email's unique password, devices, sessions, recovery methods, forwarding, and delegated access. Confirm that the approved password manager remains recoverable and that backup ownership is current.

Use manager reports or record metadata to find reuse without exposing password values. Every service should have a unique credential and known official destination. Replace only exposed, reused, or policy-required values through the relevant service.

## Review devices and sessions

Classify devices as active, shared, temporary, retired, lost, repaired, or unknown. Review sessions where the current service provides such controls. Remove access that is no longer justified and verify the result.

Run the [viewing-device security audit](/blog/viewing-device-security-audit/) for operating-system updates, application updates, screen lock, notifications, permissions, official installation, network trust, offline data, repair, and disposal readiness.

## Check household roles and sharing

Confirm primary and backup owners for recovery, credentials, devices, sources, support, incident response, and offboarding. Ordinary viewing should not require administrative credential access.

Review password-manager sharing, former household members, guest devices, and contact routes. Test that the backup can locate official recovery instructions without revealing a secret.

## Review incidents and support evidence

Use the [non-sensitive event log](/blog/household-account-security-event-log/) to identify repeated alerts, unfinished remote actions, or missing closure evidence. Ensure screenshots sent to support were minimized and redacted, and apply a retention decision to sensitive originals.

Rehearse the [credential-exposure incident plan](/blog/credential-exposure-incident-plan/) as a tabletop scenario. Confirm who secures recovery, who handles each source, and how the household distinguishes requested, pending, and confirmed actions.

## Prioritize and verify remediation

Rate findings by plausible impact and urgency: exposed recovery, unknown sessions, lost devices, or credential reuse deserve prompt action. Cosmetic labels or documentation gaps can follow, but still need an owner.

For every finding, record the intended action, official instruction, owner, due date, and completion evidence. Do not close a finding because someone says it was handled; verify the resulting account or device state.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Treating last year's screenshots as current evidence.
- Auditing Norva but ignoring recovery and sources.
- Recording passwords or codes in the control register.
- Closing findings without verifying the final state.
- Giving all gaps the same priority.
- Waiting for the annual date after a live incident.
- Assuming an unsupported device receives current security updates.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is once a year often enough?

It is a baseline. Review sooner after loss, repair, disposal, household changes, suspicious alerts, source changes, or credential exposure.

### Should the audit force password changes?

Not by default. Prioritize unique passwords and change values that are exposed, reused, or subject to a current service requirement.

### What proves a finding is closed?

Current official account or device evidence shows the intended state, the owner recorded confirmation, and no required follow-up remains.
