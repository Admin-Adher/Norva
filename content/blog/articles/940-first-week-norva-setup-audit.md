# Audit Your Norva Setup After the First Week

> **In short:** After one week, compare the intended setup with real use across account security, profiles, source connection, catalog, playback, preferences, progress, favorites, devices, privacy, and conditional offline needs. Keep verified components unchanged. Rank unresolved items by impact and evidence, assign one owner, and change one variable at a time.

The first week reveals patterns that a single onboarding session cannot. A source may import correctly but expose inconsistent metadata; a shared profile name may confuse the household; or synchronization may need a controlled retest. Use the [complete onboarding journey](/blog/norva-onboarding-complete-journey/) as the baseline, not as a reason to restart everything.

## Gather evidence before opinions

Collect the privacy-safe records created during setup: account checklist, source authorization result, import timeline, catalog sample, playback trace, preference checks, synchronization traces, device review, and privacy register. Do not collect passwords, tokens, private source addresses, live codes, or a complete viewing history.

Ask each household participant for one successful workflow and one recurring friction point. Separate repeated evidence from a one-time preference.

## Recheck account and profiles

Confirm that recovery and authentication controls remain under the account holder's control. Reconcile current devices with known events. Review profile purpose, labels, and household access. Remove obsolete test profiles or sessions only through current supported controls and only after confirming their identity.

Do not combine profiles merely to reduce clutter if the household relies on separate progress, favorites, or preferences.

## Review source and catalog outcomes

Confirm the source remains authorized, expected, and reachable through its own workflow. Compare a small current sample with the original result. Use the [catalog sample verification](/blog/verify-first-catalog-sample/) rather than judging the whole catalog from a count or artwork grid.

Record whether differences stem from a source change, active filter, grouping state, stale view, or an unknown cause. Do not edit source metadata and Norva settings simultaneously.

## Review actual playback use

Choose one frequently used screen and one secondary screen. Test a known item, basic controls, and a clean exit. Review any failures by media type, version, device, network, and time rather than reporting "playback unreliable" without a trace.

Language and subtitle checks should use items known to contain the preferred tracks. Availability remains dependent on the source, media, device, rights, and supported behavior.

## Review cross-screen continuity

Inspect one progress transition and one favorite add-remove transition under the same account and profile. Record displayed and actual states separately. If only one direction fails, preserve that asymmetry; it can be diagnostically useful.

Avoid publishing universal timing claims from household tests. The audit establishes whether the workflow is dependable enough for this household under declared conditions.

## Review privacy and retention

Check shared screens, device labels, profile names, support evidence, and any conditional offline copies. Delete unnecessary recordings and expired setup notes. Keep a category-level decision register rather than duplicating viewing or account data.

Update the review date for current official privacy and support pages. A first-week audit should not rely solely on cached policy text.

## Prioritize by impact and reversibility

Classify findings:

- Critical: unfamiliar account access or uncertain source authorization.
- High: repeatable inability to use a core intended workflow.
- Medium: confusing organization, preference, or navigation behavior with a workaround.
- Low: cosmetic inconsistency or a one-time observation.

Address critical security or authorization issues first. For other findings, prefer reversible changes and preserve the previous verified state.

## Escalate one clear case

If evidence supports a Norva issue, follow the [official-help guide](/blog/find-official-norva-help/). Send one minimum reproducible case with sanitized evidence. Do not bundle unrelated observations or expose source credentials.

## Common first-week audit mistakes

- Resetting a mostly verified setup.
- Treating catalog count as the only success measure.
- Mixing several configuration changes in one retest.
- Ignoring household confusion because playback starts.
- Keeping raw support recordings indefinitely.
- Escalating opinions without reproducible evidence.

## Frequently asked questions

### Should the entire onboarding process be repeated?

Usually not. Reuse existing evidence and retest only domains that are unresolved, changed, or important in actual use.

### What if household members want different preferences?

Review profile purposes and supported profile limits. Keep distinct contexts when they prevent progress, favorite, or language conflicts.

### When is the audit complete?

When critical issues are resolved, high-impact unknowns have owners and dates, and the household can explain the current setup, limitations, and official help route.
