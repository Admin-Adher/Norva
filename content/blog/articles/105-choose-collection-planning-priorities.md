# A Priority Framework for Planning a Media Collection

> **In short:** Prioritise work packages by household impact, frequency of the problem, risk to access or data, dependency value, maintenance effort, and reversibility. Address urgent access risks first, then work that unlocks several later tasks. Do not let easy cosmetic cleanup displace a smaller number of high-impact findability problems.

A collection backlog often mixes unlike work: missing metadata, duplicated categories, uncertain sources, broken searches, archive decisions, and cosmetic naming. One ranked list hides those differences. A multi-factor framework makes the trade-offs visible.

## Turn the backlog into work packages

Write tasks at a size that can be tested and finished. Replace “organise the library” with packages such as:

- inventory three authorised sources;
- define language fields for one media type;
- merge two proven duplicate categories;
- move inactive items into a reviewable archive view;
- test five household retrieval tasks;
- document a source that may change soon.

Every package needs an owner, scope, expected result, and rollback route.

## Score six decision factors

Use 1 for low, 2 for medium, and 3 for high. The numbers are discussion prompts, not objective truth.

### Household impact

How strongly does the problem affect a real viewing task? Use evidence from [findability goals](/blog/set-library-findability-goals/) rather than personal annoyance alone.

### Frequency

How often does the problem occur? A small issue repeated daily can matter more than a large edge case encountered once a year.

### Access or data risk

Could delay make authorised material, metadata, or context harder to recover? NDSA preservation guidance emphasises knowing what content exists, where it is stored, and how integrity and formats are managed. Adapt the risk principle without treating a household collection as an institutional archive.

### Dependency value

Does this package unblock other work? A source inventory or scope decision often enables several later cleanups.

### Maintenance effort

Will the new rule require recurring work? Score higher effort as a cost, not a benefit. Use the [maintenance effort estimator](/blog/estimate-library-maintenance-effort/) before adding detailed fields.

### Reversibility

Can the change be tested and rolled back? Prefer reversible pilots when impact and urgency are otherwise similar.

## Separate urgent risk from normal priority

Do not average away a genuine access risk. Create a first lane for:

- authorisation or ownership uncertainty;
- failing storage or unreadable media;
- source access ending soon;
- missing recovery details;
- destructive changes already in progress.

Pause normal organisation until the relevant material and context are protected through an authorised process. The Library of Congress personal archiving guidance recommends identifying important material, organising it, keeping copies in different places, and reviewing storage over time.

## Calculate, then challenge the result

One possible worksheet is:

`priority discussion score = impact + frequency + risk + dependency - effort + reversibility`

Do not treat the total as an automatic order. Review:

- Does a legal or rights issue block the work?
- Is the evidence strong enough?
- Does one person carry all maintenance?
- Would a lower-ranked package unlock the top item?
- Can the work be piloted safely?

Record the reason for overrides in a [library decision log](/blog/create-library-decision-log/).

Write one sentence of evidence under every score of 3. If no evidence exists, mark it “unknown” and investigate rather than inventing precision. Pilot the leading reversible package on a representative sample.

## Use a balanced first sequence

A practical sequence is:

1. protect urgent access and recovery;
2. define scope and source inventory;
3. fix one high-frequency findability failure;
4. establish minimum metadata rules;
5. plan migration or cleanup;
6. create a maintenance cadence;
7. review success measures.

This order may change when the household's evidence says otherwise. It is a starting framework, not a universal prescription.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Doing the easiest cosmetic work first.
- Turning subjective scores into false precision.
- Ignoring maintenance after the initial cleanup.
- Averaging an urgent access risk into a low total.
- Giving reversibility no weight.
- Letting one person's preference define household impact.
- Starting several packages without finishing a pilot.

Norva can help organise a compatible authorised source, but it cannot resolve source ownership, recover missing upstream data, or make an irreversible household decision safe.

## Frequently asked questions

### Should the highest score always go first?

No. Use the score to expose trade-offs, then consider rights, dependencies, available time, and a safe rollback route.

### How large should a work package be?

Small enough to complete and verify in one review cycle. Split any package whose result cannot be observed independently.

### What if household members score differently?

Keep both scores, discuss the evidence, and test the workflow affecting the disagreement. Do not hide different needs in an average.
