# How to Compare Two Video Versions Responsibly

> **In short:** Match the title, edit, scene, timecode, playback device, output, display mode, seat, and room. Record source lineage and metadata, mark unknowns, compare several spatial, gradient, dark, color, and motion scenes, and randomise or hide labels where practical. Report separate strengths and artifacts instead of forcing one universal winner.

A fair comparison asks a narrow question. If source, grade, edit, codec, resolution, frame rate, dynamic range, device, and display all change, the result is a preference between two complete paths, not proof of one factor.

## Define the decision

State whether the comparison supports choosing a version, diagnosing a path, understanding a badge, or evaluating one verified encode change. Name what will remain controlled and what cannot be controlled.

Do not begin with a desired winner or a marketing label.

## Verify version alignment

Confirm the same edit, duration, framing, language where relevant, and matched timecodes. Different restorations, grades, cuts, or masters can legitimately differ. Record source lineage only when known and keep private source details out of notes.

[The same-resolution guide](/blog/why-two-videos-at-the-same-resolution-can-look-different/) lists source and encode variables that dimensions do not cover.

## Fix the playback path

Use the same device, app or browser, selected representation, output, receiver, display input and mode, processing, seat, and lighting. Let adaptive streams settle. If one version needs a different decoder or path, disclose the coupled comparison.

Keep volume and captions from obscuring picture regions.

## Select a scene set

Include fine texture, faces, graphics, dark gradients, highlights, saturated color, slow pans, fast motion, film grain, smoke or water, and credits. Observe normal speed first, then pause only to locate a finding.

One scene can favor one encoder or grade. Multiple scenes reveal trade-offs.

Do not average unlike dimensions into a number unless the decision method and weighting are justified before viewing.

## Reduce expectation bias

Hide filenames and badges, alternate starting order, and use consistent switching where practical. Ask viewers to describe observed differences before preference. Keep individual notes separate and avoid revealing others' answers early.

ITU P.910 and BT.500 provide structured assessment methods; an informal comparison should not claim equivalent laboratory rigor.

## Name artifacts precisely

Record blocking, ringing, smearing, lost texture, banding, clipped shadow detail, raised blacks, tint, crop, cadence, or dropped frames at exact locations. Use [the artifact guide](/blog/how-to-recognize-common-video-compression-artifacts/) instead of "better quality."

Also record positive preservation such as stable texture or clearer gradients.

## Repeat and reverse

Replay meaningful differences, switch order, and restore baseline. If a difference disappears, report uncertainty. If preference changes by scene, preserve that interaction.

Do not replay until a viewer gives the expected answer; fatigue and adaptation can affect judgment.

## Protect rights and privacy

Compare only sources the user owns or is authorised to use. Do not share copyrighted clips, credentials, source addresses, tokens, account data, or viewing history. Reference timecodes and privacy-safe crops where permitted.

## Report responsibly

Include decision, versions, known metadata, unknowns, scenes, device and path, display and room, order, viewer count without invented demographics, separate observations, repeats, and limitations. [The video-quality checklist](/blog/a-video-quality-literacy-checklist/) provides a final completeness pass.

Current Norva playback diagnostics and labels must be verified officially. Norva organises and plays compatible authorised sources; it does not provide or certify their masters.

## Define a stopping rule

Choose the number of scenes and repeats before beginning. Stop when planned evidence is complete, a technical mismatch invalidates the comparison, or viewers report fatigue. Do not keep switching until one version wins. If results remain mixed, preserve that conclusion and list the additional verified information that would be needed for a narrower test.

## Frequently asked questions

### Must the versions have identical metadata?

Not for an observational comparison, but every difference limits which cause can be claimed.

### Is blind comparison required at home?

Not always, but concealing labels and changing order can reduce expectation bias when practical.

### Should the result choose one winner?

Not necessarily. Report strengths, artifacts, scene interactions, and uncertainty separately.
