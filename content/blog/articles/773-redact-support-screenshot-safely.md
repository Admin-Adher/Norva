# How to Redact a Support Screenshot Before Sharing It

> **In short:** Preserve the unmodified screenshot privately, then work on a duplicate. Crop everything support does not need, cover sensitive pixels with a fully opaque block, and export a flattened image rather than an editable project. Reopen the exact exported file, inspect it at multiple zoom levels, check filenames and visible notifications, and send only the minimum evidence through the official support destination.

A screenshot can reveal far more than the error being reported: email addresses, profile names, device identifiers, other notifications, viewing history, payment fragments, QR codes, reset links, or source details. Redaction should minimize disclosure while preserving enough context to diagnose the issue.

## Preserve the original before editing

Save an unmodified original in a private, access-controlled location if the incident may need investigation. Give the working duplicate a neutral filename that does not expose an email address, account name, or secret.

Do not annotate the only copy. If a fake support interaction or account incident is involved, the original message sequence and timestamp may matter. Follow the [fake support request guide](/blog/recognize-fake-media-support-request/) before sending anything back to the contact.

## Minimize the frame first

Crop away other applications, browser tabs, bookmarks, taskbars, household photos, chats, notifications, and unrelated account panels. When possible, reproduce the issue with non-sensitive test information and capture only the relevant application region.

Support generally needs the error state, action that triggered it, device or application context requested in the official case, and approximate time. Ask through the official support route if you are unsure. More pixels are not automatically better evidence.

## Inventory sensitive information

Inspect every corner and reflection. Remove or mask:

- passwords, one-time codes, recovery codes, and live reset links;
- session tokens, access keys, cookies, and QR codes that encode access;
- full email addresses, phone numbers, physical addresses, and birth dates unless strictly required;
- full payment or government identifiers;
- compatible authorized-source credentials and private server details;
- unrelated profile names, history, messages, notifications, and device names;
- faces, room details, or reflections captured in a photograph of a screen.

Do not paste any secret into a support caption. If a credential appeared in an image that was already sent, use the [credential-exposure plan](/blog/credential-exposure-incident-plan/) instead of assuming deletion from the conversation solved the exposure.

## Remove pixels rather than decorating them

Use a fully opaque shape or crop that permanently replaces the sensitive area in the exported image. Do not rely on a translucent highlighter, light blur, small emoji, or an editable overlay whose underlying content remains available.

Export to a new flattened raster image according to the editor's current official instructions. Keep the layered working file private or delete it under the household's retention policy after the case no longer needs it.

## Verify the exact exported file

Close the editor and reopen the final attachment in an ordinary image viewer. Zoom in and out, inspect the thumbnail, and confirm that every redaction remains opaque. Check whether cropping actually removed the area and whether the filename discloses information.

If the sharing service previews, compresses, or converts images, inspect the preview before sending when possible. For a high-risk secret, create a fresh sanitized screenshot rather than trusting a complex edit. Never assume that a mark visible on screen is permanent until the exported file is tested.

## Share through the minimum official channel

Open Norva support independently and attach only the sanitized copy. Describe the steps to reproduce the issue in text without passwords, tokens, recovery codes, or authorized-source credentials. The [complete security checklist](/blog/media-player-security-checklist/) can help separate account, source, device, and support boundaries.

Retain or delete support evidence according to current privacy guidance, the case's needs, and applicable obligations. Do not keep a sensitive screenshot indefinitely merely because the ticket is closed.

## Original evidence: support screenshot release checklist

| Check | Result | Evidence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Private original preserved if needed | Pass / Fail / Not needed | Location without secret |
| Irrelevant frame cropped | Pass / Fail | Regions removed |
| Sensitive pixels covered opaquely | Pass / Fail | Categories, never values |
| Flattened export reopened | Pass / Fail | Viewer used |
| Thumbnail, zoom, filename checked | Pass / Fail | Reviewer initials |
| Official support destination used | Pass / Fail | Case reference |

A second authorized reviewer is useful when the image contains unusually sensitive material.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Editing the only copy of incident evidence.
- Covering text with transparency or a movable layer.
- Forgetting browser tabs, notifications, reflections, or filenames.
- Sharing an editable project instead of the flattened export.
- Inspecting the editor view but not the final attachment.
- Sending a full screen when a narrow crop is sufficient.
- Treating redaction as a substitute for rotating an exposed secret.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is blurring enough to hide a password?

Do not rely on blur for a secret. Crop it or replace its pixels with a fully opaque block in a flattened export, then verify the file.

### Should I remove my email address?

Remove it unless the official case specifically needs it and the support channel already uses it appropriately. Share the minimum necessary information.

### What if I already sent an unredacted screenshot?

Tell official support, identify the exposed categories, rotate any exposed secrets through official destinations, and begin incident response when access may be affected.
