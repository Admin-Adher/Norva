# How to Map Household Viewing Needs Before Organizing

> **In short:** Ask each regular viewer what they try to find, where they watch, which language or accessibility options matter, and which personal state must remain separate. Classify every need as profile-specific, shared-library, device-specific, source-dependent, or unresolved. Organise only after the household agrees which needs are essential.

A shared collection fails when one person's tidy structure makes another person's routine harder. A needs map gives quieter viewers, remote-only users, and multilingual users equal influence before categories are changed.

## Interview tasks, not feature wishes

Ask each person for three recent examples:

1. something they found easily;
2. something they could not find or distinguish;
3. something they started on one screen and wanted elsewhere.

For each example, record the starting screen, input method, intended item, available clues, and outcome. “I need better recommendations” is less actionable than “I want to find another episode from the same source after finishing one on TV.”

Use these examples to define [library findability goals](/blog/set-library-findability-goals/) rather than copying a generic feature list.

## Separate five kinds of need

### Profile-specific

Progress, history, favourites, and language preferences may need to remain personal. Norva includes up to two profiles; Norva Family includes up to five. These are profile capacities, not device or simultaneous-playback limits.

### Shared-library

Title conventions, source labels, category meanings, and archive boundaries affect everyone. They need a shared rule and visible decision owner.

### Device-specific

TV remote navigation, touch filtering, and browser typing are different interaction contexts. A good plan can support equivalent outcomes without forcing identical routes.

### Source-dependent

Available titles, metadata, versions, audio, and subtitles depend on the compatible authorised source and media. An organiser cannot invent missing source information.

### Unresolved

Rights, ownership, unclear duplicates, or conflicting preferences belong in a review queue rather than being forced into a permanent rule.

## Map screen and input constraints

For every regular viewing situation, note:

- device type and supported experience;
- remote, touch, keyboard, or another input;
- distance and text legibility needs;
- shared or personal device;
- typical connection state;
- whether offline access is essential;
- assistance sometimes required.

Do not assume the person completing the map represents everyone. Let each viewer demonstrate one task on the screen they actually use.

## Make language requirements precise

Distinguish:

- interface language;
- catalogue title and description language;
- preferred audio;
- required subtitles;
- descriptive audio or captions where available;
- fallback choice when the preferred track is absent.

Available language tracks depend on the source version and media. A plan should say how to verify them, not promise that they exist. Expand this work with [the multilingual collection planning guide](/blog/plan-multilingual-media-collection/).

## Resolve conflicts with decision rules

Common conflicts include broad versus narrow categories, personal versus shared favourites, or an active item that another person considers archival. Agree in advance:

- which decisions are personal profile choices;
- which require household agreement;
- who may make reversible maintenance changes;
- which actions require an export or backup first;
- where objections are recorded;
- when a decision is reviewed.

Place those rules in [a shared collection charter](/blog/create-media-collection-charter/) and assign the ongoing work through [the responsibility guide](/blog/assign-shared-library-responsibilities/).

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Asking only the person who maintains the library.
- Turning a personal favourite into a shared category.
- Treating every device as the same interface.
- Calling all language needs “multilingual.”
- Assuming a preferred track exists on every item.
- Using profile counts as device limits.
- Resolving disagreement through irreversible cleanup.

The map records stated and observed household needs. It does not prove that every source or device can satisfy them.

## Frequently asked questions

### How many people should be included?

Include every regular viewer whose state or tasks the collection is meant to support. Add occasional viewers only when their workflow affects the plan.

### What if two people want opposite category structures?

Test the essential retrieval tasks for both. Prefer shared categories with stable meanings and use profiles or personal state where separation is supported.

### Should children receive separate profiles?

Profile capacity can separate viewing state, but Norva Family should not be presented as a parental-control product. Household decisions must follow current terms and appropriate supervision.
