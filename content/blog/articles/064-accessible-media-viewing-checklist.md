# An Accessibility Checklist for Comfortable Media Viewing

> **In short:** Ask the viewer what they need, then check the actual item for captions, audio description, language options, readable presentation, usable controls, suitable sound, and a comfortable room. Verify each choice with a short passage before the session begins. Accessibility is personal; no single preset works for everyone.

An accessible session begins with people, not a menu. Two viewers can prefer different caption sizes, spoken languages, sound levels, control methods, or lighting. A checklist creates a calm setup conversation without assuming that one need implies another.

## 1. Ask, do not guess

Use direct questions:

- Would captions or subtitles help?
- Is audio description needed or preferred?
- Which spoken and written languages work best?
- Is the screen readable from the intended seat?
- Who needs access to pause, volume, and track controls?
- Are breaks or reduced sensory load important?

Record the answers for the session, not as permanent labels about a person.

## 2. Verify media alternatives on the exact item

The W3C identifies captions, transcripts, and audio description as distinct ways to make audio and video information accessible. Check the exact item and grouped version because available tracks can differ.

For text:

- select a named track;
- test clear dialogue;
- confirm that the type of text meets the viewer’s need.

For audio description:

- look for an explicit purpose label;
- listen to a passage containing meaningful visual action;
- confirm that the selected track is appropriate.

Use [the audio-track decision guide](https://norva.tv/blog/choose-audio-track/) and [the subtitle-selection routine](https://norva.tv/blog/select-video-subtitles/) for detailed steps.

## 3. Check readability in context

Sit where the viewer will sit. Test captions and interface text over bright, dark, and busy scenes. Review size, contrast, background treatment, line wrapping, and position. Reduce glare where practical.

The [caption-readability guide](https://norva.tv/blog/make-captions-easier-to-read/) provides a repeatable three-scene check. It is a comfort exercise, not a formal accessibility certification.

## 4. Make controls reachable and predictable

The viewer should be able to identify focus, open playback controls, pause, return, and recover from an accidental action. On TV, confirm that the remote is within reach and that the focused element is visible from the seating position. On mobile or Web, check that the preferred input method is comfortable.

Do not take control away in the name of helping. Ask whether the viewer wants assistance, shared control, or independent control.

## 5. Prepare sound and the room

Choose speakers or headphones that suit the people and setting. Keep volume at a comfortable level and verify that dialogue is understandable. Consider:

- background noise;
- echo or competing conversations;
- room lighting and reflections;
- seating angle and distance;
- a clear path to the controls;
- the need for breaks.

Do not present one volume, brightness, or distance as universally accessible.

## 6. Verify the connection and device

An accessible track is only useful if playback remains usable. Confirm that the chosen device supports the media configuration and that the connection is stable in the actual viewing position. If access depends on offline availability, verify that the device, source, and rights permit it before leaving connectivity.

Norva is available across Web, mobile, and TV on supported devices. Choose the screen that best meets the viewer’s access needs rather than defaulting to the largest.

## 7. Run the two-minute pre-session check

This is the original evidence checklist for the article:

| Area | Observable confirmation |
| --- | --- |
| Viewer preference | The viewer confirms the setup |
| Audio | Correct language or description track is audible |
| Text | Correct track appears during dialogue |
| Readability | Text and controls are clear from the real position |
| Control | Viewer can pause and return |
| Environment | Sound, lighting, and seating are comfortable |
| Continuity | Playback remains stable during the sample |

If any row fails, fix that specific area and replay the same short passage. This avoids changing several variables at once.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Assuming every viewer wants the same caption style.
- Treating subtitles, captions, transcripts, and audio description as interchangeable.
- Checking a TV while standing next to it instead of from the seat.
- Hiding controls from the person who needs them.
- Claiming compliance from a household checklist.
- Expecting every item or source to provide every alternative.

Norva can retain language and subtitle preferences when suitable options exist. Track availability and quality still depend on the media and authorised source.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is this a formal accessibility audit?

No. It is a practical, person-first viewing checklist. Formal conformance evaluation uses defined criteria, evidence, and testing methods.

### Should accessibility settings be identical on every screen?

Not necessarily. Distance, display, input method, operating system controls, and room conditions differ. Preserve the viewer’s goal while adapting the setup.

### What if the requested track is not listed?

Check the exact item and version. A player preference cannot create an alternative that the media and source do not provide.

### Who should decide whether the setup is comfortable?

The viewer. A helper can offer options and perform checks, but should not replace the viewer’s stated preference.
