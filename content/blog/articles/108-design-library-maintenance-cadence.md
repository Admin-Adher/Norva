# Choose a Maintenance Cadence for Your Media Library

> **In short:** Review fast-changing, high-consequence states more often than stable cosmetic ones. Use a light intake check when new media arrives, a regular queue and category review, a seasonal structure review, and event-driven checks after source, device, account, or household changes. Pilot the schedule and adjust it from actual effort.

Maintenance fails when every task is called “weekly” or when nothing is reviewed until search breaks. A cadence should follow change rate and consequence, not calendar habit alone.

## List the states that can drift

Common maintenance areas include:

- new-item intake;
- uncategorised or uncertain queue;
- duplicate candidates;
- availability and source status;
- category meaning and empty categories;
- profile and household changes;
- language and subtitle rules;
- archive candidates;
- storage and backup state;
- retrieval-task results;
- decision log follow-ups.

Not every collection needs every task. Link each task to a real risk or findability goal.

## Match cadence to change and consequence

Rate each task on two questions:

1. How quickly can this state change?
2. What happens if no one notices for one cycle?

High-change, high-consequence states need frequent or event-driven attention. Low-change, low-consequence states can wait for a seasonal review.

NDSA's preservation levels connect inventory, storage, integrity, information security, metadata, and formats. They are intended for preservation programmes, but the layered principle helps households avoid reviewing only visible categories while ignoring source or storage risk.

## Use four cadence types

### At intake

Check scope, source, authorisation, minimum metadata, likely duplicates, and destination before accepting a new item or source into the organised view.

### Short regular review

Clear small queues, verify recent exceptions, and record decisions. This may be weekly for active collections or monthly for slow-changing ones.

### Seasonal review

Review category structure, archive candidates, household needs, source dependencies, and success measures. Use [the seasonal review plan](/blog/plan-seasonal-library-reviews/) for a complete agenda.

### Event-driven review

Trigger a check after:

- adding or removing a source;
- changing devices or applications;
- a household member or profile change;
- failed account recovery;
- a migration;
- unexpected missing metadata;
- repeated search or playback problems.

Events often matter more than an arbitrary date.

## Protect the time budget

Set a maximum session length and a stopping rule. If the queue cannot be handled within that budget:

- reduce required metadata;
- split review and execution;
- narrow the active collection;
- delegate a documented task;
- change the cadence;
- investigate why intake creates so many exceptions.

Use [the maintenance effort estimator](/blog/estimate-library-maintenance-effort/) before adopting a complex schedule.

## Assign a clear owner and backup

Every scheduled check needs one owner, an expected output, and a fallback when that person is unavailable. “The household” is rarely a useful owner.

The owner may prepare recommendations while destructive or shared decisions still require approval. Record the arrangement in [the shared responsibility guide](/blog/assign-shared-library-responsibilities/).

## Connect cadence to growth

A growing library needs thresholds that can accelerate review. If the intake queue, duplicate backlog, or maintenance time crosses a household-defined trigger, revise the plan instead of adding silent debt. The [library growth guide](/blog/plan-media-library-growth/) provides a trigger register.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Scheduling every task at the same frequency.
- Reviewing categories but not source or storage state.
- Naming a group instead of an accountable owner.
- Allowing maintenance to consume unlimited time.
- Ignoring event-driven triggers.
- Keeping a cadence after the collection's change rate drops.
- Treating an empty queue as the only success measure.

No cadence guarantees preservation or accuracy. It creates predictable opportunities to notice and act on changes.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is weekly maintenance necessary?

Only when the collection changes quickly enough to justify it. Slow collections may use monthly and event-driven checks.

### What belongs in a seasonal review?

Review scope, category health, archive boundaries, source dependencies, household needs, success measures, and the maintenance schedule itself.

### What if no one completes the schedule?

Reduce the task, assign one owner, and remove rules that create more upkeep than value. An unmaintainable plan should be simplified.
