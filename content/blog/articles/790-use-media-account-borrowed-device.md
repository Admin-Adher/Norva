# Use a Media Account More Safely on a Borrowed Device

> **In short:** Use your own trusted device or wait when practical. If borrowed-device use is necessary, get the owner's permission, verify the device and official app are updated, choose a documented guest or temporary mode when available, and avoid recovery, password-manager, or source administration. Do not save credentials or downloads, sign out visibly, clear only your own temporary data with the owner, and review devices or sessions afterward.

A borrowed device belongs to someone else's security environment. You may not know its update history, browser extensions, management state, backups, remote access, or whether another user can inspect saved data after handback.

## Prefer an alternative to direct sign-in

Consider using your own updated device, an approved screen-sharing method to a verified display, or waiting until trusted hardware is available. Do not pressure the owner to change their security settings for your convenience.

A one-time viewing need rarely justifies entering recovery email, password-manager, device-platform, or compatible-source administrator credentials on unfamiliar hardware.

## Verify owner, device, and network

Obtain explicit permission and ask whether the device is personal, shared, managed by work or school, recently repaired, or due for reset. Confirm current operating-system and official media-app updates with the owner.

If the device shows unexplained remote-control software, security warnings, unofficial applications, or unknown management, do not sign in. On public networking, follow the [public Wi-Fi account guide](/blog/public-wifi-media-account-safety/) and avoid sensitive changes.

## Use documented temporary separation

A supported guest user, browser guest window, or temporary profile can reduce persistence, but behavior varies. Some modes close local history on exit while downloads or network records may remain; some devices offer no guest mode.

Use the [guest-mode evaluation guide](/blog/guest-mode-media-viewing-where-available/) and current platform documentation. Private browsing is not the same as a separate operating-system user and does not make the device trusted.

## Minimize account and local exposure

Open the official Norva route independently. Enter only the credentials necessary for approved viewing and decline browser saving. Avoid changing passwords, recovery, payment, authorized sources, or household sharing from the borrowed device.

Do not download offline media, take account screenshots, import a password-manager vault, or connect removable storage unless there is a documented need and a safe cleanup plan. Eligible Norva offline media remains device-, source-, media-, and rights-dependent; it is unsuitable for casual borrowed-device persistence.

## Watch for screen and notification disclosure

Do not leave the device unattended while signed in. Check whether account alerts appear on the owner's lock screen or notification center. Avoid casting to a display you have not physically verified.

If another person needs the device, pause and sign out rather than simply minimizing the application.

## End access in front of the owner

Use the application's current sign-out flow and verify that reopening it no longer displays the account. End the browser guest or temporary session using official instructions. Follow the [shared-browser session guide](/blog/end-media-session-shared-browser/) without deleting the owner's unrelated history or files.

Remove only your own downloads, screenshots, clipboard contents, and temporary files with the owner's consent. Browser-data deletion does not prove that an application session or cloud account ended.

## Review from a trusted device afterward

Inspect Norva devices or sessions where current controls provide them. Remove temporary access and record status as requested, pending, or confirmed. If the password was saved, observed, phished, or entered into a device later found unsafe, change it from a known-good device and perform the [post-password session review](/blog/post-password-change-session-review/).

Handle every authorized source separately. A Norva cleanup does not remove a source account used directly on the borrowed device.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Treating private browsing as a trusted device.
- Signing into recovery email or a full password-manager vault.
- Saving a password for convenience.
- Creating offline downloads without a cleanup plan.
- Deleting the owner's browser data broadly.
- Closing the window instead of signing out.
- Forgetting source applications used outside Norva.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is browser guest mode completely private?

No. It can limit local persistence, but the device owner, network, downloads, services, and account provider may retain information.

### Should I change my password after every borrowed-device session?

Not automatically. Change it if exposure is plausible, it was saved or observed, or the device is later found untrusted.

### Does closing the app end access?

Not necessarily. Use the official sign-out flow and verify devices or sessions from a trusted device where controls are available.
