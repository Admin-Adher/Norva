# Assign Account Security Responsibilities in a Household

> **In short:** Give every important account-security duty a primary owner and a backup: recovery email, password manager, devices and sessions, compatible authorized sources, support communication, incident coordination, and offboarding. Keep the role map free of passwords and codes, grant only the access each person needs, verify procedures at official destinations, and rehearse how responsibility transfers when someone is unavailable or leaves the household.

A shared viewing setup can fail even when everyone is well intentioned. One person may believe another reviews account alerts; nobody may know who controls the recovery inbox; or a former household member may retain access. A short responsibility map turns those assumptions into testable duties.

## Separate viewing from administration

Watching through a profile does not automatically require access to the household's email, password manager, billing controls, or source credentials. Start with the least access needed for each task.

The account owner remains responsible for following current Norva terms and connecting only compatible sources the household owns or is authorized to use. That legal and administrative role should be explicit, even if other people help with routine device checks.

Use the [credential-separation guide](/blog/separate-account-and-source-credentials/) before assigning roles. Norva, an authorized source, the recovery email, a device platform, and the password manager are different security boundaries.

## Name the essential roles

A small household may give several roles to one person, but each duty still needs a name:

- **Account owner:** approves major account changes and verifies compliance with current terms.
- **Recovery custodian:** maintains the recovery email and knows the official recovery route.
- **Credential custodian:** maintains unique records in the approved password manager.
- **Device reviewer:** checks trusted devices, temporary sessions, and retired hardware.
- **Source owner:** manages each compatible authorized source at that source's official destination.
- **Support coordinator:** opens official support requests and shares only necessary, redacted evidence.
- **Incident lead:** coordinates containment, records status, and prevents conflicting changes.
- **Offboarding owner:** removes access when a device, guest, or household relationship changes.

The [household password-manager setup](/blog/password-manager-setup-media-household/) can support these roles without exposing every record to every viewer.

## Assign a backup without duplicating secrets

A backup must know how to begin, where official instructions live, and whom to contact. They do not necessarily need permanent access to every secret. Use the password manager's current recovery or sharing options only after checking its official documentation.

Record the primary owner, backup, official destination, expected evidence, and last rehearsal date. Do not record passwords, one-time codes, recovery codes, live reset links, session tokens, or full payment details in the role map.

## Define event-based handoffs

Responsibility should change after predictable events: a phone is lost, a person travels, a shared television is replaced, a guest departs, a recovery address changes, or suspicious activity appears.

For an exposure event, use the [credential incident plan](/blog/credential-exposure-incident-plan/). One incident lead should coordinate the sequence while individual owners secure their separate boundaries. Mark remote actions as **requested**, **pending**, or **confirmed** rather than assuming that a click completed them.

## Rehearse two short scenarios

First, ask the backup to locate the official recovery guidance without revealing a secret. Second, simulate retirement of a device and identify who reviews the account, source, platform, and physical device.

The exercise should expose missing knowledge, not test people's memory. Update the map when ownership, contact details, devices, sources, or official procedures change. Choose a review rhythm appropriate to the household and repeat after material events.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Treating the most technical person as the unspoken owner of everything.
- Giving every viewer access to administrative credentials.
- Naming a backup who cannot locate the official recovery route.
- Mixing Norva and authorized-source credentials in one support note.
- Forgetting access removal after household or device changes.
- Recording secrets inside the responsibility matrix.
- Assuming product controls without checking current official documentation.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does every role need a different person?

No. One person may hold several roles, but documenting each duty reveals gaps and makes handoffs clearer.

### Should children or guests receive account-owner access?

Only give each person the access required for their approved task. Ordinary viewing does not itself require control of recovery or source credentials.

### When should the role map be reviewed?

Review it after material household, device, source, recovery, or security changes and on a recurring schedule chosen by the household.
