# How to Plan a Director Retrospective From Your Own Collection

> **In short:** Define whether the project covers features, shorts, television work, co-directing credits, or only a selected period. Verify credits and work identity, audit which titles and versions are actually available in your authorized collection, then select each movie for a distinct role. Arrange chronology, phases, themes, or paired contrasts, and publish an omission note rather than implying completeness.

A director retrospective can be complete, selective, or introductory. Trouble begins when a selective set is presented as a complete filmography or when the collection's availability silently determines the critical argument.

## Define the scope before searching

Write the inclusion policy:

- credited directing roles included;
- co-directed works included or excluded;
- shorts, features, episodes, or other forms;
- date range;
- finished works versus fragments or supplements;
- collection-only boundary;
- target number of viewings.

This policy lets you explain why an absent work is outside scope rather than simply forgotten.

Use a reliable external authority to verify credit where source metadata is unclear. EIDR describes director, title, release date, referent type, and other identity data in its records, but it also explains that it is an identifier registry rather than a rich metadata provider. No single field should replace source checking.

## Separate work coverage from version readiness

A work can belong in scope while no suitable version is currently available. Record those states independently. For each available version, verify cut label, runtime, audio, subtitles, quality, and source.

The FIAF cataloguing model distinguishes works, variants, manifestations, and items. That distinction is useful when the retrospective includes restorations, alternate cuts, or different language versions.

Use [the alternate-cut decision guide](/blog/choose-between-alternate-cuts/) when more than one substantive cut is available. The viewing choice should be documented, not hidden by grouping.

## Select movies for distinct roles

Give every selection one job:

- establish an early method;
- show a major transition;
- test a recurring theme;
- reveal collaboration or genre change;
- contrast critical or audience expectations;
- close with a late reconsideration.

Two works can remain when their similarity is the evidence. Otherwise, remove redundancy and acknowledge the excluded title in the coverage note.

## Choose a viewing route

**Chronology** foregrounds change over time. **Phase-based order** groups early, transitional, mature, or late work, but those phase labels must be justified. **Thematic order** follows recurring questions. **Paired contrast** alternates works with opposed form, scale, or subject.

## Add a viewing note without overproducing

Before each movie, record one question and one verified context fact. Afterward, add one observation and one connection to another selection. Avoid writing a full review if the note will not support the next comparison.

Personal ratings can be added after reflection, but they should not retroactively determine whether a historically or formally useful work remains in the project.

## Publish the gap note

State:

- scope of the retrospective;
- works outside the collection;
- unavailable or unsuitable versions;
- uncertain credits or dates;
- deliberate exclusions;
- whether the project is complete or continuing.

This turns collection limits into transparent methodology.

## Keep the project temporary

Use [the temporary viewing-theme workflow](/blog/track-viewing-themes-without-clutter/) to save the manifest, dates, route, and notes without creating permanent categories for every director.

Norva may help search compatible authorized sources, group versions, and retain personal viewing context. It does not supply a universal movie catalogue, and retrospective coverage remains limited to connected sources and their metadata.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Failing to define whether shorts or co-directed works count.
- Treating source availability as complete filmography coverage.
- Merging alternate cuts without a version note.
- Selecting chronologically without a question or comparison.
- Presenting phase labels as objective facts.
- Hiding omissions that materially shape the project.

## Frequently asked questions

### Must a retrospective include every directed work?

No. It can be selective if the inclusion policy and gaps are explicit.

### Should I always watch chronologically?

No. Use chronology when development over time is the question; use themes or contrasts for other questions.

### How should co-directed movies be handled?

Set the rule before selection, verify the credit, and explain inclusion or exclusion consistently.
