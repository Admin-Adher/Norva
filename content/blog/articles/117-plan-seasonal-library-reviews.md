# How to Plan Seasonal Reviews of a Media Collection

> **In short:** Schedule a review every three or four months, or use household seasons that match real change. Prepare source status, retrieval results, exception age, maintenance effort, archive candidates, and recent decisions. Time-box the meeting, approve only a small action list, assign owners, and leave uncertain items in review rather than forcing cleanup.

“Seasonal” is a planning rhythm, not a requirement to use weather seasons. A school term, travel cycle, or quarterly calendar may fit the household better. Consistency matters more than the label.

## Prepare evidence before the review

Collect:

- current collection scope and charter;
- source inventory and ownership status;
- essential findability task results;
- new-item and uncategorised queues;
- duplicate and metadata exceptions;
- unavailable or changed source groups;
- active and archive candidate lists;
- maintenance time from the last cycle;
- decision log entries due for review;
- known household or device changes.

The review should interpret evidence, not spend the entire session finding it.

## Use a fixed agenda

### 1. Purpose and scope

Does the collection still serve the same people and tasks? Record a change only when current evidence supports it.

### 2. Sources and access

Are in-scope sources still compatible, authorised, reachable, and owned by a known person? Do not record credentials in the review.

### 3. Retrieval

Repeat a small set of known tasks. Use [the library success scorecard](/blog/choose-library-success-measures/) to compare with the previous cycle.

### 4. Categories and metadata

Identify empty, overlapping, or misunderstood categories and recurring unknown fields. Avoid bulk changes during the discussion.

### 5. Exceptions

Review oldest and highest-impact items first. Close, reassign, or give them a new evidence requirement.

### 6. Active and archive

Review both archive candidates and items that should return. Use [the active-archive decision card](/blog/separate-active-and-archive-collections/).

### 7. Maintenance and ownership

Compare actual effort with the planned budget. Reassign work or simplify rules that no one maintains.

### 8. Decisions and next actions

Approve a short list with owner, due date, rollback, and pass condition.

## Time-box the review

One possible structure:

- ten minutes for evidence summary;
- twenty minutes for sources, retrieval, and exceptions;
- fifteen minutes for archive and structure;
- ten minutes for workload;
- five minutes for decisions and owners.

These are planning examples, not a universal duration. Adjust to collection size and household availability. Defer detailed execution to separate work sessions.

## Use event triggers between seasons

Do not wait for the next scheduled review when:

- a source changes or access may end;
- recovery information fails;
- a migration is planned;
- a device or app change breaks essential tasks;
- storage or integrity risk appears;
- a household member or profile need changes.

The [maintenance cadence guide](/blog/design-library-maintenance-cadence/) should list these event-driven checks.

## Close the loop after the review

Within the agreed period:

1. add approved choices to [the decision log](/blog/create-library-decision-log/);
2. execute only assigned actions;
3. preserve rollback checkpoints;
4. retest the affected retrieval task;
5. close or reopen the action based on evidence;
6. update the next review pack.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Turning the review into a live bulk cleanup.
- Preparing no baseline evidence.
- Approving more work than the household can complete.
- Reviewing only visible categories.
- Treating archive as permanent deletion.
- Ignoring people who use different profiles or screens.
- Waiting for the next season during an urgent source change.

A review cadence helps notice change; it does not guarantee source availability, preservation, or correct metadata.

## Frequently asked questions

### Must the review occur four times a year?

No. Choose a rhythm that matches collection change. Twice yearly may suit a stable library; faster growth may need quarterly review.

### Who should attend?

The maintainer, relevant source owners, and regular viewers affected by shared decisions. Not everyone needs to attend every technical follow-up.

### How many actions should be approved?

Only as many as can be owned, completed, and verified before the next review. Prioritise high-impact or risk-reducing work.
