# Create a Lost-Device Response Card Before Travel

> **In short:** Build the card before departure and store it somewhere other than the travel device. Include a partial device identifier, carrier and official platform recovery destinations, trusted contacts, Norva support, account-review order, and fields for requested, pending, and confirmed actions. Never write passwords, one-time codes, recovery keys, full payment details, or authorized-source credentials on the card.

A lost-device plan is most useful during the first minutes, when stress makes it hard to remember which account protects which service. The card should accelerate safe decisions without becoming a second source of sensitive information.

## Prepare the device first

Use a strong screen lock, install current updates through trusted connectivity, and configure the device maker's official lost-device service where available. Confirm that the trusted person or backup device can reach the service without depending solely on the phone that will travel.

Apple and Android provide official tools that may locate, mark, secure, or erase a device when prior settings and current device conditions permit. Test access to the official service without triggering a destructive action.

## Record safe identifying details

Use enough information to distinguish household devices without exposing everything:

- device type, colour, case, and model;
- owner and travel companion contact;
- a partial serial or device identifier stored securely;
- mobile carrier and its official support destination;
- date the lost-device service was last checked;
- whether the device carries essential authentication or work data.

Do not print a full identifier on a card that sits in an exposed luggage pocket. Match the detail level to how the card will be stored.

## List official destinations, not search terms

Record the exact official platform lost-device URL, Norva support address, carrier contact, and organization security contact where relevant. Verify them before travel from primary documentation.

Avoid writing "search for lost phone" because an urgent web search can surface advertisements or fraudulent support pages. The card should send the traveller to a known destination.

## Define the response order

Put personal safety first. Do not confront a suspected finder or thief. Then:

1. use a separate trusted device and connection;
2. open the official platform service;
3. locate or mark the device when appropriate;
4. choose secure or erase actions using current guidance;
5. review email, platform, Norva, carrier, and authorized-source access;
6. contact local authorities or an organization when the incident requires it;
7. record each action and status.

The [lost-device offline-media response](/blog/protect-offline-media-after-device-loss/) explains why encryption does not replace account and platform action.

## Separate requested from confirmed

A remote command may wait for power or connectivity. Give the card distinct boxes for **requested**, **pending**, **confirmed by platform**, **recovered**, and **unknown**. Never tell the household that data is erased while the official service still shows a pending action.

If the phone carried Norva access, follow the [media-account phone-loss guide](/blog/secure-media-account-after-phone-loss/). Review [trusted devices](/blog/review-remove-trusted-devices/) from a known-good session rather than signing into an unfamiliar public terminal.

## Store and rehearse the card

Keep one copy in a password manager's secure note or another approved secure location and, if appropriate, a minimal printed copy in separate luggage. Do not store both the card and every recovery factor together.

Run a tabletop rehearsal: one person pretends the phone is unavailable and follows the card until the point before any real lock, revoke, or erase action. Fix unclear URLs and missing dependencies.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Storing the only copy on the travel phone.
- Writing passwords or recovery secrets on the card.
- Relying on an urgent web search for official help.
- Assuming remote erase completes instantly.
- Focusing on offline media while ignoring email and authentication.
- Including full identifiers on an exposed paper copy.
- Never rehearsing the response path.

## Frequently asked questions

### Should the card include my password?

No. It should point to secure recovery methods and official destinations without containing authentication secrets.

### Can I test remote erase before travel?

Do not trigger a destructive action for rehearsal. Verify access and navigation to the official service, then stop before locking or erasing.

### What if the device is recovered?

Follow platform guidance before trusting or reconnecting it. Review actions already taken, account state, and whether credentials were rotated.
