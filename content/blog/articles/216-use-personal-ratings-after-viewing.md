# How to Use Personal Ratings After You Finish a Movie

> **In short:** Decide what your scale means before scoring. Record the exact version and a one-line immediate reaction, wait for a consistent reflection interval, then rate three separate axes: personal response, qualities or ideas you value, and desire to revisit. Add one evidence sentence. Use a summary score only if it helps future choice, and revise it with a date rather than erasing the earlier context.

A personal rating is most useful when it helps future you remember a response or choose among movies. It becomes noise when the number tries to represent enjoyment, importance, craft, mood, and recommendation all at once.

## Define the job of the rating

Choose one primary purpose:

- remember personal response;
- rank likely rewatches;
- support recommendations within a household;
- compare a director, decade, or remake project;
- identify movies that deserve a note despite a mixed reaction.

If the purpose changes, create a separate field or rewrite the rubric. Do not silently reinterpret old numbers.

## Record the viewing context

Before rating, capture:

- work and exact cut or version;
- viewing date;
- whether viewing was complete;
- audio and subtitle choice when relevant;
- solo or group context if it materially shaped the response;
- interruption or technical issue.

This is not an excuse ledger. It prevents a rating of one version or incomplete session from being treated as a timeless judgment of the work.

## Use the three-axis card

| Axis | Question | Low end | High end |
|---|---|---|---|
| Personal response | How strongly did it work for me? | Little connection | Strong connection |
| Valued qualities | How much did its form or ideas reward attention? | Limited value | Substantial value |
| Revisit desire | Would I choose it again? | Unlikely | Strongly likely |

Set your own scale labels and keep them visible. Three axes reveal combinations a single number hides: a difficult movie may have low revisit desire but high valued qualities.

## Separate immediate and reflected response

Write one sentence immediately after viewing. Then wait for a consistent interval—later that day or another chosen checkpoint—before completing the card. The interval is a personal rule, not a scientific requirement.

Record confidence: high, medium, or low. A low-confidence rating can remain provisional without pretending to precision.

## Add one evidence sentence

Use a concrete observation:

- “The final act clarified the earlier visual pattern.”
- “The pacing made the central relationship difficult for me to follow.”
- “The comparison with the earlier version changed my response.”

Avoid plot summary. The sentence should explain the axes to your future self.

For a paired project, use [the original-remake dossier](/blog/compare-originals-and-remakes/) before rating so comparison evidence remains distinct from preference.

## Decide whether to calculate one score

You may leave the three axes separate. If a platform accepts one score, define how it is derived. Equal averaging is not neutral if revisit desire matters more to your workflow.

Write the rule once and keep it stable. Do not adjust weights to produce the number you expected.

## Connect ratings to actions carefully

A high revisit score can inform a familiar-movie list. A low personal response should not automatically remove a work from a retrospective or documentary project. [The familiar-versus-new workflow](/blog/balance-familiar-and-new-movies/) uses confidence and session fit rather than rating alone.

Similarly, a favorite can mark future intent while a rating records completed response. Use [the unwatched-favorite triage](/blog/revisit-unwatched-favorites/) before comparing those states.

## Revise without erasing

After a rewatch, keep the previous date and score, add the new one, and state what changed: version, context, attention, knowledge, or response. Personal ratings are time-bound observations.

Dublin Core separates description, date, relation, and version. A personal system benefits from the same distinction: keep the score, evidence note, date, and viewed version as connected but separate facts.

W3C notification guidance emphasizes clear outcome and recovery. If a rating is changed, the interface should confirm the new state and make accidental edits reversible.

Norva may expose favorites, history, and personal rating actions depending on the current product version. Verify available controls; an external three-axis card remains valid when only one summary score is supported.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Rating before confirming the watched version.
- Letting one number carry several undefined meanings.
- Changing the rubric without marking a new scale.
- Treating revisit desire as artistic judgment.
- Deleting earlier ratings after a rewatch.
- Comparing ratings created under different rules.

## Frequently asked questions

### What rating scale should I use?

Use the smallest scale whose labels you can define consistently. Clear meanings matter more than many points.

### Should I rate an unfinished movie?

Mark it incomplete and record a provisional response separately. Do not present it as a completed-viewing rating.

### Can ratings change over time?

Yes. Add a dated revision with context so the change becomes useful evidence rather than lost history.
