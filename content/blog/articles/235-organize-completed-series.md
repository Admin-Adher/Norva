# How to Organize Completed Series Without Hiding Them

> **In short:** Verify completion, remove the show from active “next episode” work, and preserve at least two deliberate routes back—such as history plus search, or a favorite plus a completed collection. Keep progress evidence intact. Completion should reduce urgency, not erase the series or pretend that future seasons and related works cannot appear.

A crowded active library makes every title look unfinished. Yet removing a completed show entirely destroys useful history and makes rediscovery harder. The better model is a state transition: active viewing ends, while identity, completion evidence, preference, and relationships remain available.

This manifest prevents “completed” from becoming a destructive catch-all.

## Define what completion means

Completion can refer to different boundaries:

- the viewer finished one season;
- the viewer finished every episode currently available from the connected source;
- the series itself has a verified final episode;
- a particular edit or variant is complete for this viewer.

Write the scope explicitly. “Completed available S1–S3 as checked on 14 July” is safer than “series finished.” A new season, corrected hierarchy, or source update may change what is available without invalidating the historical viewing record.

## Verify the final boundary

Confirm series, season, episode number, title, and version for the last item. Check that no special or split-season entry sits outside the assumed sequence. If metadata is uncertain, use [the post-update series audit](/blog/audit-series-after-source-update/) before applying a bulk completed state.

EIDR’s public hierarchy distinguishes a series, its seasons, episodes, edits, and manifestations. Your completion note should preserve enough of that context to identify what was actually watched.

## Remove urgency, not evidence

After verification, clear only states that imply immediate action:

- stale “continue” entries with no unfinished episode;
- temporary reminders created for the viewing run;
- sampling labels that have reached a decision;
- redundant episode bookmarks.

Keep confirmed history, last completion, and any favorite with an ongoing purpose. Do not reset progress just to create a cleaner home screen. Norva may retain progress, history, and favorites across supported devices under the same account; check current support material before making assumptions about how a particular cleanup action propagates.

## Preserve two retrieval routes

One route can fail after a source or interface change. Choose at least two:

1. search by the canonical series title;
2. browse a completed or historical view, if verified in the interface;
3. keep a purposeful series favorite;
4. retain a private collection note;
5. follow a typed relationship from a spin-off or companion work.

Test both routes after cleanup. If a favorite is one of them, use [the favorite scope decision card](/blog/favorite-series-or-episode/) to ensure it points to the series rather than an incidental episode.

## Separate finished viewing from continued interest

A completed series may still matter because you want to rewatch it, compare a variant, follow a future season, or explore related works. Add a reason beside the retained favorite or collection entry. That reason becomes the future cleanup rule.

For franchises, use [the spin-off and companion mapping workflow](/blog/map-spin-offs-and-companion-series/) instead of placing every related title under one ambiguous “completed” label.

## Set a review trigger

Do not review every completed series constantly. Choose an event:

- a verified new season appears;
- source metadata changes;
- a planned rewatch begins;
- a related work is added;
- the favorite no longer serves its stated purpose.

At review, compare current hierarchy with the manifest. Preserve the old completion as dated evidence and create a new active state only for genuinely unwatched entries.

## Recovery if the series disappears

If cleanup makes the work hard to find, stop further changes. Search by alternate title and stable identifiers, inspect source state, then restore one retrieval route. An unavailable current item is not proof that the series identity or viewing history should be deleted.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Calling “currently caught up” permanently completed.
- Resetting progress to clean the interface.
- Keeping no tested path back.
- Favoriting every completed show without an intent.
- Ignoring specials, variants, and split seasons.
- Reclassifying old completion after a new season appears.

## Frequently asked questions

### Should every completed series remain a favorite?

No. Keep a favorite only when it supports a future action. History or search may be enough for other titles.

### What if another season is announced?

Preserve the dated completion and create a new active expectation only when the new season or episodes are verified.

### Can a completed show return to the active queue?

Yes, for a rewatch or newly available entries. Record why the state changed.
