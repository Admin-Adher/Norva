# How to Choose a Media Organizer: A 12-Point Checklist

> **In short:** Choose a media organiser by first confirming what the software does, which authorised sources and devices it supports, and how it handles organisation, progress, languages, TV navigation, privacy, and account control. Test the same representative tasks with your own source. Record “not tested” instead of converting unknowns into assumptions.

Screenshots can show visual style, but they cannot establish compatibility, sync reliability, or product boundaries. The checklist below provides a repeatable comparison without declaring one product universally best.

## Before you score anything

Write down your actual setup:

- the compatible source you are authorised to use;
- the web, mobile, and TV screens that matter;
- whether several household profiles are needed;
- whether language, subtitles, or offline access are essential;
- which three tasks you perform most often.

Read [What Is a Personal Media Player?](/blog/what-is-personal-media-player/) if the roles of source, software, and device are not yet clear.

Use four result labels: **observed**, **documented but not tested**, **unavailable**, and **not tested**. Avoid a numerical score unless you define a meaningful, reproducible scale.

## The 12-point checklist

### 1. Product boundary

Does the subscription cover software, media access, or both? Identify the answer in the current terms, not in an advertisement.

Norva states that its subscription covers the software and features and that the user supplies a compatible source they own or are authorised to access. The [player-versus-provider comparison](/blog/media-player-vs-content-provider/) explains why this distinction matters.

### 2. Source compatibility

Can the product connect to your specific authorised source using a documented method? “Broad compatibility” is not enough. Verify the current support information.

### 3. Device compatibility

List the devices and browser you will actually use. Confirm support for each rather than treating a generic mobile or TV label as universal.

### 4. Catalogue organisation

Can you understand movies, series, episodes, live programmes, categories, and variants? Test a large category and one title with several source records.

### 5. Search and filters

Search for a known title, then browse without a title in mind. Check whether filters use reliable metadata such as year, rating, audio, subtitles, or availability.

### 6. Progress and history

Start one authorised item, stop at a recognisable point, and check whether progress is saved. If cross-device continuity matters, repeat on another supported screen using the same account.

### 7. Favourites and preferences

Add a favourite and select an available language preference. Confirm whether account-level choices appear consistently where documented.

### 8. TV navigation

On television, inspect focus visibility, directional movement, Back behaviour, text size, and whether controls can be reached with a remote. The W3C’s focus-visible guidance provides a useful accessibility principle even though TV interfaces have device-specific constraints.

### 9. Language and subtitle handling

Check whether available tracks are visible before or during playback and whether the labels are understandable. The source and media determine which options exist.

### 10. Privacy and security controls

Read the privacy policy. Identify the data used for accounts, progress, source connection, devices, payments, and diagnostics. Look for account deletion and trusted-device controls.

### 11. Offline conditions

If offline access matters, verify that the device, source, and associated rights permit it. Check where downloads are stored and how they are removed. Do not assume every item is downloadable.

### 12. Pricing, support, and exit

Review the current price, renewal terms, cancellation route, support channel, data-deletion process, and what happens when the subscription ends. Prices can change, so link to the live pricing page rather than relying on an old review.

## A comparison worksheet

Create one row per criterion:

| Criterion | Required? | Product evidence | Test result | Limitation or follow-up |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Product boundary | Yes | Current terms URL | Documented | Recheck before purchase |
| Source compatibility | Yes | Support page | Not tested | Test with own source |
| TV navigation | Maybe | Feature page | Observed | Record device |
| Offline access | Maybe | Privacy/help page | Unavailable | Source-dependent |

The “required?” column stops decorative features from outweighing a necessary one. The evidence column distinguishes a documented fact from a personal observation.

## Compare workflows, not feature counts

A long feature list can hide a poor daily workflow. Test three complete journeys:

1. find and start a known movie;
2. resume a series episode on another supported screen;
3. inspect source-supplied audio and subtitle options before viewing.

If a product performs your important workflows clearly, a missing low-priority feature may not matter. The planned [Norva mobile, web, and TV comparison](/blog/norva-mobile-web-tv-comparison/) will help place device-specific trade-offs in context.

## Limitations of a short trial

A trial may not reveal long-term source changes, rare playback formats, travel conditions, or household conflicts. Test representative cases, not only the easiest title.

Do not publish measured results without naming the device, source type, date, method, and limitations. A private worksheet is useful even when it does not qualify as a public benchmark.

## Frequently asked questions

### Should I choose the product with the most features?

Not automatically. Prioritise features that support your recurring tasks, source, and devices. Extra controls can add complexity without improving your workflow.

### Can online reviews replace my own test?

Reviews can identify questions, but compatibility and usability depend on your source and devices. Use primary documentation and a controlled trial where available.

### How often should I revisit the checklist?

Recheck before subscribing, after adding a major device or source, and when the product materially changes.
