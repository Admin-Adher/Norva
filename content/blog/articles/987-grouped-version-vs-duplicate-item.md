# Grouped Version vs. Duplicate Item: How They Differ

> **In short:** Grouped versions are distinct media records presented together because they appear to represent the same work. They may differ by edition, duration, quality, language tracks, subtitles, format, source, or rights. Duplicate items are redundant records of the same media. Do not delete anything until both source records and meaningful differences are verified.

Two identical posters can hide different media, while two different posters can refer to the same file. Classification therefore needs identity and technical evidence, not appearance alone.

The [media player glossary](/blog/media-player-glossary/) defines versions, metadata, containers, tracks, sources, and catalogs.

## Grouping is a presentation decision

A catalog may place records together when source-derived identity signals suggest that they represent one work. This can reduce visual repetition while preserving a choice among versions.

Norva may group versions from connected-source information. The quality of grouping depends on available identity and metadata; it cannot guarantee that ambiguous records are equivalent.

## A version has a meaningful distinction

Useful distinctions can include theatrical versus extended edition, different cut, duration, resolution, dynamic-range presentation, audio language, subtitle set, media format, or source availability. A household may need one version even when another looks similar.

Record the difference in neutral technical terms. A label such as "better" is not enough.

## A duplicate is redundant

A probable duplicate has no meaningful identity, media, access, or user-workflow distinction after verification. Even then, the source administrator should understand why both records exist before removing one.

An imported duplicate can originate in source records, identifiers, naming, multiple source entries, or catalog matching. The visible catalog alone cannot establish cause.

## Similar titles are weak evidence

Title, artwork, year, and description help identify a work but do not distinguish every edition. Compare duration, episode identity, source identifier, track inventory, technical properties, and actual playback.

For series, the same episode title can appear in different seasons, remakes, or specials. Preserve hierarchy in the comparison.

## Language tracks often explain versions

One version may carry different audio or subtitle tracks. A language badge can be incomplete or derived from metadata, so inspect actual available tracks in a controlled playback session where authorized.

A preference cannot create a missing track. Do not remove the only version that satisfies an essential language or caption need.

## Playback state may attach differently

Progress, completion, favorite state, or history may attach to a work, version, source record, or profile according to current product behavior. Grouping records visually does not prove their state is interchangeable.

Before changing grouping or source records, note current profile state for both versions.

## Verify from source to catalog

Use the [source versus catalog boundary](/blog/media-source-vs-media-catalog/) to record both source records separately. Reset filters and compare how the catalog presents them with grouping on and off where the interface supports that control.

Open details and run brief playback checks only to confirm distinctions. Do not share source credentials or private titles in support evidence.

## Classify conservatively

Use four outcomes: distinct versions of one work, probable duplicates, distinct works, or unresolved. "Unresolved" is safer than a destructive guess. If the source has an official identifier or edition label, preserve it in the private record.

The [import versus sync guide](/blog/catalog-import-vs-catalog-sync/) helps explain whether the pair first appeared during baseline creation or after a later change.

## Common classification mistakes

- Comparing only title and artwork.
- Assuming same duration means same media.
- Ignoring audio, subtitle, or edition differences.
- Treating visual grouping as state merging.
- Deleting a source record before preserving evidence.
- Calling ambiguous records duplicates with no baseline.

## Frequently asked questions

### Are two resolutions always separate versions?

They can be meaningful versions, but classification also depends on source identity, format, tracks, rights, and household need.

### Does grouping delete a record?

Do not assume so. Grouping is generally a catalog presentation concept. Verify current interface behavior and source records before any destructive action.

### What should I do when the records remain ambiguous?

Keep both, record unresolved identity, and use official support with sanitized evidence if the presentation causes a reproducible problem.
