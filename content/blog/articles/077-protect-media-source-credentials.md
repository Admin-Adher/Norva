# How to Protect the Credentials for Your Authorized Media Source

> **In short:** Give the source account a unique password, store it in a reputable password manager, enable multi-factor authentication if the source offers it, and sign in only through a verified provider route. Never paste a password or recovery code into notes, screenshots, or support messages. Prepare recovery steps before access is lost.

Source credentials can control access beyond the media player itself. Treat them as a separate security boundary. Norva organises a compatible source that you own or are legally authorised to use; it does not remove your responsibility for the source account, its terms, or its recovery process. Review the [compatible-source connection workflow](/blog/connect-compatible-media-source-norva/) before entering source details in the app.

## Separate every account boundary

At minimum, keep different credentials for:

- your primary email account;
- your Norva account;
- the compatible media source;
- the device or platform account;
- the password manager itself.

Password reuse turns one disclosure into several account risks. CISA recommends long, random, unique passwords and suggests using a password manager to generate and store them. Do not invent a memorable variation by adding the service name to the same base password.

The email account deserves particular protection because many recovery messages go there. Enable multi-factor authentication wherever the provider supports it.

## Verify the place where you sign in

Open the source provider through a saved official address or a route you have independently verified. Be cautious with sign-in links in unexpected email, text, chat, or pop-up messages. The FTC advises contacting an organisation using contact information you know is genuine rather than the details in a suspicious message.

Before entering credentials:

1. read the full domain name;
2. confirm the connection uses HTTPS;
3. stop if the browser shows a certificate warning;
4. check whether the requested information matches the normal sign-in flow;
5. refuse requests for a password, one-time code, or recovery code over support chat.

For shared networks, combine this routine with the [public Wi-Fi safety checklist](/blog/media-player-public-wifi-safety/).

## Share access without sharing secrets

If a household is authorised to use the same source, prefer the source provider's official household or delegated-access features when they exist. Do not assume such features are available. If they are not, minimise who handles the primary credentials and avoid sending them through ordinary messages.

Norva profiles can separate viewing preferences, but a profile is not a substitute for source-account access control. The Norva plan supports up to two profiles and Norva Family up to five; these are profile capacities, not statements about source permissions or simultaneous use.

## Prepare recovery before you need it

Check the provider's official account area and record:

- the recovery email or phone status;
- whether MFA is enabled;
- where backup methods are stored;
- how to review signed-in devices or sessions, if offered;
- the official support and recovery URL;
- the date of your last check.

Do not place passwords, recovery codes, or secret answers in this record. Store backup codes according to the provider's instructions, away from the device used for routine sign-in.

## Respond to suspected compromise in order

If you entered credentials on a suspicious page or notice unfamiliar activity:

1. move to a trusted device and connection;
2. change the source password through its official account route;
3. end unfamiliar sessions if the provider exposes that control;
4. enable or reset MFA as appropriate;
5. change any other account that reused the same password;
6. secure the linked email account;
7. contact the source provider through verified support details;
8. review connected applications and remove access you do not recognise.

Changing the password only in a media app may not change it at the source. Follow the source provider's own recovery instructions.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Reusing the email password for the media source.
- Storing credentials in an unprotected note or screenshot.
- Giving one-time codes to someone claiming to be support.
- Assuming a familiar logo proves a sign-in page is genuine.
- Sharing credentials when an official delegated-access option exists.
- Treating a Norva profile as a source-provider permission.
- Delaying recovery-contact updates until the account is inaccessible.

Available security and recovery controls vary by provider. Follow the provider's current documentation.

## Frequently asked questions

### Should Norva and my source use the same password?

No. Unique passwords limit the effect of one account disclosure. A password manager can create and store separate random passwords.

### Can support ask for my full password?

A legitimate support process should not require you to disclose your full password or one-time authentication code. Use the provider's official contact route if a request is suspicious.

### Does changing a password disconnect every device?

That depends on the provider. Review its active-session controls and recovery documentation rather than assuming every session ended automatically.
