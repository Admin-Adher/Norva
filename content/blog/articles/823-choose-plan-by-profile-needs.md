# Choose a Norva Plan by Recurring Profile Needs

> **In short:** Count the people who need persistent personal history, progress, favorites, and preferences. Norva currently states that its Norva plan supports up to two profiles and Norva Family supports up to five. Choose from that recurring profile count, not from temporary guests or assumed device limits. Then recheck live price, taxes, trial, renewal, supported platforms, offline conditions, cancellation path, and original billing provider before purchase.

Profile capacity is a clear decision input because it maps to everyday household organization. It should not be confused with simultaneous playback, device count, or source capacity unless current official terms explicitly connect them.

## Define a recurring profile need

A recurring profile belongs to a person who benefits from separate progress, history, favorites, language choices, subtitle preferences, or recommendations. An occasional visitor who can use a household profile may not justify permanent capacity.

Privacy and household expectations matter. Do not create a profile for another person without a clear reason, and avoid names that expose sensitive details on shared screens.

## Count current and near-term users

List the people who need profiles now, then consider predictable household changes during the likely subscription period. Do not buy around hypothetical distant possibilities, but avoid selecting a cap that will immediately require shared histories.

The [Norva-versus-Family framework](/blog/norva-vs-norva-family-decision-framework/) turns the count into a documented decision.

## Apply the published caps carefully

Current Norva material says:

- Norva: up to two profiles.
- Norva Family: up to five profiles.

It currently presents both plans as including the complete software experience. Product claims in this draft remain unverified and require a fresh check of the live offer and purchase screen.

Do not write that one plan allows more devices, more simultaneous streams, faster playback, larger libraries, or different source compatibility unless current first-party terms state that difference.

## Keep device questions separate

Norva currently advertises Web, mobile, and TV availability on supported devices. A household can own several devices while needing only one or two personal profiles, or need several profiles while primarily using one shared television.

Check supported operating systems and device conditions independently. Profile count does not establish a device cap.

## Evaluate offline needs conditionally

Offline use depends on the current feature, supported device, compatible source, eligible media, available storage, and applicable rights. Do not choose a plan on the assumption that every source item can be downloaded everywhere.

Review the live feature description on the device you expect to use.

## Compare total current terms

Before deciding, capture the displayed price, billing interval, currency, taxes, trial duration, payment-method requirement, renewal behavior, cancellation path, and provider. Avoid quoting an old price from this article because offers can change.

The [seven-day trial checklist](/blog/seven-day-trial-start-checklist/) provides the final decision gate, while the [pre-account checklist](/blog/prepare-before-creating-norva-account/) covers security and source readiness.

## Use an upgrade or downgrade trigger

Define the event that would justify changing plans: a third recurring profile for a two-profile household, a reduction to two or fewer persistent profiles, or a current plan change published by Norva. Verify effective date and billing consequences with the original provider before switching.

Never promise prorating, credits, refunds, or an immediate effective date without current provider terms.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Counting every visitor as a recurring profile user.
- Sharing one profile when separate state matters.
- Treating profile caps as device or stream caps.
- Assuming offline eligibility for every item and device.
- Relying on an old price or screenshot.
- Ignoring taxes, billing interval, and provider.
- Promising upgrade or downgrade timing without evidence.
