# How to Identify Audio Description When It Is Available

> **In short:** Look for an explicit label such as “audio description,” “described audio,” or an accessibility marker in the audio list. Select the candidate and sample a scene with important visual action. Verify that narration communicates visual information around the programme dialogue. Do not infer availability from language, list position, or an ordinary narrator.

Audio description is an accessibility alternative, but its presence and label depend on the media and source metadata. A player cannot offer a track that the selected item or version does not contain.

## Inspect every visible field

Open the audio selector before or during playback and record the exact candidate label. Note language, role text, accessibility symbol, channels, or custom title only when displayed.

An explicit role is the strongest starting evidence. A second entry in the same language is not enough by itself.

## Select an informative sample

Choose a short scene with visible movement, setting changes, expressions, on-screen text, or other information that is not already spoken by characters. Avoid a silent logo, credits, or a sequence with a programme narrator.

Listen for additional narration that conveys relevant visual information without being a production discussion. Confirm what is heard; do not assess the person's medical need or rank the narration's quality unless the affected viewer chooses to provide usability feedback.

## Distinguish related audio roles

Commentary usually discusses the work, creators, performers, or production. Audio description communicates visual information for access. An ordinary narrator belongs to the programme itself and may appear on every standard track.

When production discussion is present, use [the commentary identification workflow](/blog/how-to-identify-a-commentary-track-without-guessing/). When a role remains ambiguous, mark it unresolved rather than forcing a category.

## Original evidence: two-stage check

Create this record:

| Stage | Evidence |
|---|---|
| Metadata | Exact label and visible accessibility marker |
| Playback | Brief description of what the additional voice communicates |
| Context | Source, item, version, device, and app/browser version |
| Usability | Confirmed by viewer, not tested, or unsuitable in this context |

Presence and usability are different. A track can be correctly identified while still needing a volume, subtitle, device, or viewer-specific adjustment.

## Ask the viewer, not their history

If coordinating for another person, ask what outcome they want and whether the sample works. Do not request a diagnosis or inspect past titles to infer a need.

The household method for [coordinating accessibility preferences](/blog/how-to-coordinate-accessibility-preferences-in-a-household/) separates personal requirements from shared-screen and title-specific choices.

## Recheck scope

Identification applies only to the selected item and media version. Another episode, version, device, or eligible offline copy may expose a different list. Reopen the selector after a context change.

For a series, sample the relevant episodes before planning an entire season around one verified track. Use the [complete audio-management guide](/blog/the-complete-guide-to-managing-audio-tracks/) to document those contexts.

## When no candidate appears

Confirm the selected version and inspect the full list. Compare one known context only if it answers a specific question. Do not remove sources, clear data, reinstall, reset, or change credentials to search for a track.

If the source says an accessible alternative should exist but it is not exposed, document the exact source claim, item/version, list, device, steps, and result for the relevant source owner or support route.

## Report an ambiguous or incorrect label

Use privacy-safe screenshots of the selector where authorised. Include exact text and what a short sample established. Do not attach the media itself, source addresses, credentials, private history, or unrelated profile data.

## Common mistakes and limitations

Avoid equating any added voice with description, assuming every episode matches, changing several contexts at once, and storing personal access information unnecessarily.

W3C resources explain the accessibility purpose. Actual availability depends on the selected media, source metadata, and supported playback context.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is audio description the same as subtitles?

No. Audio description conveys visual information through audio; subtitles or captions provide text. A viewer may use either or both.

### Does an “AD” label always prove the role?

It is strong metadata evidence, but a short playback sample can verify that the selected entry behaves as intended.

### Can audio description be available in only some episodes?

Yes, track sets can differ. Verify each relevant episode or use a representative audit followed by outlier checks.
