# How to Review Viewing Privacy on Shared Screens

> **In short:** Review both the room and the interface. Identify who can see the screen or hear audio, then check profile labels, continuation history, favourites, notifications, account details, credential prompts, local media, and the next-open state. Profiles organise viewing context but are not passwords. Use a trusted-screen routine at home and a stricter sign-out and reopen check on borrowed or temporary devices.

Privacy on a shared screen is contextual. A harmless profile label in one household may reveal too much in a guest room, repair shop, or public setting.

## Classify the screen

Choose:

- trusted household screen;
- guest-accessible household screen;
- borrowed or temporary screen;
- public or broadly shared screen.

Avoid signing into a public or broadly shared screen unless a verified session process makes the exposure acceptable.

## Map physical exposure

Stand where other viewers, visitors, cameras, mirrors, windows, or adjacent rooms may reveal the screen. Consider audio as well as visuals.

Do not record people or private spaces for the audit. A written observation is enough.

## Review entry exposure

Before playback, inspect:

- account email or avatar;
- profile names and icons;
- continuation cards;
- recent searches;
- favourites;
- recommendations from the connected source;
- notification previews;
- saved credential prompts.

The [private shared-screen resume guide](/blog/how-to-resume-on-a-shared-screen-without-exposing-private-history/) provides a least-exposure entry routine.

## Review playback exposure

Check whether media title, subtitles, audio, timeline, or controls reveal information to unintended viewers. Use a non-sensitive test item.

Available languages and subtitles depend on the source and media. Do not disable accessibility or essential alerts merely to make the screen look private.

## Review account and source boundaries

Profile selection does not protect account settings. Identify whether viewers can reach account, pricing, privacy, or source screens from the current signed-in state.

Never display passwords, recovery codes, source credentials, private URLs, or payment data on a shared screen.

## Review local media and device custody

Eligible offline media is stored locally when device, source, and rights allow. Check whether temporary users can reach it and whether it should remain after the session.

A trusted device can become temporary when lent, repaired, sold, or moved.

## Define exit by trust level

For a trusted household TV, an agreed neutral profile-selection state may be sufficient. For a borrowed or untrusted device, sign out through verified controls, close the session, and reopen once to confirm the account is not exposed.

Do not assume closing a browser or switching profiles is sign-out.

## Include profile-owner consent

A privacy review should not become hidden monitoring. Ask profile owners to participate and inspect only structural exposure.

The [privacy-respecting profile review](/blog/how-to-review-profile-activity-without-invading-privacy/) explains minimum evidence. The [governance pillar](/blog/the-complete-guide-to-shared-household-media-governance/) assigns device and privacy responsibilities.

## Common mistakes and limitations

Avoid treating a household TV as automatically private, relying on profiles as passwords, capturing full histories, disabling essential alerts, and skipping the reopen test.

This workflow reduces avoidable exposure but cannot guarantee privacy on a device controlled by someone else or replace legal, device, and product guidance.

## Run an observer-position check

Stand or sit at each ordinary sightline—the next seat, doorway, reflected surface, and nearby work area—while showing a harmless test screen. Record which labels, artwork, progress, notifications, or account cues are legible without preserving the actual content.

Change the smallest boundary that reduces exposure: exit state, notification preview, screen angle, profile naming, or household handoff rule. Repeat the same positions afterward. Do not treat brightness reduction or inaccessible text as an acceptable privacy fix.

## Frequently asked questions

### Should a profile name use a real name?

Use only enough information for recognition. A privacy-conscious nickname or role may suit a shared screen better.

### Is private browsing enough on a borrowed device?

Do not assume it removes every account, app, or local-media trace. Verify sign-out and next-open state.

### Should every household member see the audit?

Include affected profile owners and coordinators, but do not display unrelated private history.
