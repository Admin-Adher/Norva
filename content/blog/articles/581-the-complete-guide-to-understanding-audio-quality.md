# The Complete Guide to Understanding Audio Quality

> **In short:** Audio quality is a chain: recording, editing and mix, track version, codec and bitrate, sample format, channel layout, loudness and dynamic range, delivery, decoding, downmix or processing, active output route, speakers or headphones, room, playback level, and listener context. Diagnose one fixed excerpt through each layer instead of ranking sound from a single badge.

The same track can sound different on television speakers, headphones, and a multichannel system. Two tracks using the same codec can also differ because their sources, mixes, rates, channel layouts, and encoders differ.

## Start with source and mix

Recording quality, microphone choice and placement, editing, noise reduction, equalisation, dynamics, spatial placement, and mastering shape the source before distribution. Dialogue, music, and effects may be intentionally balanced for a particular presentation.

A later volume increase cannot separate dialogue already masked inside a mix.

## Identify the exact track

Record language, descriptive or alternate mix where applicable, channel label, codec, bitrate context, sample rate, bit depth when exposed, and source version. Track labels are clues whose current meaning must be verified.

[Codec and bitrate answer different questions](/blog/bitrate-and-codec-two-different-audio-questions/), and neither proves the source mix.

## Separate volume and loudness

Volume control changes gain somewhere in the playback chain. Loudness describes perceived level and can be measured through defined programme-loudness methods for specific workflows. Content spectrum, duration, dynamics, channels, reproduction, and listener context matter.

[The volume-versus-loudness guide](/blog/volume-and-loudness-why-they-are-not-identical/) prevents a slider position from being treated as a programme measurement.

## Include dynamic range

The difference between quieter and louder moments can support impact but may challenge a room with noise or a low listening level. Dynamic-range control, night modes, dialogue processing, or normalisation can transform the result when supported.

Do not promise identical behavior across devices; verify official control scope and current state.

## Follow channels, downmix, and routing

A multichannel mix can be decoded, passed through, downmixed, virtualised, or mapped to available speakers. The active route may be device speakers, wired headphones, wireless headphones, a receiver, or another output. Missing or misrouted channels can affect dialogue and ambience.

Use [the source-track-versus-output guide](/blog/source-track-or-output-device-locate-the-quality-limit/) to substitute one layer.

## Include delivery and decoding

Network adaptation, alternate tracks, device codec support, protected playback, and application behavior can affect the selected audio. W3C Media Capabilities models audio configuration fields and support, but current product behavior remains contextual.

Record errors, dropouts, track switches, resampling, and fallback only when exposed. Do not infer a decoder path from sound alone.

## Include output and room

Speaker size and placement, headphones and fit, device processing, equalisation, room reflections, background noise, listening position, and safe level affect the result. Compare outputs at sensibly matched perceived level; louder can be preferred even when the underlying quality is not better.

Never encourage unsafe listening levels to reveal detail.

## Compare responsibly

Use matched excerpts with dialogue, music, transients, quiet ambience, loud effects, stereo movement, and multichannel cues where relevant. Fix level, route, device processing, seat, and room. Alternate order or conceal labels where practical.

ITU-R BS.1116 provides structured subjective methods for small impairments; an informal household check should state its limits.

## Report without overclaiming

Include track and version without private source details, device and software, delivery, decode, output route, processing, speakers or headphones, room, safe level method, exact excerpt, symptom, comparison, and unknowns. Current Norva audio controls and labels require official verification.

Norva organises and plays compatible sources users own or are authorised to use; it does not supply a catalogue or certify every source mix.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does a higher bitrate guarantee better audio?

No. Codec, source, encoder, channels, sample format, content, and playback path also matter.

### Is a multichannel label always better than stereo?

No. The appropriate track depends on source mix, decoding, output layout, downmix, and listener needs.

### Can turning up volume fix unclear dialogue?

It raises other elements too and may not address masking, downmix, output, room noise, or the mix itself.
