# How to Coordinate Accessibility Preferences in a Household

> **In short:** Ask each person what viewing outcome they need, not why they need it. Separate personal preferences from shared-screen defaults, title-specific choices, and device capabilities. Verify the available audio and subtitle options before playback, preserve each person's profile context, and document only the minimum needed to make the next session work.

Accessibility coordination is not a vote in which the majority sets one permanent household default. It is a routing exercise: the right option should reach the right viewer in the right context.

## Start with outcomes and consent

Invite each person to describe useful outcomes such as clearer dialogue, audio description, captions, larger text, reduced motion, or a preferred interface language. Do not ask for a diagnosis or require a person to justify a need.

Agree who may see the resulting preference note. A shared note can say “use audio description when available” without naming a condition or storing private health information.

## Separate four preference layers

Classify every request as one of these:

- **personal:** belongs to one viewer's profile or session;
- **shared-screen:** helps everyone in a particular room;
- **title-specific:** depends on the tracks supplied with one item or version;
- **device-specific:** depends on the TV, browser, phone, remote, or output equipment.

This prevents a living-room choice from being mistaken for a universal account rule. Profiles organise viewing context, but they are not access-control systems or guarantees that every preference will persist everywhere.

## Verify availability instead of assuming it

Accessible alternatives depend on the connected source, media version, metadata, and current player controls. Before a planned viewing session, inspect the visible audio and subtitle lists. For spoken narration, use the process in [how to identify audio description](/blog/how-to-identify-audio-description-when-it-is-available/) rather than trusting a language label alone.

Run a brief sample with the person's consent. Confirm that narration, dialogue, captions, and volume are usable together. A label is evidence to investigate, not proof that a track meets a particular need.

## Use a preference card

Create one small card per viewer:

| Field | Privacy-safe entry |
|---|---|
| Desired outcome | “Dialogue remains understandable” |
| First option | “Preferred language track” |
| Alternative | “Captions plus standard audio” |
| Scope | Personal, room, title, or device |
| Verification | “Sample before group playback” |
| Escalation | “Ask viewer; do not guess” |

Do not include passwords, source addresses, medical details, or a list of titles watched.

## Resolve shared-screen conflicts

When needs differ, prioritise participation rather than habit. Possible solutions include selecting a track everyone can use, enabling captions alongside audio, choosing a different supported version, or using a personal device when the group agrees. Do not silently disable an option because one person is not present; first confirm whose profile and screen context are active.

For recurring shared-profile situations, [define an audio-selection rule](/blog/how-to-choose-audio-on-a-shared-household-profile/) that says who chooses, when to ask, and how to restore the previous state.

## Original evidence: coordination sheet

Use four columns: viewer outcome, scope, verified option, and fallback. Test the sheet with one familiar title on one supported device. Then repeat on a second device only if that context matters.

Record whether the option appeared, whether it worked during a short sample, and whether it remained selected after leaving playback. The result is a local operating note, not a product guarantee.

## Review without surveillance

Review the card when a device changes, a source changes, a person requests an update, or a repeated mismatch occurs. Do not inspect private history to infer whether someone used an accessibility option. Ask directly and accept that preferences can vary by title or day.

Place this routine inside a broader [household media-governance agreement](/blog/the-complete-guide-to-shared-household-media-governance/) so responsibility, privacy, and escalation are already clear.

## Common mistakes and limitations

Avoid one household-wide default, medical-detail collection, assumptions based on language labels, unapproved profile changes, and claims that a preference will persist across every source or device.

## Frequently asked questions

### Should one person's preference become the household default?

Only when everyone affected agrees and the chosen context supports it. Personal needs should otherwise remain personal or session-specific.

### Can a profile protect accessibility information from other users?

A profile organises viewing context; it should not be treated as a password or privacy boundary. Store only minimal, non-medical notes.

### What if the requested option is not listed?

Check the selected version and source, verify the current controls, try no destructive changes, and document the missing option for the appropriate source owner or support path.
