# When to Separate Active and Archive Collections

> **In short:** Create an archive view when infrequently used material makes daily browsing harder or consumes disproportionate maintenance. Archive status should preserve identity, source context, and a return route; it is not deletion. Use written criteria, test a reversible sample, and review archived groups seasonally or when household needs change.

An active collection is optimised for current retrieval. An archive is organised for retention and occasional access. Keeping them distinct can reduce browsing noise without declaring older or infrequent material worthless.

## Look for signs that one view serves two purposes

Consider separation when:

- essential browse paths contain many rarely chosen items;
- category maintenance is dominated by inactive material;
- duplicate or uncertain items obscure current versions;
- a source is retained mainly for occasional reference;
- household members disagree because one values retention and another values simplicity;
- growth repeatedly recreates empty or low-use categories.

Do not use a single recent-use threshold as an automatic rule. Some material matters precisely because it is rare.

## Define active status positively

An item or group may remain active when it supports at least one documented need:

- currently in progress;
- selected for a near-term household plan;
- frequently searched or browsed;
- required for a current multilingual or accessibility need;
- part of an active series or version set;
- needed to resolve a current collection decision.

Link these criteria to [findability goals](/blog/set-library-findability-goals/) rather than a subjective idea of popularity.

## Define archive candidacy carefully

Archive candidates may be:

- complete and inactive series;
- superseded but historically meaningful versions;
- material retained for occasional reference;
- source groups no longer central to daily viewing;
- resolved projects or seasonal collections;
- duplicates retained pending a future rights or quality decision.

Archive status should not hide uncertainty. Use a separate review queue for ambiguous rights, unreadable material, or unconfirmed duplicates.

## Preserve the path back

Every archive group needs:

- a stable label;
- source and ownership context;
- a broad content description;
- date and reason archived;
- person who approved it;
- retrieval route;
- return-to-active trigger;
- next review date.

NARA inventory guidance emphasises describing groups and systems with location and context. Although its rules concern records, the principle helps a household avoid creating an unlabelled storage attic.

## Choose logical or physical separation

**Logical separation** uses categories, views, tags, or filters while the underlying source remains unchanged. It is usually easier to reverse.

**Physical separation** moves files or media between storage systems. It introduces format, integrity, rights, and recovery concerns and should occur only through authorised supported processes.

Norva organises compatible authorised sources; it does not grant rights to move source media. Test logical organisation first when it satisfies the goal.

## Avoid confusing archive, unavailable, and removed

- **Archive** is a household organisation decision.
- **Unavailable** can describe source access for an item or version.
- **Removed** means the item or reference no longer appears in the relevant view.

These states need different evidence and approval. Do not relabel an unavailable item as archived merely to hide a source problem.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Treating “not watched recently” as permission to delete.
- Creating an archive with no inventory or retrieval route.
- Physically moving media when a logical view would suffice.
- Archiving unresolved duplicates as if they were reviewed.
- Letting one household member decide shared retention alone.
- Never testing whether archived material can return.
- Maintaining active and archive copies as uncontrolled duplicates.

An archive does not guarantee long-term preservation. Storage, integrity, formats, and source conditions still require appropriate review.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is archive status permanent?

It should be reviewable. Define return triggers such as renewed use, a household request, source change, or restoration of missing context.

### Should archived items disappear from search?

That depends on the agreed workflow. Some households need broad search with an archive label; others prefer an explicit archive route. Test findability before deciding.

### Does an archive need separate storage?

Not necessarily. A logical archive view may meet the goal without moving media. Physical changes require stronger recovery and rights checks.
