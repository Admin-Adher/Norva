# How to Recognize a Fake Media Support Request

> **In short:** Treat unexpected support contact as unverified, especially when it creates urgency or requests a password, one-time code, recovery code, source credential, payment, download, or remote control. Stop using that channel and open the known official support page independently. Preserve non-sensitive evidence, report the impersonation, and follow the credential-exposure plan immediately if you shared a secret or approved access.

A familiar logo, accurate account detail, or convincing profile name does not prove who sent a message. Information can be copied from public pages, previous messages, or a compromised contact. Verification must happen through a destination you select independently.

## Notice the pressure pattern

Support impersonation often creates a problem and demands an immediate exception. The message may claim that an account will close, a payment failed, a device was compromised, or a benefit will disappear unless you act in minutes.

Pause when the sender discourages you from opening the official website, speaking to another household administrator, or ending the call. Urgency is a reason to verify more carefully, not to surrender control.

## Reject requests for secrets or control

Do not disclose a password, recovery code, one-time authentication code, live reset link, session token, or credential for a compatible authorized source. Do not type a secret into a page opened from an unsolicited message.

Also stop if the person asks you to install unfamiliar software, enable remote control, share the whole screen, disable a security control, move a conversation to a private channel, or pay through an unusual method. The FTC identifies unsolicited calls, pop-ups, and demands for remote access or payment as common support-scam patterns.

## Inspect identity without trusting appearances

Lookalike domains, substituted letters, shortened links, copied avatars, and display names can appear legitimate at a glance. A caller ID or sender label is not independent proof.

Use the [official sign-in destination guide](/blog/verify-official-sign-in-destination/) to avoid navigating from the suspicious message. Type the known address, use a trusted bookmark, or open the installed application through its normal route. Never let the sender dictate the verification channel.

## Verify through official Norva support

End the unsolicited interaction. Open `https://norva.tv/support` directly and ask whether the contact or case is genuine. Provide only the minimum identifiers and a redacted copy of the suspicious message.

The [support-screenshot redaction guide](/blog/redact-support-screenshot-safely/) helps remove passwords, codes, tokens, addresses, notifications, and unrelated personal information. Keep an unmodified original privately if it may be needed as evidence.

## Respond according to what happened

If you only received the request, block or report it through the channel's official controls after preserving the sender, time, destination, and message. Do not continue the conversation to investigate personally.

If you clicked but entered nothing, close the page and review browser or device state using current official guidance. If you installed software, enabled remote access, paid, or shared any secret, move to a known-good device and use the [credential-exposure incident plan](/blog/credential-exposure-incident-plan/). Contact the relevant payment provider through its official route when money is involved.

Record actions as **requested**, **pending**, or **confirmed**. A report submission, password-change request, or remote removal is not complete until the official service confirms the intended state.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Trusting a logo, caller ID, profile badge, or display name by itself.
- Calling a number displayed in a suspicious pop-up.
- Sending a code to prove account ownership.
- Installing a remote-control tool before independent verification.
- Arguing with the sender instead of ending the channel.
- Reporting through a link supplied by the suspected impersonator.
- Assuming a reported message means exposed access is contained.

## Frequently asked questions

### Can support ask for account information?

Official support may need limited case information, but never send passwords, recovery codes, one-time codes, live reset links, session tokens, or authorized-source credentials.

### Is a verified-looking social profile safe?

Appearance alone is not proof. End the contact and verify the case through Norva's known official support destination.

### What if I already gave the sender a code?

Use a known-good device, secure the affected recovery and account boundaries, review sessions, and begin the exposure plan immediately.
