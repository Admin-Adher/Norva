# Your First Norva Privacy Review

> **In short:** Inventory the categories of data used during onboarding, not their secret values. Review account details, profile labels, connected-source information, device records, viewing context, support evidence, and any offline copies. For each category, document its purpose, who can see it, how long it is needed, and which official control applies. Remove unnecessary exposure and schedule another review.

A privacy review turns scattered setup choices into an understandable household record. It is not a legal opinion and should not replace the current Norva privacy policy or terms. Start with the [new-account security guide](/blog/secure-new-norva-account/) because unauthorized access and unnecessary disclosure often overlap.

## Read current official disclosures

Open the privacy policy, terms, and support pages through norva.tv. Record the review date and the section relevant to each question. Policies can change, so do not rely on a screenshot or old summary as the sole source.

Use the [privacy-disclosure evaluation](/blog/evaluate-norva-privacy-disclosures/) for a deeper pre-subscription reading of purposes, recipients, retention, rights, and contact routes.

## Inventory categories, not secrets

List broad categories used during setup: account and contact data, profile labels, device and session information, connected-source configuration, viewing progress, favorites, preferences, support records, and conditional offline media. Do not copy passwords, tokens, source addresses, full titles, or precise device identifiers into the inventory.

Norva describes itself as a media player and organizer. Users connect a compatible source they own or are legally authorized to use; the subscription does not include a media catalog.

## Map purpose and visibility

For every category, ask why it is needed and who can see it: the account holder, another household profile, a shared television, the device operating system, or Norva under the disclosed service flow. A profile label that is harmless on a personal phone may reveal too much on a shared TV.

Replace unnecessary full names, locations, and source clues with neutral labels. Data minimization should preserve the function while reducing exposure.

## Review source and device boundaries

Confirm that source credentials remain only in the intended protected workflow and are absent from screenshots, notes, profile names, or support messages. Reconcile current trusted-device records with known onboarding events and remove obsolete access through available official controls.

Record decisions, not authentication material. A simple "recognized shared TV, kept" is safer than copying its complete technical record.

## Inspect viewing context

Review progress, history, favorites, recommendations, and preferences visible to each profile or shared screen. Norva can use source-derived information for organization and recommendations and can keep supported viewing context across devices. The household should still decide which profiles are shared and which labels are appropriate.

Actual media, track availability, and source data remain dependent on the connected source and rights.

## Review support evidence

Search the household's recent screenshots, screen recordings, exported logs, and messages created during onboarding. Delete unnecessary duplicates and redact private material before any future sharing. Use only official support routes.

Keep a minimal case timeline rather than a large media archive. Support evidence can reveal more than the original issue if notifications, faces, account identifiers, or catalog titles are visible.

## Review conditional offline copies

Where supported and authorized, offline media is described as encrypted on the device and not uploaded to Norva. Check which device holds the copy, why it is still needed, when it should be removed, and what happens if the device is lost or shared.

Do not treat local encryption as permission to retain media indefinitely or as a substitute for device security.

## Common privacy-review mistakes

- Copying secret values into the audit itself.
- Reviewing only the account page and ignoring shared screens.
- Keeping pairing codes or source details in screenshots.
- Assuming a custom profile name is private.
- Retaining support recordings without a purpose.
- Treating policy summaries as permanently current.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does the review require listing every title watched?

No. Record the category and where it appears. Avoid reproducing viewing history unless a specific, legitimate diagnostic need requires a minimized example.

### Is this review a substitute for the privacy policy?

No. It is a household operational checklist. The current official policy and terms remain the sources to review and fact-check.

### How often should the household repeat it?

Use triggers such as a new device, source, profile, support case, offline workflow, household change, or updated policy, plus a periodic review interval chosen by the account holder.
