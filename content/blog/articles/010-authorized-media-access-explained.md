# What “Use Only Media You’re Authorized to Access” Means in Practice

> **In short:** Use only a media source that you own or have permission to access, within the source’s terms and applicable law. Confirm who granted access, what the permission covers, and whether connecting a third-party player is allowed. Keep credentials private. If the rights are unclear, do not connect the source until you obtain reliable guidance.

This article provides an operational checklist, not legal advice. Copyright, contract, and consumer rules vary by country and source. When a real dispute or uncertainty exists, consult the source owner or a qualified professional.

## Start with the source, not the player

A media player organises and plays compatible information. It does not create permission to access media.

Norva’s Terms of Service say the user must connect a compatible media source they own or are otherwise legally authorised to access. The user is responsible for the source, its terms, and associated rights. The subscription covers the software and its features.

The division between roles is detailed in [Media Player vs. Content Provider](/blog/media-player-vs-content-provider/).

## Four questions to answer before connecting

### 1. Where did the source come from?

Identify the organisation, account, storage location, or rights holder that supplied it. A link forwarded without context is not a reliable permission record.

### 2. Who owns the account or media?

Confirm whether it is your personal account, a household account you are permitted to use, employer-managed access, or media you own. Do not assume that possession of credentials establishes permission.

### 3. What does the permission cover?

Read the relevant terms. Permission may be limited by user, household, device, territory, duration, or method of access. Do not convert a narrow permission into a broader one.

### 4. Is connection to this player permitted?

A source can be authentic yet restrict how it is accessed. Check its documentation or ask the source owner before connecting third-party software.

If you cannot answer these questions with a primary record, pause the setup.

## Create a simple authorisation record

For each source, keep a private record containing:

| Field | What to record |
| --- | --- |
| Source owner or operator | The named person or organisation |
| Account holder | Who controls the authorised account |
| Basis of access | Ownership, subscription, licence, or explicit permission |
| Relevant terms | A current URL or dated copy |
| Device or household limits | Only what the terms actually state |
| Connection permission | Documentation or confirmation |
| Review date | When the information was last checked |
| Contact route | Where to ask about changes |

This template does not prove legal compliance by itself. It helps prevent forgotten assumptions and gives a reviewer a factual starting point.

## Protect connection information

Source credentials and private links can provide access to account resources. Treat them like passwords:

- do not publish them in screenshots;
- do not paste them into public forums;
- use secure, supported connection forms;
- verify the site or app before entering them;
- remove access from devices you no longer control;
- change credentials if exposure is suspected;
- avoid sharing one credential beyond what the source permits.

Norva’s privacy policy explains that source settings are used to connect the service to the source on the user’s behalf. It also identifies service providers involved in operating Norva. Read the current policy before adding sensitive settings.

The dedicated [credential-protection guide](/blog/protect-media-source-credentials/) provides a fuller security workflow.

## Permission and availability are separate

An item appearing in a catalogue does not automatically answer whether you may use it. Conversely, an authorised source can temporarily fail or mark an item unavailable.

Avoid making rights decisions from interface labels alone. Use the account agreement, licence, owner’s permission, and applicable law as the relevant records.

The World Intellectual Property Organization provides general background on copyright, but only local, situation-specific advice can resolve a legal question.

## Situations that require a pause

Do not continue when:

- the source’s owner is unknown;
- access was obtained through an unsolicited message;
- terms prohibit the intended connection method;
- credentials belong to someone who did not grant permission;
- a trial or employment relationship has ended;
- territorial or household restrictions are unclear;
- the source requests unsafe software or credential sharing.

A player’s technical ability to connect is not a statement that the connection is authorised.

## Common misconceptions and limitations

“Publicly reachable” does not mean “free to use.” “A friend shared it” does not establish permission. “The app accepted it” proves only technical compatibility, not rights.

This checklist cannot interpret every licence or national law. It should not be used to make a legal conclusion in a contested case. Norva’s public terms may also change, so review the current version when connecting a new source.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does owning a media player subscription include media access?

Norva states that its subscription covers its software and features. You must provide a compatible source you are authorised to use.

### Can I connect a household source?

Only if the source terms and account holder permit the intended household and device use. Do not infer permission from shared access alone.

### What should I do when permission ends?

Disconnect the source, remove saved credentials from devices you control, and follow the source owner’s account instructions.
