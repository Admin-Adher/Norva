# How Audio-Language Filters Improve Library Browsing

> **In short:** An audio-language filter lets you narrow the catalogue before opening each title. Use it to create a likely candidate set, then confirm the actual track on the title or playback screen. Available languages depend on the connected source and the individual media item, so the filter is a discovery aid rather than a universal guarantee.

Language can be a hard requirement rather than a preference. If several people are choosing together, finding out after playback begins wastes time. A good filter brings that decision earlier while keeping the final track choice visible.

## Move language into the browsing question

Start with a complete sentence: “Show me titles in this category that are listed with French audio,” or “Find recent films with an English track.” The sentence establishes a hierarchy:

1. choose the type or category;
2. filter by the required audio language;
3. add another condition only when the result remains too large;
4. verify the selected title before playback.

This is more reliable than treating a language label as a permanent category. A title can have several audio tracks, and categories are poorly suited to representing combinations.

For the related text-track workflow, read [how subtitle-language filters save time](/blog/subtitle-language-library-filters/).

## Understand what the filter can and cannot prove

The catalogue display is based on metadata from the compatible source you are authorised to use. A language field may describe an available track, a particular version, or the information known to the source. It should not be interpreted beyond what the title details actually show.

Use this distinction:

- **Filter result:** a title is a candidate based on available metadata.
- **Title details:** the item exposes the options associated with that version.
- **Playback selection:** the chosen track is applied.
- **Account preference:** a preferred language may be retained when that option exists.

Norva can retain language and subtitle preferences across supported devices. The actual languages and subtitles still depend on the source and media.

## Use a four-stage language check

Apply this repeatable check to three representative titles:

| Stage | Action | What to confirm |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Browse | Apply one audio-language filter | The result becomes more relevant |
| Inspect | Open a title | The listed version shows the needed option |
| Select | Review the available audio choices | The intended track can be selected |
| Resume | Reopen on a supported screen | The current preference behaves as expected |

This is a user-side verification method, not a claim that every source labels tracks identically. Record any mismatch between the filter label and the title options before changing broader library settings.

The step-by-step guide to [choosing an audio track](/blog/choose-audio-track/) focuses on the final selection rather than catalogue filtering.

## Handle multilingual versions carefully

When several versions of one title exist, language may be one reason to keep them distinct or group them as variants. Before grouping:

- confirm they represent the same work;
- compare their available audio and subtitle options;
- identify which version should be the default;
- keep the version choice understandable after grouping.

Do not merge entries solely because the artwork and title match. The [version-grouping decision guide](/blog/group-multiple-media-versions/) covers identity and trade-offs.

## Combine language with one useful refinement

Useful combinations include:

- audio language plus category;
- audio language plus a broad year range;
- audio language plus current availability;
- audio language plus Favorites.

Avoid stacking audio, subtitles, year, score, category, and availability immediately. If the result is empty, remove one condition at a time. This reveals whether the language field is missing, the catalogue truly has no match, or another filter is responsible.

## Save a preference without hiding exceptions

A saved audio preference reduces repetitive selection when the preferred track exists. It cannot create a missing track and should not make alternative tracks inaccessible. Review [setting audio and subtitle preferences](/blog/set-audio-subtitle-language-preferences/) for the account-level workflow.

When a shared profile serves people with different needs, selecting per title may be clearer than repeatedly changing one global preference. Separate profiles can help keep preferences distinct when the account plan supports the required number of profiles.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Assuming a poster or translated title proves an audio track exists.
- Treating “multilingual” as a precise list without opening the details.
- Leaving several filters active and blaming the language control for an empty result.
- Confusing audio language with subtitle language.
- Expecting a preference to override options unavailable in the source.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does an audio-language filter guarantee that every result will play in that language?

It identifies candidates using the metadata available to the organiser. Confirm the actual track on the selected title or playback controls because options depend on the source, version, and media.

### Should audio and subtitle filters be used together?

Yes, when both are requirements. Apply one first, inspect the result, then add the second so you can understand which condition narrows the list.

### Why does the preferred language not appear on one title?

The preferred track may not be available for that version, or the source metadata may differ. Inspect the title and choose among the options actually provided.
