# A Monthly Routine for Keeping Catalog Clutter Under Control

> **In short:** Once a month, review what entered the catalogue, inspect exceptions and broken relationships, sample category and metadata drift, then complete one small reversible improvement. Compare the same indicators with the previous month. If a migration, repeated import failure, or widespread mismatch appears, stop routine maintenance and open a bounded cleanup project.

Monthly maintenance works because it finds causes while they are still recent. It should not become a recurring attempt to perfect every label.

## Prepare a repeatable maintenance card

Keep one dated card with:

- catalogue and source snapshot date;
- new or changed item count where available;
- uncategorised, orphaned, failed, and unresolved counts;
- categories or version groups reviewed;
- personal-context samples checked;
- one improvement selected;
- exceptions, owner, and next date.

Use the same definitions each month. A trend is only meaningful when “orphaned” or “unresolved” has not silently changed.

## Pass 1: review intake drift

Look at a sample of recently added or refreshed items. Check whether they entered the expected section, category, series, and version group. Review partial-import or mapping alerts before manually moving records.

Questions to ask:

- Did a source introduce a new label or spelling?
- Did an old category reappear?
- Are multiple versions grouping as expected?
- Did an item arrive without enough identity metadata?
- Is one exception type growing month over month?

Use [the uncategorised-item triage](/blog/triage-uncategorized-media/) when the intake path is unclear.

## Pass 2: inspect relationship health

Sample series boundaries, grouped versions, and items reachable only through search. Run [the orphan-finding workflow](/blog/find-orphaned-library-items/) on any suspicious records.

Do not measure health by total card count alone. A smaller catalogue can still have wrong parent-child links, while a large one may be well organised.

Include at least one partly watched item and one favourite in the sample. Confirm that normal refresh has not separated visible records from expected personal context.

## Pass 3: review structure and language

Choose a rotating section rather than scanning everything monthly. Review:

- empty, stale, or overlapping categories;
- category-name consistency;
- title, year, genre, language, and subtitle anomalies;
- version labels that no longer explain their differences;
- unusually weak artwork or synopsis matches.

Rotate by source, category family, alphabetic range, or series. Record the boundary so coverage advances instead of repeatedly examining the first screen.

## Pass 4: complete one bounded improvement

Select one high-value, well-understood, reversible batch. Examples include correcting a category mapping, resolving a small orphan queue, standardising one category family, or reviewing five uncertain version groups.

Capture the baseline, expected count, validation task, and rollback. Close the batch only after reconciliation and a normal refresh. Leave additional opportunities in the backlog rather than expanding scope mid-session.

## Know when routine maintenance is insufficient

Open a dedicated project when you see:

- a source migration or reconnection;
- repeated partial imports;
- large-scale identity conflicts;
- personal context unexpectedly missing;
- a destructive change affecting many records;
- several months of the same growing exception.

Use [the clean-enough exit criteria](/blog/know-when-to-stop-cleaning/) to keep the monthly session bounded. The Library of Congress recommends identifying and organising personal digital materials and making preservation an ongoing practice; regular, documented attention is more sustainable than emergency cleanup.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Reviewing only visually prominent categories.
- Fixing symptoms without checking intake rules.
- Changing measurement definitions between months.
- Opening many cleanup batches at once.
- Ignoring already watched or rarely browsed items.
- Treating every source-side change as local clutter.

Monthly sampling cannot prove every record is correct. It is an early-warning and maintenance routine, with escalation for systemic issues.

## Frequently asked questions

### Must the routine happen exactly every 30 days?

No. Choose a regular cadence that matches source changes and household use. Consistent scope and follow-through matter more than the exact date.

### How long should it take?

Bound it by passes and one improvement, not a universal minute count. If diagnosis expands, record the evidence and schedule a separate project.

### What if nothing appears wrong?

Record the samples and results, then stop. A month without corrective edits is a valid maintenance outcome.
