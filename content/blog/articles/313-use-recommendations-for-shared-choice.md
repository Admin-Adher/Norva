# A Recommendation Workflow for Shared Viewing Decisions

> **In short:** Agree on the session before opening recommendations: who is watching, available time, required language or subtitles, acceptable formats, and any private exclusions. Choose one seed, verify no more than a small set of candidates, and apply a decision rule everyone accepts. Keep a ready fallback and decide which profile owns the resulting progress.

Group browsing becomes slow when each person silently evaluates different criteria. A shared brief turns recommendations into evidence for one decision instead of a sequence of negotiations.

## Build the group brief

Record only what the decision needs:

| Fallback |  |

W3C media accessibility requirements establish that captions, subtitles, descriptions, and language alternatives are functional needs. Treat them as must-haves, not optional tie-breakers.

## Protect private preferences

Allow each person to state a pass privately without explaining a sensitive reason. The facilitator records only “not suitable for this session.” Do not expose personal history or turn past viewing into a public profile of taste.

The group can distinguish **must-have**, **nice-to-have**, and **not for tonight**. A title rejected for the current session can remain a valid personal recommendation.

## Choose and verify one seed

Select a title everyone understands as the starting reference. State what should remain—creator, period, theme, tone brief, or format—and what may change. Then inspect source-based recommendations without claiming a specific internal ranking method.

Norva does not provide the media catalogue; suggestions and availability depend on the connected compatible source. DCMI metadata fields help explain relationships, while exact product behavior requires current verification.

## Build a small ready shortlist

Use [the focused-shortlist workflow](/blog/turn-recommendations-into-shortlist/) and verify each candidate’s exact work, version, duration, language, subtitles, and source state. Apply [the version-readiness gate](/blog/verify-version-before-following-recommendation/) before presenting a candidate as playable.

Three candidates are often enough for the card, but this is a facilitation example, not a universal limit. Stop earlier when a clear consensus exists or later only when the group agrees that no candidate fits.

## Apply one transparent decision rule

Choose before seeing the finalists:

- **Consensus:** every participant accepts one candidate.
- **Ranked choice:** each person privately ranks the verified candidates; the agreed aggregation rule decides.
- **Rotation:** the designated chooser selects among candidates that meet all must-haves.
- **Random among approved:** use only after everyone accepts the eligible set.

Do not invent a Norva voting feature. The rule can be applied verbally or on a private card.

If browsing begins to drift, use [the three-hop stop rule](/blog/three-hop-discovery-session/) and return to the brief.

## Close the shared session

Confirm the active profile, exact selected version, and checkpoint before playback. After viewing, decide whether the title becomes a shared favorite, a personal favorite, a recommendation for someone else, or no saved state. Keep rejected candidates only when a future action exists.

Also remove the temporary group brief when its occasion ends. Retain only the chosen checkpoint and any candidate with a newly agreed future session, preventing one evening’s compromises from becoming permanent household preferences.

## Original evidence: shared decision card

Pilot the card with one non-sensitive session. Record the brief, candidate evidence, access readiness, rule, final choice, and fallback. Ask each participant afterward whether their must-have was represented without revealing their private exclusions.

The card measures decision transparency in one group. It does not claim satisfaction rates or recommendation performance.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Browsing before agreeing on constraints.
- Treating accessibility needs as tie-breakers.
- Asking people to justify private exclusions.
- Presenting unavailable candidates as ready.
- Changing the decision rule after seeing choices.
- Saving every rejected title to a shared list.

## Frequently asked questions

### What if no candidate meets every must-have?

Use the fallback, revise one optional constraint by consent, or leave the session unfilled. Do not override a required access need.

### Can one person choose the seed?

Yes, when the group agrees the seed represents the brief and does not determine the final choice unfairly.

### Which profile should record progress?

Choose explicitly based on the household’s verified profile arrangement before playback begins.
