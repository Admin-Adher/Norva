# How to Review Series Episodes Efficiently on a Tablet

> **In short:** Review episodes by moving through the series hierarchy in order: series, season, episode, version, progress, then next action. Keep one visible identity field from each level and avoid selecting by artwork alone. A short episode ledger makes the review reliable when titles are similar or a handoff is planned.

A tablet is well suited to comparing episode rows because it can show more context than a phone while remaining touch-friendly. Efficiency comes from a stable review sequence, not from opening every episode.

## Set a clear review goal

Choose one outcome before entering the series page:

- find the next unwatched episode;
- confirm where a previous session stopped;
- compare available versions;
- review a season without changing progress;
- prepare a handoff to another supported screen.

If you are only planning, avoid starting playback merely to inspect metadata. An accidental start may affect history or progress depending on observed product behaviour.

## 1. Confirm the series identity

Check the full series title and any visible year or artwork context. Similar titles, remakes, specials, and grouped versions can create ambiguity. Do not rely on a cropped poster alone.

Norva organises the catalogue from a compatible source you connect. Metadata completeness depends on that source, so a missing field should be treated as unknown rather than inferred.

## 2. Select the season deliberately

Read the season label before touching an episode row. If the interface returns to the last opened season, confirm it instead of assuming season one. Note whether specials are presented as a numbered season, separate group, or source-defined label.

W3C guidance on headings and labels supports descriptive structure, but the exact hierarchy must be observed in the current interface.

**Observable result:** the season heading and the episode list agree.

## 3. Scan identity before progress

For each candidate episode, read the episode number and title first. Then inspect duration or description only as secondary confirmation. Finally review any progress indicator.

This order reduces a common error: choosing the row with a familiar progress bar while overlooking that it belongs to an adjacent episode.

Record three rows at most before making a decision. A short local comparison is easier to verify than a long copied list.

## 4. Separate episode choice from version choice

First choose the intended episode. Only then compare grouped variants or source-specific versions. Check visible fields such as source label, duration, audio, subtitles, or quality label without assuming that every field is available or accurate.

Use the [tablet version-comparison workflow](/blog/how-to-compare-media-versions-on-a-tablet-detail-screen/) when several variants look alike. Language and subtitle availability depends on the source and media.

## 5. Define the next action

Choose one action: resume, start, add or remove a favourite where supported, return to browsing, or prepare a handoff. Before activation, restate the identity in one line: “series — season — episode — version.”

For a mid-episode move, follow the [wrong-episode prevention handoff](/blog/how-to-hand-off-mid-episode-without-opening-the-wrong-episode/) rather than trusting the first highlighted row.

The ledger is intentionally small. Fill it from visible interface evidence, then compare the chosen row once more before activation. Do not add inferred air dates, ordering, or track availability.

## Preserve context while returning

When leaving an episode detail view, verify that the season and approximate row remain visible. If the page resets, use the ledger to rebuild the route. Note active filters or grouped-version settings that might change the list.

The [complete tablet viewing workflow](/blog/the-complete-guide-to-tablet-viewing-workflows/) adds account, audio, interruption, and close-session checks around this review.

## Common mistakes and limitations

Avoid selecting from artwork alone, assuming the first row is next, confusing series progress with episode progress, comparing versions before confirming the episode, and starting playback only to reveal metadata. Source metadata may contain gaps or unexpected ordering.

This workflow does not determine canonical episode order or correct source metadata. If labels conflict, document what is visible and verify against the authorised source rather than editing history through guesswork.

## Frequently asked questions

### Should I trust a “next” label without checking the episode?

Use it as a clue, then confirm series, season, episode, and visible progress. A label should not replace identity verification.

### How many episodes should I compare at once?

Usually the previous, candidate, and following episode provide enough local context. Expand only when the season structure is unusual.

### Can saved progress identify the right version?

Progress helps, but similarly named variants can still differ. Confirm the version fields visible on the detail screen.
