# What a Signs-and-Songs Subtitle Track Is Designed to Show

> **In short:** A signs-and-songs track is generally intended to present selected written information visible in the programme and song lyrics relevant to the audience. It is not necessarily a full dialogue subtitle track or a complete caption track. Because naming and coverage vary, verify the exact label and sample ordinary dialogue, a visible sign, and a song.

The label describes an editorial cue scope, not a universal technical format. The track may use the same timed-text technology as other subtitles while containing a different selection of cues.

## Understand the intended scope

The track may include translations or transcriptions of:

- signs, letters, messages, or location cards;
- text shown inside the scene;
- song lyrics that matter to the programme;
- selected written information not already clear to the intended viewer.

It may omit most spoken dialogue. It may also omit non-speech sounds that a caption track would include for accessibility.

## Read the label cautiously

Possible labels can be abbreviated, localised, or incomplete. Record the exact text and language. Do not classify every limited subtitle track as signs and songs, and do not infer scope from list position.

Use [the subtitle language-label guide](/blog/how-to-read-subtitle-language-labels-without-guessing/) when two entries share a language or role is absent.

## Run a three-scene sample

Choose:

1. an ordinary dialogue scene;
2. a scene containing meaningful on-screen writing;
3. a scene with a song or lyrics where available.

Record whether cues appear in each. If no relevant sign or song exists in the tested item, mark that part not tested rather than absent.

## Original evidence: cue-scope worksheet

| Scene type | Cue expected for test | Observed result |
|---|---|---|
| Ordinary dialogue | Continuous text if full | Present/absent/unclear |
| Visible writing | Selected text | Present/absent/not tested |
| Song | Lyrics | Present/absent/not tested |
| Non-speech event | Caption-style information | Present/absent/not tested |

Classify the track only after the relevant scenes provide enough evidence.

## Compare with forced and full tracks

A forced track often targets selected passages needed by an intended audience, while a full subtitle track aims for broader dialogue coverage. Signs and songs may overlap with forced cues but should not automatically be treated as identical.

Use [the forced-versus-full comparison](/blog/forced-or-full-subtitles-how-the-tracks-differ/) to keep role and automatic state separate.

## Compare with captions

Captions are intended to support access to audio information and can include speaker identification and relevant non-speech sounds, depending on the resource. A signs-and-songs track should not be selected as a substitute merely because it displays some text.

The [caption accessibility guide](/blog/the-complete-guide-to-caption-accessibility/) explains coverage, readability, and user needs.

## Choose the right track

Select signs and songs when the viewer understands the main dialogue and wants the written or musical elements the track actually covers. Choose a verified full subtitle or caption track when broader dialogue or audio information is needed.

Ask the viewer and verify the item. Do not infer need from private viewing history.

## Account for versions and episodes

Another media version may use a different label or cue set. A series may provide the role inconsistently. Record item, episode, version, device, and selected state beside every conclusion.

If the track disappears or changes, preserve the list and compare one context rather than editing source resources.

## Report a misleading label

Include exact label, language, item/version, three-scene results, steps, expected role, and observed scope. Use privacy-safe selector screenshots, but do not attach media, credentials, source addresses, or private history.

## Common mistakes and limitations

Avoid treating signs and songs as full subtitles, assuming lyrics are always included, calling every limited track forced, and concluding “missing” from a title with no testable scene.

The source supplies the cue set and label. A player can expose supported timed text but cannot guarantee editorial completeness.

## Frequently asked questions

### Will a signs-and-songs track translate all dialogue?

Usually that is not its intended scope. Verify the actual cues and choose a full track when continuous dialogue coverage is needed.

### Is it a caption track?

Not necessarily. It may omit speaker and non-speech audio information expected from captions.

### Why does the track show nothing in one scene?

The scene may contain no targeted sign or song. Test a relevant scene before diagnosing absence.
