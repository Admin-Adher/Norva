# How to Turn Recommendations Into a Focused Shortlist

> **In short:** Give the shortlist one occasion and one viewer brief. Keep a candidate only when its relationship is explainable, exact version is verified, access needs are met, and it adds a distinct choice. Separate Ready from Research, release duplicates and weak fits, and set an expiry so temporary discovery does not become permanent favorite clutter.

A recommendation row maximises possibilities; a shortlist reduces them to a decision. The transformation requires gates, not copying cards into another list.

## Define the shortlist contract

Write:

- intended viewer or group;
- occasion and session length;
- required language, subtitles, or format;
- source and supported-screen context;
- qualities to preserve from the seed;
- qualities to vary;
- decision date or expiry.

Do not create a general “maybe someday” list. That is a different curation problem.

## Apply the five-gate funnel

| Gate | Pass question |
|---|---|
| Brief fit | Does it answer the current discovery question? |
| Explainability | Can visible metadata explain the relation? |
| Version readiness | Is the exact usable version known? |
| Session fit | Are duration and access needs compatible? |
| Distinct value | Does it add a real alternative? |

Failing a gate does not always reject the work. Unknown version or availability moves it to Research. Weak relevance or duplicate value moves it to Release.

## Verify before classifying Ready

Use [the recommendation version check](/blog/verify-version-before-following-recommendation/) for edition, episode, duration, language, subtitles, source, and supported-screen context. W3C media accessibility requirements make clear that captions, subtitles, and language alternatives can be essential.

If a relevant card is unavailable, follow [the unavailable-title tree](/blog/handle-unavailable-recommendations/) and preserve its identity outside the ready set.

Norva’s source-based suggestions depend on the connected compatible source. Exact fields and current controls must be verified before publication.

## Keep choices meaningfully different

Two candidates that share every important field can create the illusion of choice. Record one distinct reason for each: different period, creator, format, duration, genre, language, or viewing occasion. DCMI metadata terms provide consistent field names.

If the reason is only “another poster,” release one. If several options repeat the same direction, use a broader seed or [a three-hop discovery session](/blog/three-hop-discovery-session/) to find a genuinely different candidate.

## Set the right shortlist size

Do not impose a universal number. The list is focused when the viewer can compare every ready candidate without reopening discovery. A group may prefer three, while a solo viewer with strict constraints may need only one.

Sort by readiness and occasion fit, not an invented relevance score. Keep Research visibly separate so unresolved versions do not compete with playable choices.

Run a pairwise test when two finalists remain: can the viewer explain the meaningful difference, and would that difference change tonight’s decision? If both answers are no, retain the better-verified candidate and release the duplicate choice. This reduces comparison effort without pretending that one title is universally superior.

## Close and expire the list

After the decision:

- selected title moves into the session;
- strong unselected candidates keep a new explicit occasion or leave;
- research items retain one missing question and review trigger;
- rejected candidates are released;
- durable favorites are created only when a lasting purpose emerges.

Expiry prevents recommendation history from becoming a permanent backlog.

## Original evidence: shortlist funnel

Take ten visible candidates from one bounded session and apply the five gates. Record Pass, Fail, or Unknown with one sentence of evidence. Ask another reviewer whether every Ready candidate can be selected without additional investigation.

The output documents decision quality for that session. It does not claim conversion rates or universal list-size effects.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Creating a shortlist without an occasion.
- Mixing Ready and Research.
- Keeping near-duplicates as variety.
- Ignoring language and subtitle requirements.
- Treating every recommendation as a favorite.
- Leaving the temporary list without an expiry.

## Frequently asked questions

### Can one candidate be enough?

Yes. A shortlist exists to support a decision, not to reach a minimum count.

### Where should research items live?

Use a clearly separate temporary note or current product feature when verified. Do not present them as ready.

### Should unselected titles remain?

Only when each gains a new explicit occasion or durable purpose. Otherwise release them.
