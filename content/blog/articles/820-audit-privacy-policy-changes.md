# How to Audit Changes to a Privacy Policy

> **In short:** Preserve the old and new official versions with their dates, then compare service scope, data categories and examples, purposes, recipients and processors, international destinations, retention, security claims, user controls, rights, contacts, and change-notice methods. Classify each difference as wording, clarification, expansion, reduction, removal, or unknown. Verify material statements against current product settings and support, and never infer legal impact from a text diff alone.

A policy diff finds changed text; an audit explains what the change could mean operationally and which evidence would confirm it. This method supports informed questions and is not legal advice.

## Preserve trustworthy versions

Record the official URL, displayed effective or update date, retrieval date, language, and service version. Keep a lawful local copy or archive reference when permitted. Screenshots can help with layout but are difficult to search and can omit collapsed sections.

The [ten-question privacy-policy guide](/blog/read-privacy-policy-ten-questions/) provides a baseline review for each version before comparison.

## Compare meaning, not only words

A company can rewrite a clause for clarity without changing practice, or change meaning with only a few words. Read complete sections and examples. Track whether a category, purpose, actor, destination, retention criterion, safeguard, or control was added, narrowed, expanded, or removed.

Automated text comparison is useful for discovery but cannot determine materiality by itself.

## Start with scope and definitions

Check the products, countries, people, account types, device platforms, and defined terms covered by each version. A new definition can change how later clauses should be read even when those clauses remain unchanged.

Also verify whether a separate source, store, or payment provider policy is newly referenced.

## Map category-purpose pairs

For every data category, compare concrete examples and stated purposes. A new field under an old heading can matter. A purpose that changes from account delivery to broad improvement language deserves clarification.

Use the [purpose-limitation guide](/blog/purpose-limitation-media-apps/) to document changed purpose language without presenting the change itself as proof of wrongdoing.

## Track providers and destinations

Record added, removed, renamed, or repurposed providers; new subprocessor links; new international regions; and changed notification methods. Confirm corporate renames before treating them as new data flows.

The [third-party processor review](/blog/review-third-party-processors/) provides a provider-by-provider evidence register.

## Compare retention and outcomes

Check fixed periods, start events, account-lifecycle criteria, backups, limited records, deletion, anonymisation, and downstream instructions. Use the [retention-language guide](/blog/data-retention-period-questions/) to avoid converting a changed criterion into an invented deadline.

Policy language should also be compared with current account-closure and local-data controls.

## Review security claims cautiously

Note whether a security statement adds or removes a boundary, qualifier, device dependency, or named measure. "Encrypted" without data state and endpoint is less informative than a scoped statement.

Do not interpret fewer technical details as proof of weaker security, or more details as proof of flawless implementation. Ask for current evidence.

## Verify notice and product behavior

Check whether the provider announced the update through the method described in the earlier policy. Then inspect current settings, permissions, provider pages, store flows, and support material. Record mismatches for clarification.

Norva's live policy currently displays a revision date and describes how material changes may be communicated. Human review must verify the current date and wording.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Comparing only displayed update dates.
- Counting changed words instead of reading meaning.
- Ignoring definitions and service scope.
- Treating a provider rename as automatically new processing.
- Assuming policy language proves implementation.
- Assigning legal consequences without qualified analysis.
- Failing to record source URLs and retrieval dates.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is every policy rewrite a material change?

No. Some edits clarify wording, while others change categories, purposes, actors, controls, or outcomes. Evidence and context determine significance.

### Can an automated diff complete the audit?

No. It finds text changes but cannot reliably interpret scope, operational practice, legal effect, or product behavior.

### What if the old version is unavailable?

Use lawful archives, prior notices, support records, and current provider clarification, while clearly marking gaps and uncertainty.
