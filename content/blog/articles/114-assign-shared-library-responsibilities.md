# How to Assign Responsibilities in a Shared Media Library

> **In short:** Give every recurring task one named executor, and give risky or shared changes a separate approval route. Identify the owner of each authorised source, the reviewer of completed work, and a backup person who understands recovery without receiving secrets unnecessarily. Record roles in the collection charter and revisit them when people or sources change.

Shared responsibility does not mean everyone edits everything. Clear roles protect personal state, source access, and maintenance time while preventing the whole library from depending on one person's memory.

## List the work before assigning people

Include:

- approving collection scope;
- verifying source ownership or authorisation;
- handling new-item intake;
- reviewing metadata exceptions;
- merging proven duplicates;
- maintaining categories;
- reviewing language labels;
- managing archive candidates;
- monitoring source changes;
- documenting migrations;
- testing findability;
- maintaining the decision log;
- preparing safe support evidence.

Remove any task that has no link to the collection purpose.

## Use four practical roles

### Executor

Performs the documented action. Routine reversible work should have one clear executor to avoid duplicated effort.

### Approver

Authorises shared, destructive, privacy-sensitive, or source-level changes. The approver checks evidence and rollback before execution.

### Reviewer

Confirms the result against agreed retrieval or maintenance checks. The reviewer should not merely count completed edits.

### Knowledge backup

Understands where procedures and recovery routes are documented. This role reduces single-person dependency without requiring passwords to be shared broadly.

Put role boundaries in [the shared collection charter](/blog/create-media-collection-charter/).

## Assign source custody explicitly

For each compatible authorised source, record:

- responsible account holder;
- who may connect or disconnect it;
- who verifies current authorisation;
- official recovery route;
- where non-sensitive setup documentation lives;
- change or expiry review date.

The Library of Congress inventory and custody guidance separates knowledge of content from responsibility for it. A household workflow is simpler, but the distinction remains useful.

Never put credentials, tokens, or recovery codes into the responsibility matrix.

## Separate routine and risky actions

Routine actions may include moving uncertain items into a review queue or recording a source change. Risky actions may include:

- removing a source;
- deleting media or catalogue records;
- merging uncertain duplicates;
- clearing profile state;
- changing shared category meanings;
- beginning a migration;
- clearing app data or reinstalling during troubleshooting.

Require approval, evidence, and a rollback route for risky actions.

## Balance the workload

Estimate minutes and frequency for each task. A person who owns every weekly queue, source check, and seasonal review is a failure point even if they volunteer.

Use [the maintenance cadence guide](/blog/design-library-maintenance-cadence/) and [maintenance effort estimator](/blog/estimate-library-maintenance-effort/) to distribute actual workload rather than the number of task names.

## Plan handover

The backup person should be able to find:

- current collection plan and charter;
- source inventory without secrets;
- official recovery routes;
- current maintenance queue;
- last completed review;
- migration and rollback records;
- support contact route.

Test handover with a harmless task, such as locating the latest decision and explaining the next review. Do not simulate account compromise or distribute secrets for the exercise.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Assigning every task to “everyone.”
- Giving the executor automatic approval for destructive work.
- Sharing credentials to create a backup person.
- Counting tasks instead of workload.
- Leaving source ownership implicit.
- Asking a reviewer to approve work with no pass condition.
- Failing to update roles after household changes.

Roles do not override source terms, rights, or personal privacy choices. They document who may act within those boundaries.

## Frequently asked questions

### Can one person hold every role?

In a single-person collection, yes, but the person should still separate execution, review, and recovery steps. In a shared collection, risky changes benefit from another approver.

### Does a backup person need every password?

No. They need documented official recovery routes and appropriate authorised access. Store secrets only through secure approved methods.

### How often should roles be reviewed?

Review them seasonally and whenever a source owner, household member, device, or maintenance cadence changes.
