# How to Resolve Disagreements About Shared Favorites

> **In short:** Determine whether each favorite is a personal reminder or a shared planning item. Personal saves belong to the profile owner. Shared saves need an agreed purpose, removal rule, and review date. Never infer ownership from who used the TV last, and do not delete disputed items until the affected people have confirmed a reversible resolution.

A favorite can mean “I want to watch this,” “we may watch this together,” “do not forget this series,” or simply “I liked it.” Conflict begins when those meanings are mixed in one shared context.

## Identify the real disagreement

Ask each person which problem they are trying to solve. Common conflicts include clutter, accidental removal, surprise recommendations, unclear ownership, or a shared list that no longer supports group planning.

Keep the conversation about organisation. Do not use private viewing history to prove who saved an item or question someone's taste.

## Classify saves by purpose

Use three practical classes:

- **personal reminder:** one person intends to return to the item;
- **group candidate:** several people may choose it for a shared session;
- **reference save:** retained for comparison, discussion, or a future event.

If the current interface does not expose separate labels, record the distinction in a small household note or use separate profiles where appropriate. Do not claim that Norva provides a control unless it is visible and documented in the current product.

## Assign decision rights

The profile owner decides personal saves. People named in a group plan decide group candidates. The account coordinator may maintain the process but should not become the owner of everyone's choices.

This mirrors the broader principle in [shared-category decision governance](/blog/who-decides-when-shared-categories-change/): technical access does not create decision authority.

## Use a no-surprise removal rule

Before removing a disputed shared save:

1. name the item and its current purpose;
2. identify the people affected;
3. ask whether it can move to a personal context;
4. agree a review or expiry date;
5. capture the decision without storing unrelated history;
6. verify the intended list after the change.

For accidental deletions, do not promise recovery. Preserve what is known, avoid broad changes, and use the appropriate documented support path if the state matters.

## Original evidence: decision ladder

Create a five-field card:

| Question | Example outcome |
|---|---|
| Who owns the intention? | One viewer or named group |
| Why is it saved? | Personal return or group choice |
| Who may remove it? | Owner or affected group |
| When is it reviewed? | After the planned session |
| What remains private? | Unrelated history and preferences |

Test the card on three non-sensitive saves. If the household cannot classify one, leave it unchanged and discuss the rule rather than the title.

## Separate governance from taste

No one needs to defend why an item is a favorite. The governance question is whether the save sits in the right context and whether its owner can find it again. A household may agree that a shared list stays short, but that rule should include a safe route for personal saves.

Use the privacy principles in [reviewing profile activity without intrusion](/blog/how-to-review-profile-activity-without-invading-privacy/). Inspect only the state necessary to resolve the dispute, with consent.

## Schedule a light review

Review shared planning items after a group event, when a profile changes ownership, or when the list stops being usable. Avoid daily cleanup. A review should remove obsolete group decisions, not audit personal interests.

Document the rule in the [household media-governance guide](/blog/the-complete-guide-to-shared-household-media-governance/) alongside roles, privacy boundaries, and change approval.

## Common mistakes and limitations

Avoid treating every favorite as shared, deleting items to reduce visual clutter without consent, creating permanent records of personal interests, and assuming profiles enforce secrecy.

Favorite behavior may depend on the current interface, account context, source, item identity, and version grouping. Verify visible behavior before writing a household rule around it.

## Frequently asked questions

### Who owns a favorite on a shared profile?

The interface may not identify an individual owner. The household should agree purpose and decision rights instead of guessing from activity.

### Should old favorites be deleted automatically?

Not without a verified product setting and household consent. Use a review date and confirm ownership before removal.

### Can separate profiles prevent every disagreement?

They reduce mixed context but do not replace communication, account governance, or privacy care on shared screens.
