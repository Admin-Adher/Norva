# How to Choose Audio on a Shared Household Profile

> **In short:** Ask who is watching and what language or accessibility outcome is needed. Verify the candidate track instead of trusting order or a broad label. Choose for participation in the current session, tell the group when the shared context changes, and check the end state. A shared profile is organisational context, not proof of separate preferences for each person.

The goal is not to establish one permanent “household winner.” It is to make each session understandable and accessible without surprising the next viewer.

## Name the session audience

Before playback, ask who is actively watching. A person's preference should not be inferred from their previous history or from who normally uses the room.

When someone needs audio description, a particular spoken language, or reduced subtitle dependence, let that outcome guide the decision. Do not require medical details.

## Verify the options

Open the audio list and read every visible label. Confirm the target language or role with a short sample. Do not assume the first track is original, a duplicate language is a dub, or added narration is audio description.

If the group is deciding between original and dubbed audio, use [the deliberate-choice framework](/blog/original-audio-or-dubbed-track-how-to-make-a-deliberate-choice/).

## Use an inclusion-first rule

Try this order:

1. satisfy a stated accessibility requirement;
2. choose a language everyone understands where possible;
3. add compatible subtitles when useful;
4. verify the selected track and output;
5. agree a fallback before the session starts.

When no configuration works for everyone, consider separate sessions or personal devices with consent. Do not frame separation as punishment for an access need.

## Original evidence: session card

| Field | Privacy-safe entry |
|---|---|
| Audience | Named group or “shared session” |
| Required outcome | “Dialogue understandable” |
| Selected label | Exact visible text |
| Role verified | Standard, dub, description, commentary, or unclear |
| Scope | This session/title/version |
| Fallback | Agreed alternative |
| End-state action | Recheck or restore |

Do not record diagnoses, private title history, or source credentials.

## Communicate the change

Say what changed and why in neutral terms: “This session uses the described English track.” Avoid statements about who “always changes settings.”

If the shared profile has a broad preference, explain whether the session choice is intended as a title-specific exception. The [preference-versus-title guide](/blog/account-preference-or-title-specific-choice-which-should-win/) separates household policy from observed product behavior.

## Check the end state

At the end, decide whether to leave the selection, restore a verified shared default, or require the next viewer to recheck. Use only current supported controls and avoid claiming a persistence scope that has not been tested.

If the next session resumes the same item, the explicit title choice may be useful. If another person opens a different item, a simple selector check may be safer than broad setting changes.

## Coordinate accessibility respectfully

The household routine for [accessibility preference coordination](/blog/how-to-coordinate-accessibility-preferences-in-a-household/) separates personal, shared-screen, title, and device needs. Store only outcomes and fallbacks, not medical explanations.

Audio-description availability and other alternatives depend on the media and source. Verify relevant episodes before planning a whole season.

## Handle disagreements

Focus on the session outcome rather than taste. If two options meet everyone's needs, rotate or let the group choose. If only one supports participation, access takes priority over habit.

Do not inspect who selected the last track through private activity. Ask directly and keep the discussion about current state.

## Common mistakes and limitations

Avoid one permanent household default, silent changes, assumptions from list position, storing sensitive needs, and treating a profile as a privacy or access-control boundary.

Track availability, labels, and persistence depend on supplied media and current controls. This guide is a household decision method, not a product guarantee.

## Frequently asked questions

### Who chooses when viewers disagree?

Prioritise required access and mutual comprehension, then use an agreed group rule for equally usable options.

### Should the last viewer restore the old track?

Agree the end-state rule in advance. A recheck may be safer when persistence scope is unknown.

### Would separate profiles solve the problem?

They can reduce mixed context, but they do not replace track verification, communication, or shared-screen privacy.
