# How to Report a Mislabeled Audio Track

> **In short:** Capture the exact displayed label, source, item, media version, selected marker, device, and app or browser version. Verify the heard language or role in two short samples, compare one known-good entry if available, and state the expected label without guessing beyond the evidence. Redact credentials, source addresses, private history, and unrelated profiles.

A useful report distinguishes three things: the metadata shown, the behavior heard, and the context that presented both. “Wrong audio” is too broad to route or reproduce.

Write the report soon after the controlled check, while exact labels and steps are still available. If another person owns the source or profile, ask for consent before including their context and let that owner handle any required authentication.

## Confirm that the issue is a label mismatch

A mislabeled track is visible and selectable, but its label conflicts with verified behavior. If the expected entry is absent, use a missing-track diagnostic. If the correct label is selected but output behaves unexpectedly, record a playback or output mismatch instead.

Define the mismatch precisely: wrong language, missing role, incorrect commentary role, incorrect audio-description role, or two indistinguishable labels.

## Preserve the visible metadata

Before correcting anything, record:

- every relevant audio entry in order;
- exact text, spelling, capitalization, and symbols;
- selected/default marker;
- source, item, episode, and media version;
- account and anonymised profile;
- device and app or browser version;
- online or eligible offline state;
- local date and time zone.

A privacy-safe screenshot of the selector can support this record when authorised.

## Verify what is heard

Choose two short, dialogue-rich scenes. Describe the spoken language and role without transcribing substantial dialogue or attaching media.

For production discussion, follow [the commentary identification method](/blog/how-to-identify-a-commentary-track-without-guessing/). For visual narration, use [the audio-description identification method](/blog/how-to-identify-audio-description-when-it-is-available/).

If the result remains ambiguous, report ambiguity rather than a definite mislabel.

## Add one controlled comparison

Compare a clearly labelled track in the same item, the same track on another version, or a neighboring episode—whichever answers the narrow question. Keep device, output, and profile stable where possible.

For same-language entries, use [the duplicate-language comparison](/blog/why-two-audio-tracks-may-share-the-same-language-label/) and avoid calling one redundant.

## Original evidence: report packet

Use this structure:

1. **Displayed label:** exact text.
2. **Selected context:** source, item, version, device.
3. **Verified behavior:** language or role heard in two samples.
4. **Expected distinction:** minimal correction supported by evidence.
5. **Reproduction:** numbered steps from title opening to sample.
6. **Comparison:** one relevant known context.
7. **Privacy review:** secrets and unrelated data removed.

State “expected role label to identify commentary” rather than inventing a specific replacement string when the correct naming convention is unknown.

## Route the report appropriately

Media metadata may originate with the connected source, while a player may decide how supplied fields are presented. The evidence packet helps the source owner and Norva support determine the appropriate route; do not claim which layer caused the issue.

Use official support channels and retain the case reference. Avoid duplicate reports unless instructed.

## Protect private and copyrighted material

Do not send source credentials, addresses, payment data, account email, private history, unrelated profile names, or copies of the audio. A short functional description and selector screenshot are normally more relevant to label identification.

## Verify a correction later

If the label changes, reopen the same item/version, record the new exact text, and sample playback. Close the report only when the visible and heard states align for the tested context. Do not promise that another version or episode is also corrected.

## Common mistakes and limitations

Avoid reporting after one silent scene, confusing preference with metadata, prescribing an unsupported label, and blaming the player or source without evidence.

The report improves reproducibility but cannot guarantee a correction or establish who owns the metadata pipeline.

## Frequently asked questions

### Should I attach an audio clip?

No. Describe the role briefly and follow support instructions; do not distribute media content.

### What if two labels are identical but both tracks work?

Report only when the missing distinction creates a real usability issue, and include the controlled comparison.

### Can I edit the label myself?

Use only verified, authorised controls. Do not alter source data merely to hide a reproducible issue.
