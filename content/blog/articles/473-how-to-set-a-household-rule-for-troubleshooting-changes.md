# How to Set a Household Rule for Troubleshooting Changes

> **In short:** Use one rule: preserve the first state, assign one coordinator, change one variable, record expected and observed results, and keep a rollback path. Personal reversible checks may proceed; source removal, profile deletion, data clearing, reinstall, reset, credential change, or device erase require explicit approval. Stop when identity, authorisation, privacy, or rollback becomes uncertain and contact support.

Shared troubleshooting fails when several people “try things” at once. The rule protects evidence and prevents a small playback issue from becoming an account, source, or profile problem.

Give the incident a short shared label, such as the affected device and date, so household members refer to the same event. Ask everyone to route observations to the coordinator instead of starting a second repair path. That small communication rule prevents duplicate work and preserves a readable sequence of evidence.

## Assign one incident coordinator

The coordinator records actions and prevents parallel changes. They do not gain unrestricted account authority and should include source owners, profile owners, or device custodians when their state is affected.

## Preserve the baseline

Before changes, capture:

- device and app/browser versions;
- account and anonymised profile;
- source, item, and version;
- exact symptom;
- expected and observed results;
- connectivity and output;
- visible error text;
- last successful action.

Remove credentials, private URLs, payment data, and unrelated history.

## Classify the proposed change

Use three levels:

- **observe:** read visible state, no mutation;
- **reversible:** one filter, route, or available track that can be restored;
- **disruptive:** sign-out, source removal, profile deletion, data clearing, reinstall, reset, credential change, or erase.

Disruptive actions need the appropriate owner, rollback, and support rationale.

## Change one variable

Write the action and prediction before performing it. Activate once, wait for an observable result, and record it.

W3C error-identification guidance supports clear descriptions, but it does not diagnose a Norva issue.

## Roll back or establish a new baseline

If the test fails, restore the prior state through verified controls. If rollback cannot be verified, stop. Do not stack another experiment on top of an unknown state.

The [major-change log](/blog/how-to-record-major-library-changes-without-micromanaging/) records the final shared action, not every experiment.

## Set stop conditions

Stop when:

- account or profile identity changes unexpectedly;
- source authorisation is unclear;
- a credential appears;
- another person's private history would be exposed;
- the device reports a hardware or safety warning;
- rollback is unknown;
- repeated tests alter progress;
- the issue needs support access.

Use [the handoff failure report](/blog/how-to-document-a-cross-device-handoff-failure/) as a support model when two devices are involved.

## Rehearse the rule

Use a harmless scenario such as changing and restoring an available subtitle track on a test item. Verify that the household can state baseline, action, result, and rollback without improvisation.

The [shared governance guide](/blog/the-complete-guide-to-shared-household-media-governance/) sets decision roles.

## Common mistakes and limitations

Avoid parallel troubleshooting, undocumented resets, copying secrets into notes, using another person's profile, and continuing after rollback fails.

This rule reduces uncontrolled changes but cannot diagnose every issue or guarantee recovery. Follow current device, source, and Norva support instructions.

## Make the rule usable under pressure

Run a tabletop scenario in which playback fails on one shared device. The incident coordinator should record the baseline, name one proposed change, state its rollback, and stop if credentials, account access, or another household service would be affected.

Ask another member to repeat the rule from the record. If they cannot identify who approves a reset or when to escalate, simplify the wording before a real incident. Keep the exercise free of actual passwords, source endpoints, and destructive actions.

## Frequently asked questions

### Who approves a profile change?

The profile owner and account coordinator should review changes that affect retained state. Household rules cannot create product permissions.

### Should restart be the first step?

Preserve the current state first. A restart may be reasonable later, but it can remove useful evidence.

### How many tests should run before support?

No universal number applies. Stop when the issue is reproducible, risk increases, or rollback becomes uncertain.
