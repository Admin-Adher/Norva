# Audit Trusted Devices After Changing Your Password

> **In short:** Confirm the password changed through the official account flow, update the password-manager entry, and review recovery options. Compare a privacy-safe list of expected screens with any session or device information currently exposed by official controls. Sign out or remove access only through documented actions, inspect shared screens, and keep source credentials separate. If a complete session view is unavailable or unexplained access remains, contact official support.

A password change is an important action, but it should not be treated as proof that every prior session, device, source credential, or billing relationship is resolved. A focused access audit closes those separate questions with evidence.

## Record the trigger and scope

State whether the change followed routine recovery, suspected reuse, phishing, lost device, household change, or confirmed exposure. Record the official action time and broad account identifier in redacted form. Never store the old or new password.

Use the [maintenance handbook](/blog/media-app-maintenance-audit-handbook/) to trigger this audit outside the monthly schedule.

## Verify the account change

Sign in through the official Norva destination on a trusted supported screen. Confirm the password manager holds a unique new value and the account email remains controlled. Review any recovery options actually shown by the current flow.

The [secure account guide](/blog/secure-new-norva-account/) covers password, phishing, and shared-device practices.

## Build the expected-screen list

List broad categories such as personal mobile, household TV, and private browser, with owner role and expected status. Reuse the [monthly relationship register](/blog/monthly-account-device-review/) if it is current. Do not collect serial numbers, exact locations, or advertising identifiers.

Mark lost, sold, repaired, loaned, retired, and unknown screens distinctly.

## Compare official session evidence

Use only session, sign-in, or device information the current official account controls actually expose. Do not claim Norva provides a complete trusted-device dashboard. Record the displayed label, time context, and action options in redacted form.

When no sufficient view is documented, use official support for a material security concern.

## Handle shared and retired screens

On screens still in your control, sign out through current documented controls where appropriate and confirm who owns the remaining session. For a lost or transferred screen, follow device-maker security guidance as well as Norva support. Do not contact a new owner to request screenshots of your account.

Never distribute the new password merely to restore household convenience.

## Keep source secrets separate

The Norva password and connected-source credentials protect different boundaries. If only the Norva password was exposed, do not rotate source credentials blindly. If a source secret may also have been exposed, follow the source provider's official process and the [source credential rotation guide](/blog/rotate-source-credentials-after-exposure/).

Record which boundary was reviewed, not the secret itself.

## Check billing and support boundaries

Confirm the original subscription provider and billing owner. A Norva password change may not alter a store or payment-provider session. Review current provider controls separately and retain only legitimate evidence.

If sign-in fails, build a redacted [account-lockout support packet](/blog/account-lockout-support-packet/) rather than sending codes or passwords.

## Look for continuing signals

Monitor official account messages, unexplained profile or source changes, and session information for a short risk-based period. Do not inspect household viewing history as a proxy for identity. Record observed events with timestamps and preserve original messages safely.

Escalate credible ongoing access immediately rather than waiting for the next review.

## Close each device disposition

Use retained, signed out, retired, lost-device process, unexplained, support escalated, or not visible. Every unexplained result needs an owner and next action. Delete temporary screenshots and secure the final ledger.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Assuming a password change ends every session.
- Inventing a complete device-control feature.
- Rotating unrelated source secrets without evidence.
- Sharing the new password to restore a screen.
- Recording codes, passwords, or serial numbers.
- Waiting to escalate credible ongoing access.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does changing the password automatically sign out every device?

Do not assume so. Review current official session information and support guidance for the actual account flow.

### Should I rotate the connected-source password too?

Only when that secret may be exposed or source policy requires it. Keep the two credential boundaries separate.

### What if I cannot see a device or session list?

Document the gap and use official support for a material concern rather than claiming a control exists.
