# Portrait or Landscape: Choose the Right Orientation

> **In short:** Choose portrait for compact search, lists, and one-handed reading when those tasks fit comfortably. Choose landscape when it gives the actual video, controls, and subtitles more usable room. Keep the orientation that remains stable and accessible on the current device and view.

Orientation is not just a picture-size choice. Rotating a mobile device can move controls, change line lengths, reveal or hide metadata, alter subtitle placement, and interrupt assistive feedback. The useful question is which layout supports the task with the least strain and ambiguity.

## Compare the two layouts by task

| Task or constraint | Portrait may help | Landscape may help |
|---|---|---|
| Search and vertical lists | More rows fit naturally | Wider labels may truncate less |
| One-handed use | Commonly easier on a phone | Often requires two hands or support |
| Wide video | May leave unused areas | Can give the image more width |
| Captions | Shorter lines can scan well | More width may reduce wrapping |
| Privacy in public | Smaller visible footprint | Wider viewing angle may expose more |
| Device support | Often the default | May vary by app or specific view |

This table describes tendencies, not guarantees. Device shape, system settings, application design, and content aspect ratio all affect the result.

## Use portrait for discovery and detail checks

Portrait often suits scrolling through titles, opening filters, reading vertically arranged metadata, or using the device with one hand. It may also reduce how much of a wide screen is visible to people nearby.

However, a narrow layout can truncate long titles, languages, editions, or episode labels. Do not infer hidden text. Open the relevant detail area or use the [small-screen metadata reading guide](/blog/read-metadata-on-small-screen/) to separate essential identity from secondary information.

## Use landscape for supported wide playback

Landscape can align the display more closely with wide video and leave additional horizontal space for a timeline or captions. It can also make touch controls farther apart, which may reduce accidental taps for some people.

The tradeoff is reach. A large phone or tablet may need two hands, a stand, or a stable surface. Avoid balancing a device in a posture that strains the neck, wrist, or grip. More image area is not useful if basic controls become difficult to reach.

## Test captions in the chosen orientation

Subtitle lines can wrap differently after rotation. Controls, notches, home indicators, or other system regions may also change position. Enable the intended available track and observe several lines after rotating. Check that text remains synchronized, readable, and unobstructed.

If a caption style is adjustable at system or app level, test the final combination rather than judging an isolated settings preview. The [small-screen subtitle workflow](/blog/check-subtitles-small-screen/) explains the complete language, timing, and layout check.

## Respect app and system behavior

Apple notes that many apps provide a different view when an iPhone rotates and that orientation can be locked. Android documents an auto-rotate setting, while also warning that rotation can interrupt spoken feedback for TalkBack users. W3C accessibility guidance says content generally should not be restricted to one orientation unless that orientation is essential.

These sources describe platform and accessibility expectations, not a promise that every app view rotates. If the screen remains fixed, verify the system lock and test another supported app before deciding the device is faulty. Do not repeatedly force rotation during active playback.

## Make accessibility part of the decision

If using a screen reader, switch control, magnification, voice control, or another assistive tool, test focus order and spoken labels after one rotation. Confirm that the current item remains identifiable and that focus has not jumped to an unrelated control.

Some people need stable orientation to prevent motion discomfort or loss of context. Others need landscape for larger controls or captions. The [mobile accessibility preflight](/blog/mobile-accessibility-preflight/) treats the person's working setup as the deciding constraint.

## Run a thirty-second orientation test

1. Open the exact title or episode and note the current position.
2. In portrait, show controls and read the most important labels.
3. Rotate once if supported and wait for layout movement to finish.
4. In landscape, repeat the same control and caption checks.
5. Choose the layout with better reach, readability, stability, and privacy.
6. Lock that orientation if unplanned rotation would be disruptive.

If playback pauses, changes position, or loses a track during the test, follow the background-and-return checks instead of treating orientation as the only cause.

## Common mistakes and limits

- Assuming landscape always enlarges useful video content.
- Choosing based on image area while ignoring control reach.
- Rotating while a menu is open and losing the selected context.
- Forgetting that captions can wrap or move.
- Treating a fixed app view as proof that system rotation is broken.
- Repeatedly rotating during screen-reader feedback.
- Ignoring shoulder visibility in a public setting.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is landscape always best for video?

No. It often gives wide video more room, but posture, controls, captions, privacy, content shape, and app support can make portrait the better choice.

### Why does an app not rotate when rotation lock is off?

The app or particular view may not support that orientation. Confirm the system setting and compare with another app documented to rotate.

### Should I lock orientation after choosing?

Lock it when accidental movement would disrupt controls, captions, focus, or comfort. Remember to restore the setting later if other tasks need auto-rotation.
