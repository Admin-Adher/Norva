# Using Rating Filters Without Hiding Great Discoveries

> **In short:** Treat a rating filter as a way to reduce options, not as a definition of quality. Start with a permissive threshold, combine it with the category or context that matters, and periodically remove it to recover unusual or newly added titles. First confirm whether the displayed value is an audience score, editorial score, or age classification.

A number feels decisive, which is exactly why rating filters require care. Scores compress many opinions and contexts into one field. They can make a long list manageable, but a strict threshold can quietly erase niche, divisive, or insufficiently rated choices.

## Identify what the rating represents

“Rating” can refer to different things:

- a numerical or star score;
- an audience or editorial assessment;
- an age or content classification;
- a personal rating you assigned.

These fields answer different questions. A score may help prioritise discovery; an age classification describes suitability guidance. Never treat one as a substitute for the other.

The exact data visible in a media organiser depends on the connected source and its metadata. If a value or scale is unclear, inspect the title details and the source rather than guessing.

## Match the threshold to the decision

There is no universal score that separates worthwhile films from the rest. Choose a threshold according to how much exploration you want:

| Viewing situation | Sensible approach |
| --- | --- |
| You need a short list quickly | Use a moderate threshold after choosing a category |
| You want unfamiliar discoveries | Keep the threshold low or remove it |
| You are revisiting favourites | Start from Favorites instead of a global score |
| You are exploring an era | Filter by year first, then use rating lightly |
| The result set is already small | Do not add a rating filter unless it solves a real problem |

For era-first browsing, see the [year-filter workflow](/blog/use-movie-year-filters/). It is usually easier to understand one broad context before applying a score.

## Run a reversible three-pass exercise

Choose one category containing enough titles to compare. Then:

1. **Unfiltered pass:** scan the first two rows and note the variety.
2. **Permissive pass:** apply a low or moderate threshold and note what disappears.
3. **Strict pass:** raise the threshold and inspect whether the remaining list is genuinely more useful.
4. Return to the unfiltered view.

Record three observations:

- Did the strict list become easier to choose from?
- Did it lose an interesting genre, era, or language option?
- Was the score field present and understandable on most items?

The exercise creates evidence about your own decision process. It does not prove that a particular threshold is broadly correct.

## Preserve discovery routes outside ratings

A rating filter should not become the only way into the library. Keep other paths available:

- [Recent, Favorites, and All Titles](/blog/recent-favorites-all-titles/) for personal context;
- category for mood or subject;
- year for era;
- audio or subtitle filters for language requirements;
- a deliberate sort order for presentation.

Norva can organise the catalogue supplied by a compatible source and preserve favourites and preferences across supported devices. The quality and meaning of source-provided ratings remain dependent on that source.

## Separate filtering from sorting

Filtering removes items from the current result. Sorting keeps them present but changes their order. If you want highly rated entries near the top without excluding the rest, a rating-based sort may be a better fit than a threshold.

The guide to [sorting a personal media library](/blog/sort-personal-media-library/) compares this with alphabetical, date-based, and recency-based ordering.

## When not to use a rating filter

Avoid it when:

- many entries have missing scores;
- the scale or source is unclear;
- you are selecting for age suitability;
- you want to explore overlooked or polarising work;
- a narrow category already gives a manageable set;
- you are using Favorites as a personal recommendation layer.

A missing rating does not mean a poor title. It means the field cannot support that comparison.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Applying a strict score before choosing a viewing context.
- Confusing a numerical score with an age classification.
- Assuming scores from different systems are directly comparable.
- Forgetting that unrated items may disappear.
- Leaving a filter active and later thinking the collection is incomplete.

When a result looks unexpectedly small, remove the rating filter first and verify the active controls one at a time.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the best minimum movie rating?

There is no universal minimum. Use the least restrictive threshold that makes your current result set manageable, and remove it when you want broader discovery.

### Can ratings tell me whether a movie is suitable for a child?

Do not use an audience or editorial score for that purpose. Suitability guidance is a different type of information, and its availability and meaning depend on the source.

### Is sorting by rating better than filtering?

Sorting is preferable when you want high-scoring entries first without excluding everything else. Filtering is useful when a large set must become smaller.
