# How to Find a Title When You Remember Only a Person

> **In short:** Write the person clue as a fingerprint: name or partial name, likely role, approximate period, film or series, co-star or collaborator, character, and one story clue. Search the strongest verified name form first, inspect the person’s returned credits, then add one reliable context clue. Verify both the person identity and the work before selecting a result.

Remembering a face or surname can be enough, but person metadata has its own ambiguity. Two people can share a name, one person can use several credited forms, and series-level credits may differ from individual episodes.

Separate what you remember from what the catalogue can search. A character name may help recognise results even if it is not indexed.

## Choose the first name query

Start with the most distinctive reliably spelled name part. If results are broad, add another verified part. Try documented alternate or credited forms separately rather than combining them.

The Library of Congress person-name search guidance notes that name order and phrase behaviour depend on the search type. That reinforces a practical rule: observe the actual interface and avoid assuming that “family, given” and “given family” behave identically.

## Inspect person identity before credits

Check:

- full displayed and alternate names;
- stable person identifier where available;
- known role or field;
- script and diacritics;
- associated titles and period;
- whether two same-name records appear.

Use [the credit-consistency audit](/blog/check-person-credit-consistency/) if one person appears split or several people appear merged. Do not select a title merely because a familiar name is present.

## Narrow through one intersection

Apply one reliable clue at a time:

- person + approximate year;
- person + film or series type;
- person + another person;
- person + remembered title fragment;
- person + role;
- person + series or episode context.

Where the interface does not support compound fields, search the person, then filter or scan the resulting credit set manually. Record which clue reduced the candidate set.

## Verify credit scope

A person may be credited at:

- whole-series level;
- one season;
- selected episodes;
- a specific film version;
- an archive extra or commentary;
- an uncredited or uncertain source note.

Open the candidate details and compare the credit with the remembered role. If a global series card lists a creator, do not assume that person appears in every episode.

## Confirm the title independently

Use at least two work clues beyond the person:

- year or decade;
- synopsis premise;
- another cast or creator;
- runtime or episode position;
- artwork, treated cautiously;
- original or localised title;
- version or source.

When several works share the same name, use [the similar-title result method](/blog/search-through-similar-title-results/). If the person returns nothing, run [the zero-results diagnostic](/blog/diagnose-zero-search-results/) and try a known title from the same source.

## Record a missing-credit hypothesis

If the title is found through another route but person search misses it, capture the work ID, person name form, expected role and scope, source, interface, and date. Confirm current searchable fields before calling it a metadata defect.

Norva can organise metadata from compatible authorised sources, but available person credits, alternate names, and searchable fields depend on source data and current product behaviour.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Searching a character name as though it were the actor.
- Combining uncertain first and family names.
- Ignoring stage names or diacritics.
- Assuming series credit means episode participation.
- Choosing the first recognisable poster.
- Treating a missing search result as proof the credit is absent.

Person records can be incomplete, and some contributions are disputed or unavailable. Preserve provenance and uncertainty.

## Frequently asked questions

### Should I search the surname alone?

Yes when it is distinctive and reliably spelled. Add a given name, role, or period only if results remain too broad.

### What if I remember only a character?

Search the character where supported, or use it as a recognition clue while searching the series, franchise, story word, or likely actor.

### Why does a person appear twice?

The catalogue may contain alternate forms or duplicate identities from different sources. Compare identifiers and credits before assuming they should merge.
