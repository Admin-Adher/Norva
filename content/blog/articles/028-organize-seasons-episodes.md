# How to Keep Seasons and Episodes Easy to Navigate

> **In short:** Preserve a predictable three-level path—series, season, episode—and let clear metadata do most of the work. Check numbering, titles, specials, progress, and duplicates on a representative sample. Fix the source metadata when possible instead of building manual categories that duplicate the season structure.

Episodic libraries become difficult when hierarchy and metadata disagree. A viewer expects to choose a show, select a season, and understand which episode comes next. Every exception added to that path should have a clear reason.

## Protect the series-season-episode hierarchy

Treat the series page as the stable home for all episodes of one show. Seasons should divide that set into understandable groups, while episode rows or cards should answer four questions quickly:

- Which episode is this?
- What is its title?
- Have I started or completed it?
- What comes before and after it?

Avoid reproducing the same hierarchy through manual categories such as “Season 1”, “Season 2”, and “Season 3” at the global library level. Those labels lose context when several shows share them.

## Check metadata before changing the interface

Episode order depends on the information supplied by the connected source. Start with [how media metadata works](/blog/media-metadata-explained/): inconsistent show names, season numbers, episode numbers, or identifiers can split one series or combine unrelated entries.

Use the source's authorised management tools when a correction belongs there. A media organiser can present the information it receives, but it should not be expected to infer every ambiguous numbering scheme.

Review these fields together:

| Field | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Series name | Keeps episodes under the same parent |
| Season number or label | Defines the intermediate group |
| Episode number | Establishes expected order |
| Episode title | Makes adjacent entries distinguishable |
| Availability | Prevents a listed episode from being mistaken for a playable one |

## Handle specials without breaking the main sequence

Specials can be difficult because their intended viewing point may not match a simple numbered season. Keep the source's established grouping unless you have reliable information supporting a different order.

Do not silently renumber a special as a regular episode merely to make the list look tidy. A visually neat but inaccurate sequence creates a worse experience than an explicitly labelled exception.

If a series has several editions or duplicated episode entries, use the [duplicate-title diagnostic](/blog/diagnose-duplicate-media-titles/) before merging anything. Similar text does not prove that two episode records represent the same item.

## Make progress useful, not dominant

Playback progress is a personal state layered over the episode hierarchy. It can help identify the next action without changing the canonical episode order.

A useful series view distinguishes:

- not started;
- in progress;
- completed;
- unavailable through the current source.

Continue Watching should act as a shortcut back into the hierarchy, not become a separate archive of every partially opened episode. The guide to [keeping Continue Watching useful](/blog/manage-continue-watching/) offers a maintenance method.

## Run the six-series navigation audit

Choose six representative shows:

1. a single-season series;
2. a long-running series;
3. a series with specials;
4. a series with similar episode titles;
5. a series you are currently watching;
6. a series containing an unavailable or ambiguous entry.

For each one, record:

- whether all episodes appear under one correct series;
- whether seasons are ordered predictably;
- whether episode number and title are both readable;
- whether a special is clearly distinguished;
- whether progress points to the expected episode;
- how many remote-control or pointer actions are needed to resume.

The audit does not create evidence about Norva performance. It is a repeatable way to reveal information-architecture problems in your own connected catalogue.

## Avoid these organisation traps

- **Sorting all episodes by title.** Alphabetical order can hide narrative sequence.
- **Creating one global category per season.** It removes the series context.
- **Merging episodes because artwork matches.** Artwork is not a reliable identity key.
- **Treating unavailable as unwatched.** These are different states.
- **Correcting only the display label.** The underlying metadata may continue to split or duplicate the series.

After any structural change, revisit a recent episode and an old season. A fix that helps the current season may unintentionally make archives harder to browse.

## Frequently asked questions

### Should specials appear before or after regular seasons?

There is no universal answer. Preserve the ordering and labels provided by a reliable source unless you have authoritative information for that series. Make the exception visible rather than forcing it into a regular sequence.

### Why do episodes from one show appear as separate series?

Inconsistent series names or identifiers in the connected source are common possibilities. Compare the relevant metadata before changing categories or merging records.

### Should completed episodes disappear?

Usually they should remain available in the series history while progress provides a shortcut to what comes next. Hiding completed episodes entirely can make revisiting or checking continuity harder.
