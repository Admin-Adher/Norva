# How to Protect Your Account on a Shared Browser

> **In short:** Sign in only on a device you trust enough for the task. Use a separate guest, private, or operating-system account according to official browser guidance; refuse saved credentials; hide personal notifications and tabs; use the service's verified sign-out; close every related private or guest window; and confirm the account no longer opens.

A shared browser creates two risks: another person may see the session while it is active, and local account state may remain after you leave. Private browsing reduces some local persistence but does not make the session invisible to websites, networks, administrators, or downloaded files.

## Decide whether the device is trustworthy enough

Avoid signing in on a device that appears tampered with, is unexpectedly managed, runs unknown remote-control software, or belongs to an untrusted public setting. A private window cannot protect against a compromised operating system or someone watching the screen.

When possible, use your own device. If temporary access is necessary, limit the session to the minimum task and avoid viewing highly sensitive account details.

## Choose a separate local context

Use the browser vendor's documented option that fits the device. Google explains [Chrome Guest mode](https://support.google.com/chrome/answer/6130773) for shared or borrowed computers and separately documents Incognito. Apple documents [Private Browsing in Safari](https://support.apple.com/guide/safari/ibrw1069/mac).

Read the limits. Chrome states that Incognito does not make activity invisible to sites, organizations managing the network, or internet providers. Downloads and bookmarks can remain after the session. Other browsers have their own behavior.

## Avoid saving credentials and personal data

Decline prompts to save passwords, addresses, payment information, or profile changes. Do not synchronize your personal browser profile to a borrowed device. Avoid copying secrets to a shared clipboard or leaving account recovery details visible.

Use the service's normal secure sign-in flow. Never send credentials through support messages or screenshots.

## Control what the screen reveals

Before playback, close unrelated personal tabs and disable notification previews through normal system controls. Check browser profile name, bookmarks bar, recent-address suggestions, and desktop items that can appear when leaving fullscreen.

The [tab management guide](/blog/manage-tabs-during-viewing/) helps maintain one playback owner without exposing unrelated pages.

## Keep downloads and exports in mind

Private or guest modes may still allow files to be downloaded to the device. Avoid downloads unless required and authorized. If you create one, locate and remove the file according to device policy, then empty temporary locations only when you have permission.

Do not clear another person's entire download history or storage. Clean only the material created by your session and follow organizational rules.

## Sign out explicitly

Use the service's verified sign-out control. Closing a tab is not proof that the server-side session ended. After sign-out, close all related tabs and every private or guest window; some browsers treat multiple private windows as one session.

Follow [the clean sign-out routine](/blog/end-browser-session-cleanly/) for a structured close.

## Verify closure from a fresh context

Reopen the service from a fresh guest or private context and confirm it does not expose the account. Do not enter the account again merely to test. Check that no related window remains open and no download contains private information.

If the service provides a verified account-session management page, use it according to official support documentation. Do not assume or describe such a feature without confirming it exists.

## Troubleshoot narrowly

If sign-out or login state behaves unexpectedly, preserve the exact page and message. Do not immediately clear all browser data, which can affect other users. Use a site-specific investigation and the [browser site-data guide](/blog/troubleshoot-browser-site-data/) only with permission.

## Use a shared-browser checklist

Record device owner, trust decision, local context, saved-password prompt declined, private tabs closed, downloads reviewed, sign-out completed, fresh-context verification, and any unresolved warning. Never put the actual password, token, or personal source name in the checklist.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Assuming private browsing makes activity anonymous.
- Signing in on a device that is not trustworthy.
- Adding a personal browser profile to a borrowed computer.
- Saving credentials for convenience.
- Forgetting downloads survive some private sessions.
- Closing one tab instead of signing out and closing all windows.
- Clearing another user's data without permission.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is a private window enough on a public computer?

No. It limits some local browser persistence but cannot protect against an untrusted device, network visibility, shoulder surfing, or files you download.

### Is Guest mode better than Incognito?

They have different browser-specific purposes and limits. Follow the vendor's current documentation and choose the smallest local context that fits the shared-device situation.

### How do I know the session ended?

Use verified sign-out, close every related private or guest window, and confirm a fresh context does not open the account.
