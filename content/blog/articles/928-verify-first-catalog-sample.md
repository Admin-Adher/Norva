# Verify a Small Sample of Your First Norva Catalog

> **In short:** Select three to five recognizable, non-sensitive items before inspecting your first Norva catalog. Include different media structures, record what the authorized source actually provides, then compare type, title, hierarchy, variants, metadata, and filter visibility. A small planned sample produces clearer evidence than scrolling randomly through the entire catalog.

Catalog verification asks whether representative source information is organized usefully. It is not a census and it does not establish rights. Complete the [first import observation](/blog/observe-first-catalog-import/) before beginning so a partial import is not mistaken for a final catalog.

## Choose the sample before viewing results

Write down three to five items you know through the authorized source. A practical sample may include one straightforward movie, one series with several episodes, one title with multiple versions if the source provides them, and one item with expected language information.

Avoid choosing only unusual cases. Also avoid sensitive titles if the record might later be shared. The objective is diagnostic coverage with minimal disclosure.

## Establish the source baseline

Check each sample item through the source's own authorized administration or viewing workflow. Record only the fields needed for comparison: broad media type, displayed title, season and episode structure, known variants, and expected audio or subtitle labels.

Norva does not supply the media catalog. It organizes information from a compatible source the user owns or is legally authorized to use. Missing or inconsistent source data can limit the resulting presentation.

## Check media type and identity

Find each sample in Norva under a fixed profile and filter state. Confirm that a movie is presented as a movie and a series as a series. Compare the visible title and year only where the source provides them.

Do not classify a different artwork choice as an identity failure by itself. Use several signals, such as title, year, episode context, and description, while minimizing what you copy into evidence.

## Check hierarchy and sequence

For the series sample, inspect season grouping and a few episode numbers. Confirm that the sequence is plausible relative to the source baseline. Use the dedicated [series structure onboarding check](/blog/verify-first-series-structure/) when the hierarchy is complex or ambiguous.

Record exactly what differs. "Episode 3 appears before Episode 2" is more useful than "series layout wrong."

## Check variants without assuming merges

If the source exposes multiple versions, observe whether Norva groups or separates them under the current settings. Grouping can depend on source-derived identity information. Similar titles alone do not guarantee that two records represent the same work.

Never delete a source record merely to make the interface look tidy. First determine whether the records have materially different quality, language, edition, or metadata.

## Check language and subtitle labels

Open the relevant details or playback controls and compare available audio and subtitle choices with the source baseline. Norva may keep language preferences where supported, but actual availability depends on the source, media, device, and rights.

A label difference is not automatically a missing track. Record the exact sanitized label and whether the track can be selected during a controlled playback test.

## Check filters from a neutral state

Reset catalog filters before concluding that an item is absent. Then apply one filter at a time and record the change. Grouping, availability, source, audio, subtitle, year, or rating filters may intentionally hide a result.

The [complete onboarding journey](/blog/norva-onboarding-complete-journey/) provides the broader sequence for moving from catalog checks to playback and synchronization.

## Common verification mistakes

- Sampling only one item or only unusual items.
- Inspecting before the first import reaches a stable state.
- Leaving an active filter in place while searching.
- Assuming title similarity requires variant grouping.
- Treating source-dependent language availability as guaranteed.
- Sharing a full household catalog when a few redacted rows suffice.

## Frequently asked questions

### How large should the first sample be?

Three to five well-chosen items are often enough to cover basic structures. Expand only when the first sample reveals a specific pattern that needs testing.

### Does a catalog mismatch always come from Norva?

No. It may originate in source data, current filters, grouping rules, device presentation, or the application. Preserve the source baseline and fixed test conditions before assigning a cause.

### Should I test playback during the catalog sample?

Use playback only to resolve a specific catalog question, such as a selectable language track. Run the separate first-playback verification for controls, continuity, and progress behavior.
