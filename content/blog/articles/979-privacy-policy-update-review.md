# How to Review a Media App Privacy Policy Update

> **In short:** Verify the notice and effective date on the official site, preserve references to the old and new versions, and compare scope, data categories, purposes, sharing, retention, rights, security language, contact routes, and account choices. Record operational impact and unanswered questions separately. This process supports an informed decision but is not legal advice.

A privacy-policy update can range from a clearer sentence to a material change in data use. Reading only the highlighted summary may miss a new definition, exception, or cross-reference. A structured review helps the household focus on what changed and what that change means for its actual use.

The [maintenance and audit handbook](/blog/media-app-maintenance-audit-handbook/) can assign an owner and review deadline.

## Verify the notice

Open the policy from the service's official domain rather than an unexpected message link. Record the displayed revision or effective date, the notice date, and the URL. Confirm that the notice applies to the product and region in use.

Do not sign in through a link merely to inspect a public policy. If authentication is required, navigate independently through the official site.

## Preserve references, not copies of personal data

Save the official URLs and dates. If an earlier version is legitimately available, retain a protected copy or archive reference for comparison. Do not attach account exports, screenshots of viewing history, or source configuration to the policy review.

Quote only short passages when exact wording is essential. A paraphrased change summary is usually easier to compare and reduces copying.

## Compare definitions and scope

Start with who the policy covers, which products and regions it applies to, and how key terms are defined. A changed definition of account, device, usage data, service provider, or household can alter the meaning of later sections.

Record additions, removals, clarifications, and moved text separately. Relocation is not automatically a substantive change.

## Compare data and purposes

List changed data categories and stated purposes. For a media app, relevant categories may include account, profile, device, source configuration, viewing context, diagnostics, support, payment-related, and conditional offline information.

Do not infer collection merely from a generic example. Record the wording and the household workflow it may affect, then flag uncertainty for clarification.

## Compare sharing and service relationships

Review recipients, processors or service providers, legal disclosures, corporate changes, and international transfer language. Note whether a category or purpose is newly linked to a recipient.

Avoid reducing the analysis to "shared" or "not shared." Purpose, category, recipient, condition, and control all matter.

## Compare retention, rights, and controls

Check retention criteria, deletion or access routes, objection or preference controls, complaint contacts, and account closure consequences. Record whether the procedure, eligibility, or response description changed.

Use the [first Norva privacy review](/blog/first-norva-privacy-review/) to map the updated disclosure to actual household data surfaces.

## Review security language carefully

Distinguish encryption in transit, encrypted local storage, organizational safeguards, and user responsibilities. General security language is not a guarantee that every data category uses the same control or that no incident can occur.

Do not convert a policy statement into a technical claim broader than its wording and scope.

## Decide the operational response

Possible actions include no change, adjust a profile or device setting, remove obsolete sessions, delete unnecessary support evidence, stop a conditional offline workflow, ask official support a focused question, or seek qualified legal advice.

The [privacy-disclosure evaluation](/blog/evaluate-norva-privacy-disclosures/) helps decide whether current terms remain acceptable before subscribing or continuing.

## Common review mistakes

- Trusting a policy link in an unsolicited message.
- Reading only the update summary.
- Ignoring changed definitions.
- Treating moved text as automatically new.
- Converting broad security language into a guarantee.
- Copying personal account data into the review.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is every wording change material?

No. Some changes improve clarity or structure. Compare scope, obligations, purposes, recipients, retention, rights, and choices before classifying impact.

### Does this checklist provide legal advice?

No. It is an operational reading method. Consult qualified counsel for legal interpretation or a rights dispute.

### What if the previous policy is unavailable?

Record that limitation, review the current policy in full, use any official change notice, and ask the verified contact route a focused question instead of guessing.
