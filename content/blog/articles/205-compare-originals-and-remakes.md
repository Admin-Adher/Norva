# A Library Workflow for Comparing Originals and Remakes

> **In short:** Create separate work records for the original and remake, link them with an explicit remake or adaptation relationship, and keep each movie's versions beneath its own identity. Verify the exact cuts you will watch, choose matched comparison questions, view close enough together for evidence to remain usable, and record observations without treating the later movie as merely another edition.

An original–remake project fails when the library collapses two works into one title group or compares different cuts without noticing. Identity and version control must come before interpretation.

## Confirm the relationship

Verify that the later movie is actually a remake, not a sequel, reissue, restoration, alternate cut, or separate adaptation from a shared source. Record the relationship and the evidence used.

The Library of Congress moving-image cataloguing guidance states that remakes are new works related to earlier works, not versions. It separately describes versions as works whose content has been modified through additions, deletions, or both.

Use [the alternate-cut workflow](/blog/choose-between-alternate-cuts/) when the relationship is version-to-work rather than remake-to-original.

Dublin Core relation properties such as `isVersionOf`, `hasVersion`, and broader `relation` illustrate why the relationship should be typed. Do not use a version relationship when the works are distinct.

## Choose the exact viewing versions

For each movie, verify:

- cut or edition label;
- runtime;
- current availability;
- audio and subtitle tracks;
- restoration or presentation notes relevant to comparison;
- whether a grouped record contains other versions.

If only one movie has an extended cut, decide whether that asymmetry serves the project. Record it rather than pretending both viewings are equivalent.

## Define matched comparison questions

Select three to five questions before watching:

- What narrative elements are retained, removed, or added?
- How does setting alter the conflict?
- Which character functions change?
- How do pacing and runtime redistribute emphasis?
- How does form, tone, or genre framing differ?
- Which elements come from a shared source rather than the earlier movie?

Do not assume every similarity proves direct borrowing. Record what is observed and keep source-history claims separate unless verified.

## Pick a viewing order

Original first supports historical sequence. Remake first can reveal how a later version stands alone before comparison. Alternating scenes is usually impractical and can overemphasize surface equivalence.

Choose one order and state why. For a larger period project, [the decade retrospective guide](/blog/build-decade-movie-retrospective/) can situate each work without making the pair represent its entire era.

Write observations before assigning scores. A personal rating answers whether the viewer valued the movie; it does not explain the relationship. [The post-viewing rating workflow](/blog/use-personal-ratings-after-viewing/) keeps those tasks separate.

## Preserve the comparison without clutter

Save the relationship, selected versions, comparison questions, viewing dates, and a short conclusion. A temporary project collection can be archived after completion while the work-to-work link remains.

Norva may group versions and organize compatible sources a user is authorized to access, but relationship metadata depends on connected-source data and user verification. Do not infer a remake solely from a similar title or artwork.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Grouping a remake as an alternate cut.
- Comparing two unknown or mismatched versions.
- Assuming shared source material means one remakes the other.
- Writing conclusions before using matched questions.
- Letting a rating replace evidence.
- Presenting a two-film comparison as complete production history.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is every later adaptation a remake?

No. It may independently adapt shared source material. Verify the relationship with reliable evidence.

### Should the original always be watched first?

No. Choose the order that supports the question and document it.

### Can both movies share one library record?

They should retain distinct work identities while being connected through a typed relationship.
