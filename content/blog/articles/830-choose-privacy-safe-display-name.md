# Choose a Privacy-Safe Display Name for Your Account

> **In short:** Choose a short label that the intended household recognizes without revealing a full legal name, email address, birth date, street, workplace, school, phone number, account role, source host, subscription identifier, or security clue. Assume it may appear on a shared television, screenshot, notification, log, or support exchange. Test recognition against exposure, follow current character rules, and remember that a display name is not a password or verified identity.

A display name is designed for recognition. It can still become personal data when linked to an account, and visibility can extend beyond the person who selected it.

## Map where the name can appear

Check account menus, profile pickers, shared televisions, device lists, notifications, screenshots, support attachments, and exported settings. Do not assume an account-level name stays private merely because it was entered during sign-up.

The [personal-data versus media-data guide](/blog/personal-data-vs-media-data/) explains why context and linkage matter.

## Remove direct identifiers

Avoid full legal names, complete email addresses, phone numbers, birth dates, membership numbers, and street addresses. A first name or initials may be acceptable in one household and too revealing in another; assess who can see the screen.

Do not use a display name to store contact information. Account recovery belongs in protected account settings.

## Avoid security and source clues

Never include password hints, recovery phrases, one-time codes, provider transaction references, source usernames, source addresses, server locations, or subscription status. A label such as "Admin" can also reveal household authority and invite unnecessary attention.

The [privacy-safe source naming guide](/blog/privacy-safe-source-display-name/) handles source labels separately, while the [device-label guide](/blog/privacy-safe-device-labels/) covers televisions and phones.

## Use household-specific but neutral recognition

Consider a neutral nickname, initials that are not globally identifying, a color, or an agreed role that reveals little outside the household. Avoid sensitive characteristics such as medical condition, religion, relationship conflict, or a child's school.

Examples should remain fictional. "Blue Profile" is safer as a teaching example than a real person's exact name and birth year.

## Test from a guest's perspective

Imagine a visitor, repair technician, hotel guest, screenshot recipient, or support agent seeing the label. Can they infer a person's full identity, location, work schedule, child status, or security role? If yes, simplify it.

Also test collisions. Two identical labels can cause the wrong profile or settings to be selected. Privacy and usability need a balanced result.

## Check current product constraints

Verify whether Norva currently permits account display-name changes, which characters and length are accepted, and whether account and profile names are separate. Do not invent editing controls or synchronization behavior.

Norva's current privacy notice lists display name as account information. Product claims in this draft remain unverified pending human review.

## Change carefully on shared devices

Before renaming, note the current label and tell household members. After saving, verify the correct account and profiles on supported devices. A rename should not be treated as an account transfer or security reset.

If a name appeared in an old screenshot or support ticket, changing it does not erase those copies automatically.

Recheck the label after each major household or device change.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Assuming account labels never leave settings.
- Using a complete email address for convenience.
- Adding a birth year or street name.
- Embedding a source address or billing reference.
- Using password hints in a label.
- Choosing identical names that cause selection errors.
- Assuming a rename deletes historic screenshots or logs.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is a display name a verified identity?

No. It is generally a user-facing label and should not be treated as proof of legal identity or account ownership.

### Can I use my first name?

Possibly, if the household context and screen visibility make the exposure acceptable; a neutral nickname may reveal less.

### Does changing the label secure the account?

No. Use unique passwords, stronger authentication where supported, recovery controls, and device review for account security.
