# How to Run a Caption Review Without Speaking for Users

> **In short:** Invite caption users as participants, obtain informed consent, ask them to define the viewing outcomes that matter, and observe real tasks. Let participants control personal disclosure and correct notes. Report “this participant encountered…” before claiming a broader pattern, separate preferences from barriers, and combine user evidence with standards and technical checks.

Accessibility review fails when evaluators imagine user needs without involving people or expect one participant to represent everyone. Participation improves evidence but does not remove the need for standards review.

## Define the decision

State what the review will influence: track discovery, caption content, timing, size, contrast, placement, controls, responsive behavior, or support workflow. A focused decision supports better tasks and avoids collecting irrelevant personal information.

Do not frame the participant as a final approval gate for the entire product.

## Recruit for relevant experience

Seek participants who use captions in the contexts being tested. Include different devices, languages, and access strategies when those differences matter. One participant can reveal a serious barrier but cannot establish population prevalence.

Do not require a medical diagnosis as proof of caption use.

## Make participation accessible

Ask about communication, scheduling, captioning, interpreter, device, breaks, and remote-session needs. Share tasks in advance when helpful. Compensate and handle consent according to the organisation's process.

Avoid surprise changes that make participation itself inaccessible.

## Use real tasks

Ask participants to:

- find and select a caption track;
- identify speakers and sound cues;
- read dense dialogue;
- adjust supported size or background;
- continue after resume or device change;
- recover from an ambiguous label.

Observe outcomes and invite commentary without leading toward an expected answer.

## Original evidence: participant-led record

| Field | Record |
|---|---|
| Participant-defined outcome | Their wording, with consent |
| Task and context | Device, track, scene type |
| Observed barrier | Factual behavior |
| Participant impact | Their description |
| Workaround | Used/not used and cost |
| Evidence | Timestamp/screenshot reference |
| Scope | Individual, repeated, or untested |

Let participants review sensitive quotes or remove them.

## Separate observation, interpretation, and recommendation

Observation: focus disappeared after closing the selector. Interpretation: the participant could not locate the next action. Recommendation: restore visible focus to a predictable control.

Keeping these layers separate makes evidence easier to challenge and correct.

## Respect multilingual review

For language-specific caption meaning, involve fluent participants and avoid asking one person to represent every regional variety. Use [the multilingual caption guide](/blog/how-to-assess-multilingual-caption-accessibility/) for script, direction, and translation review boundaries.

## Combine user and standards evidence

The [complete caption accessibility guide](/blog/the-complete-guide-to-caption-accessibility/) provides coverage, timing, readability, and control checks. User evidence explains impact and real strategies; standards define requirements and shared expectations.

Use [the caption accessibility checklist](/blog/a-caption-accessibility-checklist/) to ensure no major dimension is skipped.

## Report uncertainty honestly

Distinguish one participant's preference, one reproducible barrier, repeated findings, and untested contexts. Do not invent percentages or say “users want” when only one person commented.

Remove diagnoses, contact details, private title history, account data, and unnecessary recordings from shared reports.

## Close the loop

Tell participants how findings will be used, invite corrections, and retest material changes with relevant users where possible. Do not claim a fix is accessible based only on developer confirmation.

## Common mistakes and limitations

Avoid speaking for users, recruiting one “representative” person, leading questions, inaccessible sessions, unpaid emotional labour assumptions, and publishing sensitive quotes without consent.

User participation complements rather than replaces automated, code, standards, and product-support review.

## Preserve disagreement as evidence

Two viewers can report different barriers in the same scene. Keep their observations separate, including context and preferred presentation, instead of voting one away or converting them into a single persona. Distinguish what the reviewer observed, what a participant said, and what the team inferred. Before sharing notes, remove names and unrelated account or viewing details, and confirm that any quotation is authorised and accurately represented.

## Frequently asked questions

### Can one participant reveal a valid issue?

Yes. A reproducible barrier can be valid, but one participant does not establish how common it is.

### Should reviewers ask for diagnoses?

Usually the task and access strategy are enough. Collect only information necessary and consented to.

### Can internal staff represent caption users?

Staff with relevant lived experience can contribute, but participation should be voluntary and should not substitute automatically for broader user involvement.
