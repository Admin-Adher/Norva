# An Audio Quality Literacy Checklist

> **In short:** Name the audible symptom and exact excerpt, then trace source, mix, encode, delivery, decoding, route, output, room, safe level, and listener context. Record verified codec, bitrate context, sample format, channels, loudness, and dynamics without treating one value as quality. Change one layer, rest, repeat, preserve unknowns, and restore settings.

Use this checklist to route a question, not certify every source or device. [The complete audio-quality guide](/blog/the-complete-guide-to-understanding-audio-quality/) explains each layer.

## 1. Define the task and symptom

- Name the goal: clearer dialogue, stable sync, correct route, artifact diagnosis, or version choice.
- Record track, scene, and exact timecode.
- Describe warble, pre-echo, clipping, dropout, hiss, masking, delay, missing channel, tonal change, or another observation.
- Separate observation, preference, and hypothesis.

## 2. Verify source and mix

- Record version, language, alternate mix, stereo or multichannel purpose, and source lineage when known.
- Mark differences in mastering, dialogue balance, dynamics, or edit.
- Never treat a label as proof of current selection.
- Use only sources the listener owns or is authorised to use.

## 3. Verify encode and sample metadata

- Record codec and configuration, bitrate definition, channels, sample rate, and bit depth where exposed.
- Keep codec, bitrate, sample rate, and bit depth as separate properties.
- Mark encoder and hidden values unknown.
- Do not rank audio from numbers alone.

## 4. Check loudness, peaks, and dynamics

- Record valid programme-loudness and true-peak evidence and scope when available.
- Separate user volume, normalisation, dynamic-range control, limiting, and dialogue processing.
- Match comparisons at a safe level.
- Stop with discomfort, fatigue, or unexpected peaks.

## 5. Check delivery and decoding

- Record local or network context, track switches, errors, dropouts, and fallback.
- Verify device, operating system, app or browser, and capability diagnostics.
- Do not infer hardware decode, codec, or bitrate from sound alone.
- Keep wireless latency separate from compression artifacts.

## 6. Verify route and channel mapping

- Trace player, system output, connection, receiver, and physical speakers or headphones.
- Record passthrough, downmix, virtualisation, and listening mode.
- Use quiet known cues; never loud identification signals.
- Confirm route after pause, seek, reconnect, and return.

## 7. Check output and environment

- Record speaker placement, blocked grilles, headphone fit, channel orientation, room noise, reflections, seat, and distance.
- Lower level before switching outputs.
- Do not compare identical slider numbers.
- Keep comfort and preference separate from fidelity observations.

## 8. Choose excerpts

- Include dialogue, transients, ambience, sustained tones, dense music, silence, spatial cues, bass, and dynamic change as relevant.
- Use exact timecodes and short trials.
- Observe more than one excerpt.
- Rest between repeats.

## 9. Compare responsibly

- Match source scope, route, processing, output, room, and safe programme loudness.
- Conceal labels and reverse order where practical.
- Record observations before preference.
- Repeat only planned differences.
- Use [the responsible audio comparison guide](/blog/how-to-compare-two-audio-versions-responsibly/).

## 10. Use a protocol

- Pre-register question, versions, excerpts, level method, route, order, rests, and stopping rule.
- Preserve individual notes and disagreement.
- State why formal ITU methods were or were not used.
- Follow [the repeatable listening protocol](/blog/build-a-repeatable-audio-quality-listening-protocol/).

## 11. Protect rights, privacy, and safety

- Remove source addresses, credentials, tokens, account data, device addresses, and history.
- Do not share copyrighted excerpts without permission.
- Avoid unsafe volume and stop suspected hardware overload.
- Do not diagnose hearing conditions or prescribe medical solutions.

## 12. Report and restore

- Include exact context, steps, expected and observed result, impact, one-layer comparison, unknowns, and limitations.
- Restore track, route, balance, processing, and volume.
- Verify current Norva controls through official information.
- Remember that Norva organises and plays compatible authorised sources; it does not supply a catalogue or certify source mixes.

## Common mistakes and limitations

Avoid unmatched loudness, visible labels, unlimited repetition, multiple simultaneous changes, or universal codec and output claims. A household check is not calibrated laboratory assessment.

## Frequently asked questions

### Which number best represents audio quality?

No single number represents the full source-to-listener chain.

### Should volume be raised to hear artifacts?

No. Use a safe, comfortable, matched level and stop with fatigue or discomfort.

### Can this checklist prove formal compliance?

No. Formal evaluation needs an applicable standard, controlled method, qualified scope, and documented analysis.
