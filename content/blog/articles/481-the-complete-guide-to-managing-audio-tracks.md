# The Complete Guide to Managing Audio Tracks

> **In short:** Inspect the audio list for the selected title and version, read every visible label, choose by language and role, then verify with a short playback sample. Recheck after an episode, version, device, resume, or offline-context change. When something differs, record the exact source, item, version, track label, device, and result before changing another variable.

Audio management is more than picking a language. A list may distinguish standard dialogue, dubbing, commentary, audio description, channel layouts, or versions—but only when the source metadata and current interface expose those distinctions.

## Know where track information comes from

Norva describes itself as a player and organiser for compatible sources users connect. It does not create the source's media or guarantee that every file contains the same alternatives. The visible track list can be affected by:

- the connected source;
- the selected item and media version;
- the source metadata attached to each track;
- the app, browser, device, and output path;
- whether playback is online or using an eligible local copy;
- the current profile, account, or title-level selection behavior.

Treat this as a diagnostic map, not a claim that every factor always changes the list.

## Read the list before playback

Open the audio selector and record all visible details. A language label is useful, but it may not identify purpose. Two entries can share a language while serving different roles or representing different encodes.

Use [the audio-track list literacy guide](/blog/how-to-read-an-audio-track-list-before-playback/) to separate what the interface confirms from what still requires a sample. Never infer “commentary,” “dub,” or “audio description” from position alone.

## Choose deliberately

Decide what outcome matters for this session:

- original spoken performance, when clearly identified;
- a dubbed track in a preferred language;
- audio description, when available and verified;
- commentary for an intentional secondary viewing;
- a track compatible with the listener's device and output needs.

If the labels are ambiguous, sample safely rather than guessing. A deliberate choice can vary by title, viewer, room, or day; there is no universally superior track.

## Verify the selection

After choosing, play a short section with clear dialogue. Confirm:

1. the spoken language matches the selection;
2. narration or commentary is present only when intended;
3. dialogue is audible through the current output;
4. subtitles, if used, complement rather than contradict the chosen audio;
5. the selector still indicates the expected track when reopened.

Do not use a single opening logo or silent scene as proof. Verification should remain brief and should not be presented as a technical quality benchmark.

## Understand preference scope

An account or profile preference may express a useful default, while a title-specific choice handles an exception. The exact precedence and persistence must be verified in the current product context. Do not promise that one choice will override another everywhere.

If the selection changes between episodes, after resume, or on another device, compare the contexts before treating it as a defect. The articles on [audio after resume](/blog/how-to-recheck-audio-after-resuming-playback/) and [pre-handoff audio verification](/blog/how-to-verify-audio-before-moving-to-another-device/) provide focused routines.

## Diagnose with one-variable comparisons

When a track is missing or unexpected, preserve the baseline:

- account and anonymised profile;
- source and item identifier;
- season and episode where relevant;
- selected media version;
- full visible track labels;
- device and app or browser version;
- online or eligible offline context;
- expected and observed result.

Then change one variable: episode, version, device, or playback context. Avoid source removal, data clearing, reinstall, resets, or credential changes unless an authorised owner and official support path require them.

## Original evidence: context ledger

Create one row per verified selection:

| Context | Visible label | Intended role | Sample result | Persistence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Title/version | Exact text | Standard, dub, description, or unknown | What was heard | Same after reopen? |

Use “unknown” when evidence is insufficient. This small discipline prevents a remembered guess from becoming household metadata.

## Manage series and offline copies

Do not assume every episode in a season contains identical tracks or labels. Sample representative episodes, then investigate outliers with a [series audio audit matrix](/blog/build-an-audio-track-audit-matrix-for-a-series/).

For offline use, verify the eligible item and selected alternatives before leaving connectivity. A local copy may reflect a particular item, version, and download state; compare it with the online context rather than assuming equivalence.

## Report problems precisely

A useful report states what was selected and what was heard. Include exact labels, source, item, version, device, time zone, steps, expected result, observed result, and a privacy-safe comparison. Redact credentials, source addresses, private history, and unrelated profiles.

## Common mistakes and limitations

Avoid ranking tracks by list position, treating language as role, assuming season-wide consistency, expecting preferences to persist universally, and changing several variables at once.

Track labels and behavior depend on supplied media and current controls. This guide provides a verification method, not a guarantee about any specific title or source.

## Frequently asked questions

### Why can two tracks have the same language?

They may differ by role, mix, codec, channels, or source metadata, but only visible evidence and a playback sample can establish the practical difference.

### Does choosing a track once make it permanent?

Not necessarily. Verify persistence for the relevant title, episode, version, profile, device, and playback context.

### What should I do when a track is missing?

Preserve the current list, verify the item and version, compare one known context, and report the result without destructive changes or secrets.

### Is original audio always the best choice?

No. Choose according to viewer needs, comprehension, accessibility, and the options actually supplied.
