# Build a Trusted-Device Inventory for the Household

> **In short:** List every supported device the household intentionally uses with Norva, then record its custodian, location or role, sign-in state, profiles used, eligible local media, update state, and exit action. Mark trust as a household judgement based on custody and access. Never put passwords, recovery codes, source credentials, payment data, or private history in the inventory.

An inventory answers “which screens intentionally retain our account?” It does not prove device security or product compatibility forever.

Choose one inventory owner and one confirmation contact per device. The owner maintains the record; the custodian confirms physical possession and intended sign-in. This division prevents an old label from being treated as current evidence.

## Separate supported from trusted

- **Supported:** Norva currently works through an approved web, mobile, or TV route.
- **Trusted:** the household accepts the device's owner, custody, lock, location, and account retention.

A device can be supported but not trusted for sign-in. Trust can change when it is lent, lost, sold, repaired, or reassigned.

## Identify each device reliably

Use a safe label that distinguishes the device without exposing a serial number publicly. Record type, household role, custodian, and operating-system or app version when useful.

Do not name every entry “TV” or “Tablet.” Distinct labels help incident response.

## Record account state, not credentials

Include:

- whether Norva is intentionally signed in;
- intended account;
- profiles normally used;
- whether credentials were saved;
- eligible local media present;
- notification or privacy concerns;
- last verified custodian.

Never record the password or source secret.

## Add an exit action

Every row needs a plan:

- sign out;
- remove eligible local media;
- restore device settings;
- follow manufacturer reset instructions;
- verify the account is no longer exposed;
- update the inventory.

The [device sale or return workflow](/blog/how-to-prepare-a-shared-device-before-it-is-sold-or-returned/) handles a permanent exit.

## Review after trigger events

Update the inventory after:

- a new device is approved;
- ownership or custody changes;
- a device is lost or unknown;
- someone moves in or out;
- a repair or factory reset;
- account or source credentials change;
- a material app update.

Do not change the review date when no review occurred.

Add last verified date, version, and notes only when operationally useful. Store the inventory where household coordinators can access it without exposing secrets.

For devices that move between trusted and temporary use, record the transition rather than creating duplicate rows. Note who approved the change, whether sign-out was verified, and what must happen before trust is restored.

## Verify the list physically

Ask custodians to confirm each device. Open the verified Norva route only when authorised and check the intended sign-in state. Do not scan networks or access another person's device without consent.

The [shared governance guide](/blog/the-complete-guide-to-shared-household-media-governance/) assigns the device custodian role.

## Handle an unknown entry or device

Do not immediately label it malicious. Preserve the identifier, compare household purchases, guests, repairs, and old device names, then use current account controls and support.

Follow [the unknown-device incident workflow](/blog/how-to-handle-an-unknown-device-as-a-household-incident/) when identity remains unresolved.

## Common mistakes and limitations

Avoid storing secrets, equating support with trust, using vague labels, listing devices nobody verified, and forgetting an exit action.

The inventory is an organisational record, not a security scan or guarantee. Device support, custody, and account controls can change.

## Reconcile from both directions

Compare the written inventory with devices physically present, then compare each device's official account view with the inventory. A device missing from either direction needs a named owner and resolution; do not assume an unfamiliar label is malicious before checking benign explanations.

## Frequently asked questions

### Should serial numbers be stored?

Only if genuinely needed and in an appropriately protected record. A household-facing inventory can use safer distinguishing labels.

### Does a trusted device stay trusted forever?

No. Reassess after custody, ownership, repair, loss, or access changes.

### Can the inventory show simultaneous-use rights?

No. Device records do not define plan or source concurrency conditions.
