# Recommendations, Search, or Browsing: Choose the Right Route

> **In short:** Use search when you can name the target or a precise attribute. Browse categories when you want to compare a defined range. Use recommendations when you have a trusted seed and want related alternatives. Switch routes only when the question changes; otherwise the switch adds browsing without improving the decision.

The three routes are complementary. Choosing deliberately makes discovery faster because each interface begins with a different kind of certainty.

## Use the route selector

Ask three questions in order:

1. **Can I name the target or a precise field?** Use search.
2. **Can I define the range I want to compare?** Browse a category or filter.
3. **Do I have a relevant seed but an open destination?** Follow recommendations.

If all answers are no, write a discovery brief first. “Something for tonight” needs constraints such as viewer, duration, language, format, or mood before any route can work well.

## Choose search for direct retrieval

Search is appropriate for a title, creator, series, episode, year, or other field the current interface supports. Use exact and alternate title forms when metadata varies. Inspect results for media type and version rather than selecting the first matching artwork.

Search can also verify whether an apparently missing recommendation exists in the connected source. Finding it does not prove why it was not suggested; it only separates availability from recommendation coverage.

## Browse when the comparison set matters

Category browsing works when the viewer wants to inspect a bounded group: a genre, year range, media type, language, or another available facet. Keep active filters visible and clear them before concluding that a title is absent.

DCMI terms for subject, date, type, format, and language show why a browse range depends on metadata quality. Compare [related titles and category browsing](/blog/related-titles-vs-category-browsing/) when both could answer the same brief.

## Use recommendations for relational exploration

Start from a verified title that represents the quality you want to preserve. Record the relation you hope to vary or retain: creator, period, subject, series connection, or something else visible. Then evaluate suggestions as leads from the compatible source, not as guaranteed matches.

Norva describes recommendations from a connected source. Exact logic and ordering require product verification. The subscription does not include the media library.

## Switch routes with a reason

A useful route sequence might be:

- search to find the exact seed;
- recommendations to identify related candidates;
- category browsing to compare those candidates against a wider range;
- search again to verify a specific version.

Write the reason at each switch. If the reason is merely “more results,” return to the original brief. Use [the focused-shortlist workflow](/blog/turn-recommendations-into-shortlist/) once several plausible cards appear.

W3C’s multiple-ways principle addresses offering more than one route to locate content. The discovery lesson is similar: multiple routes help only when each remains understandable and purposeful.

## Apply a stopping rule

Stop when one candidate is ready, the shortlist reaches its agreed size, or the brief cannot be answered with current source metadata. For recommendation-led exploration, [a three-hop session](/blog/three-hop-discovery-session/) prevents the seed from drifting beyond recognition.

Record unresolved needs separately. Endless switching does not create missing subtitles, versions, or source availability.

## Original evidence: route log

Run three tasks: retrieve one known title, compare one defined category, and find one related alternative. Record chosen route, time-neutral action count, switches, result, and missing information. Do not publish completion times as product benchmarks.

The route log shows whether the initial choice matched the task. It cannot compare internal search or recommendation algorithms.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Searching with an open-ended mood and no fields.
- Browsing an unbounded category without a stop rule.
- Following recommendations without checking the seed.
- Switching routes whenever results become less novel.
- Ignoring active filters and version identity.
- Treating source absence as a route failure.

## Frequently asked questions

### Can I combine routes in one session?

Yes. Give each transition a purpose and preserve the original discovery brief.

### Which route is fastest?

That depends on the task and metadata. The best route is the one matched to your starting certainty.

### What if search and browse disagree?

Check filters, title variants, media type, source availability, and metadata before judging either route.
