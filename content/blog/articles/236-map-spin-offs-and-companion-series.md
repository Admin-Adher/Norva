# How to Map Spin-Offs and Companion Series Clearly

> **In short:** Give every series its own identity, then connect works with typed, evidence-backed relationships such as spin-off, sequel, prequel, remake, shared setting, or companion production. Keep continuity and release order separate. A map may support several viewing routes; it should not claim that one route is universally correct.

Related shows become confusing when a folder or playlist implies a relationship it cannot explain. Two works may share a character but not continuity, or one may be a remake rather than a sequel. A typed graph keeps those differences visible.

## Build the typed relationship graph

Start with one row per work:

| Work ID | Canonical title | Release span | Continuity | Source checked | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
|  |  |  |  |  |  |

Then record every connection separately:

| From work | Relationship | To work | Direction | Evidence | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
|  | Spin-off / sequel / prequel / remake / companion / shared setting |  | One-way / reciprocal |  | High / medium / low |

The works are nodes; the typed connections are edges. Never merge two series records merely because their titles or characters are similar.

## Establish each work independently

Confirm canonical title, alternate titles, release dates, series identity, season structure, and stable identifiers where available. EIDR’s public model distinguishes series from seasons, episodes, edits, and manifestations. A related series should remain a separate series node even when it belongs to the same franchise.

Use [the miniseries-versus-multi-season test](/blog/distinguish-miniseries-multiseason/) when one node’s format is unclear. Format and relationship are different questions: a limited series can still be a prequel or companion work.

## Use precise relationship types

- **Spin-off:** centers a derived character, group, setting, or premise.
- **Sequel:** continues after another work within a stated continuity.
- **Prequel:** is positioned earlier in that continuity, regardless of release date.
- **Remake or adaptation:** retells or newly adapts material; it is not automatically the same continuity.
- **Companion series:** is designed to complement another work but may not continue its plot.
- **Shared setting:** overlaps a world or setting without proving direct narrative dependence.
- **Crossover:** contains a verified meeting or event involving distinct works.

Do not assign a stronger edge than the evidence supports. Promotional language, official series material, credits, and authoritative catalog records are stronger than title resemblance or fan ordering.

## Separate chronology from release order

Store at least two date concepts:

1. **Release order:** when the works or episodes became public in the relevant market.
2. **Story chronology:** where events are said to occur within a continuity.

The two can differ. Chronology may also be partial, disputed, or non-linear. Mark unknowns rather than forcing exact positions.

## Generate routes without rewriting the map

A route is a view over the graph, not a new truth. Useful routes include:

- original release order;
- verified story chronology;
- one character’s appearances;
- minimum-context route;
- one continuity only;
- completed works followed by unwatched related works.

Label the route and its rule. If a crossover requires alternating episodes, verify each episode identity before adding it. Avoid presenting a community preference as an official requirement.

## Connect the map to library state

Keep completion and favorites on the relevant work node. If a series is finished but its spin-off is not, [organize the completed series](/blog/organize-completed-series/) without marking the whole graph complete. Use [the favorite scope card](/blog/favorite-series-or-episode/) to decide whether interest applies to one series, a single episode, or the broader collection.

Norva can group variants and organize compatible authorized sources, but relationships and route claims still need evidence from the underlying metadata or authoritative references. Do not infer a franchise graph merely because items appear together in recommendations.

## Audit and maintain the graph

Date every edge and evidence note. When a new work appears:

1. create a new node;
2. verify its independent identity;
3. add only supported relationship edges;
4. update affected viewing routes;
5. preserve old routes as dated decisions if people already used them.

FIAF cataloguing guidance is a useful reminder that precise audiovisual description depends on distinguishing works, variants, and relationships rather than collapsing them.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Treating shared branding as shared continuity.
- Calling a remake a sequel.
- Using chronology as though it were release order.
- Merging two works into one series record.
- Offering one route without explaining its rule.
- Letting recommendations serve as relationship evidence.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is a crossover automatically a spin-off?

No. Record crossover and spin-off as separate claims, each with its own evidence.

### What if story chronology is disputed?

Keep multiple labeled interpretations or mark the edge uncertain. Do not manufacture a precise order.

### Can films and specials appear in the same graph?

Yes. Give each work the correct entity type and use typed relationships rather than converting everything into a series episode.
