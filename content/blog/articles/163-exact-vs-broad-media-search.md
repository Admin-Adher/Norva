# Exact or Broad Search: Which Query Should You Try First?

> **In short:** Start broad when you remember only part of a title, spelling is uncertain, or alternate titles may exist. Start exact only when you know the full phrase and the interface’s exact-match syntax. After the first query, let the result shape decide: narrow a large relevant set; broaden or repair a zero-result query; verify a single result with independent metadata.

Exact and broad are not rival philosophies. They are tools for different levels of certainty and different result sets.

## Define exact and broad for the interface

A **broad search** may match words across a title or several indexed fields, regardless of precise phrase order. An **exact search** may require the same words in the same order or a specific field value.

Those meanings vary by product. The Library of Congress Search Help uses quotation marks for exact phrases in its search, and its Advanced Search guide distinguishes “contains all,” “contains any,” “exact phrase,” and “starts with.” Do not transfer that syntax to Norva unless current guidance confirms it.

## Use the certainty-result search quadrant

Choose a starting action:

| Memory certainty | Expected result size | Start with | Goal |
|---|---|---|---|
| Low | Unknown | Broad distinctive fragment | Discover stored wording |
| High | Large | Exact or fielded title | Confirm a known phrase |
| High | Small | Broad title plus context | Avoid brittle overconstraint |
| Low | Large | Broad fragment, then one filter | Learn and narrow |

After the query, reclassify based on the actual results rather than defending the initial strategy.

## Start broad in four situations

Use a broad query when:

- only one or two title words are remembered;
- spelling, plural, punctuation, or number form is uncertain;
- the remembered name may be translated or localised;
- you remember a person or series context rather than the exact title.

Choose the rarest reliable word through [the partial-title method](/blog/search-with-partial-titles/). Broad does not mean vague: “moon” may be broad, while a distinctive surname or unusual title word can be both short and informative.

## Start exact in limited situations

An exact query is useful when:

- the complete title is certain;
- many records share its component words;
- word order changes meaning;
- punctuation or a quoted phrase is part of known supported syntax;
- a known identifier or field is being verified.

Exact matching can fail when the stored title includes a subtitle, alternate spelling, localised form, edition label, or metadata error. Treat zero results as a signal to broaden, not proof the item is absent.

## Follow the broad-to-exact two-pass

### Pass 1: discovery

Run one broad, high-information term. Inspect titles, years, people, types, and sources in the first results. Identify how the catalogue spells and structures the likely record.

### Pass 2: confirmation

Use the discovered title form plus one verified context clue. If exact syntax is supported, apply it only now. Confirm the chosen record using year, creator, synopsis, series placement, or version.

This sequence lets the catalogue teach you its stored wording before you demand an exact match.

## Respond to each result shape

- **Zero:** remove the least certain term, clear filters, try alternate title forms, and check a known control.
- **One:** verify identity; do not assume ranking equals correctness.
- **Several relevant:** add year, type, person, or series context.
- **Many irrelevant:** replace a common word with a distinctive one or use a supported field/filter.
- **Expected record with wrong context:** investigate metadata instead of refining the query indefinitely.

For repeated names, use [the similar-title workflow](/blog/search-through-similar-title-results/). For huge result sets, use [the large-catalogue search plan](/blog/search-very-large-media-catalog/).

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Quoting an uncertain phrase.
- Treating broad search as entering many vague words.
- Adding several filters before seeing the baseline.
- Assuming exact search checks every metadata field.
- Interpreting one result as verified identity.
- Reusing another service’s operators without confirmation.

Broad queries cannot recover absent aliases, and exact queries cannot correct wrong metadata. Search strategy and catalogue quality remain connected.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is exact search always faster?

It can reduce results, but only when the stored phrase and syntax match. A failed exact query often costs more attempts than a well-chosen broad fragment.

### Should I use quotes around every full title?

Only when the interface documents quotation marks as exact syntax and the title form is certain. Otherwise, quotes may be ignored or treated as literal characters.

### What if broad and exact searches show the same results?

The interface may not support a distinct exact mode, or the result set may already be small. Confirm current guidance and focus on contextual verification.
