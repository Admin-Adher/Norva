# How to Handle an Unknown Device as a Household Incident

> **In short:** Preserve the unfamiliar device label and where it appeared, then compare it with the household inventory, old device names, guests, repairs, browser sessions, and recent handoffs. An unknown label is not proof of compromise. If it remains unexplained, protect credentials through official account and source processes, use only verified session controls, contact support, and document every action without sharing secrets.

A calm verification-first response avoids both underreaction and destructive guessing.

Choose one coordinator to own the timeline. Other household members can confirm custody or supply context, but they should not independently remove sessions, rename devices, or change credentials. A single action record makes it possible to distinguish evidence gathered before containment from results observed afterward.

## Freeze concurrent changes

Ask household members not to rename devices, reconnect sources, reset apps, or change profiles until the identifier is recorded. Parallel changes can erase the evidence.

Capture:

- exact device label;
- screen or account area where it appeared;
- local date and time zone;
- account context;
- app/browser version if visible;
- whether the device shows current activity;
- a privacy-safe screenshot when authorised.

Do not capture account email, source URLs, passwords, or private history.

## Compare the trusted-device inventory

Check [the household device inventory](/blog/build-a-trusted-device-inventory-for-the-household/) for old names, repaired devices, browsers, guest screens, and recently sold equipment. Ask custodians to confirm physical possession.

Do not scan or access another person's device without consent.

## Check benign explanations

Consider:

- a renamed tablet or TV;
- a compatible-browser session;
- a device after reset or repair;
- a guest device intentionally used earlier;
- a duplicate label;
- stale household documentation.

Record evidence for or against each explanation. Do not dismiss the incident solely because one seems likely.

## Contain through verified controls

If the device remains unexplained:

1. preserve current evidence;
2. review available Norva account or session controls;
3. sign out or remove access only when the control and consequence are verified;
4. follow official authentication-provider recovery when account credentials may be exposed;
5. notify the source owner and follow the source provider's official process if source credentials may be involved;
6. contact Norva support.

Do not claim remote revocation or password-change effects without visible confirmation.

## Protect source credentials

Follow [the household credential boundary](/blog/how-to-keep-source-credentials-out-of-household-sharing/). Never paste the suspect credential or private endpoint into a support ticket.

Changing a source secret is a source-owner action and may affect every connected device. Coordinate it rather than improvising.

## Verify the resolution

A confirmed known device should receive an updated safe label and custodian. A device removed through verified controls should be rechecked only through the official account view. An unresolved device stays an open incident with support.

The [governance guide](/blog/the-complete-guide-to-shared-household-media-governance/) defines incident ownership and communication.

## Communicate proportionately

Tell household members what they need to know: affected account or source, required sign-in action, and device review. Do not broadcast private viewing details or unverified accusations.

## Common mistakes and limitations

Avoid immediately blaming a person, deleting profiles, resetting every device, sharing credentials to compare them, and declaring removal successful without evidence.

This workflow is not a security guarantee or forensic investigation. Product, source, and authentication controls vary; use official support when identity remains unknown.

## Close the incident explicitly

Record whether the device was identified, removed through verified controls, or remains unresolved; name the follow-up owner and date. Do not keep sending broad household alerts after containment is confirmed.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does an unknown label prove account compromise?

No. Verify old names, browsers, guests, repairs, and inventory gaps before drawing a conclusion.

### Should every password be changed immediately?

Follow legitimate risk-based official recovery guidance. Coordinate account and source credentials separately.

### What if no device-management control is visible?

Preserve evidence, protect credentials through verified provider processes, and contact Norva support. Do not invent a control path.
