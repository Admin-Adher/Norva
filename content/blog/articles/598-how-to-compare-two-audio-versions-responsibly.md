# How to Compare Two Audio Versions Responsibly

> **In short:** Match the source edit, mix purpose, excerpt, playback device, route, processing, output, room, and safe programme loudness. Hide labels and vary order where practical. Compare dialogue, transients, ambience, tonal balance, dynamics, spatial image, noise, and artifacts across several excerpts. Report trade-offs and unknowns instead of one universal winner.

If a stereo mix and multichannel mix use different mastering, a result is a comparison of complete versions, not proof of a codec or channel-count effect.

## Define the question

State whether the choice concerns language, dialogue, spatial presentation, compression, compatibility, or preference. List controlled variables and known differences before listening.

Do not begin with a badge-based expected winner.

## Verify versions and rights

Use only tracks the listener owns or is authorised to use. Record source and mix lineage, edit, duration, language, codec, bitrate context, channels, sample rate, bit depth, loudness, and dynamics where verified. Mark unknowns.

Never expose source addresses, tokens, credentials, or copyrighted excerpts.

## Match level safely

Louder audio often seems clearer or fuller. Use valid programme-loudness measurement or careful safe perceived matching, with fixed route and processing. Lower level before switching and stop with discomfort.

Peak matching alone does not guarantee programme-loudness matching.

## Select an excerpt set

Include dialogue, quiet ambience, transients, sustained tones, dense music, reverberation, stereo movement, multichannel cues, bass, and silence. Use exact timecodes and short trials to limit fatigue.

One excerpt cannot rank every dimension.

| Excerpt | Dimension | Version A | Version B | Order | Controlled variables | Confidence |

| Ambience | Noise/image | Observation | Observation | Order | Context | Level |

Do not average different dimensions unless weighting was justified before listening.

## Conceal and alternate

Hide filenames and labels, let another person switch when practical, and reverse order. Ask for observations before preference. Keep listeners' notes separate until a round finishes.

ITU-R BS.1116 and BS.1534 provide formal methods; household testing should not claim equivalent rigor.

## Repeat meaningful differences

Replay from before the event, insert a short rest, and reverse order. If the difference disappears, report uncertainty. Stop after a planned number of trials rather than listening until an expected result appears.

[The repeatable listening protocol](/blog/build-a-repeatable-audio-quality-listening-protocol/) provides the session structure.

## Keep a decision threshold

Before the comparison, define what would make a difference useful: clearer dialogue on the actual output, fewer recurring artifacts, better route compatibility, or a stable personal preference across multiple excerpts. A tiny difference that appears once may not justify changing the selected version, while a repeatable compatibility problem may matter even if it is not subtle.

Record inconclusive outcomes as inconclusive. If two versions use different mixes, languages, or edits, limit the conclusion to those complete versions. Do not convert a listening preference into a claim that every file with the same codec, bitrate, or channel label will behave identically.

## Name artifacts and trade-offs

Describe pre-echo, warble, smearing, clipping, noise, image instability, dialogue masking, or dynamic changes at exact moments. Use [the audio-artifact guide](/blog/how-to-recognize-audio-compression-artifacts/) instead of "more detailed."

One version can preserve transients while another has steadier ambience; report both.

## Report responsibly

Include question, versions, verified metadata, unknowns, excerpts, level method, route, processing, output, room, order, repeats, separate observations, preferences, fatigue, and limitations. Use [the audio-quality checklist](/blog/an-audio-quality-literacy-checklist/) for completeness.

Current Norva track metadata and controls require official verification. Norva organises and plays compatible authorised sources; it does not certify their mixes.

## Common mistakes and limitations

Avoid unmatched loudness, different routes, labels visible, unlimited repetition, or claims about one codec from unmatched masters. Preference is not universal quality proof.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is blind comparison required?

Not always, but concealed labels and reversed order reduce expectation bias when practical.

### Should the result choose one winner?

No. Preserve dimension-specific strengths, trade-offs, and uncertainty.

### Can two language tracks be a codec test?

Usually not without verified matched mixes and masters; language production can differ substantially.
