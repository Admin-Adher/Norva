# How to Resume on a Shared Screen Without Exposing Private History

> **In short:** Treat the shared screen as a privacy decision before it becomes a playback target. Classify who can access it, inspect the account and profile information that may appear, suppress private notification previews through documented device controls, and sign in only when the exposure is acceptable. At exit, pause, remove temporary local state, sign out when required, and test reopening.

Cross-device continuity can make history and progress convenient, but those same states may be visible to anyone using a signed-in shared screen. A profile organises viewing context; it is not a security boundary from people who can enter the same account.

## Classify the target screen

Choose one:

- trusted household screen with known users;
- temporary borrowed screen;
- communal or public screen.

For a borrowed, communal, or public target, avoid sign-in unless a verified session flow and device policy make the exposure acceptable. Do not rely on closing a window as proof of sign-out.

## Review what may become visible

Before authenticating, consider whether the target could display:

- profile names and icons;
- continuation cards;
- history-derived recommendations from the connected source;
- favourites;
- account email or settings;
- saved credentials;
- local eligible media;
- unrelated device notifications.

Norva states that history, progress, favourites, and preferences can follow the same account on supported devices. Check the public privacy policy for current data practices.

## Choose the intended profile privately

Sign in away from onlookers when appropriate and select the intended profile before opening media. Do not expose passwords, recovery codes, private source URLs, or payment information.

Use [the profile-preserving handoff workflow](/blog/how-to-change-screens-without-changing-household-profiles/) to verify profile identity before the continuation card.

## Limit the session's visible scope

Open the exact item using title, episode, and version fields. Avoid browsing unrelated history on the shared display. If a search box retains sensitive terms, clear them only through documented controls and only when the data belongs to the current user.

Disable or hide notification previews only when the device owner agrees, and restore them afterward. Preserve essential accessibility and safety alerts.

## Verify the media state

Match account, profile, authorised source, item, version, and position. The [cross-device state guide](/blog/a-state-by-state-guide-to-cross-device-viewing-handoff/) keeps these checks separate from screen privacy.

Select available audio and subtitles, then verify output at a low comfortable level. Shared-room audio itself may reveal private content, so test with a non-sensitive section.

## End the session cleanly

1. Pause and record the final position.
2. Remove temporary eligible local items when appropriate.
3. Restore temporary notification or display changes.
4. Return to a neutral screen.
5. Sign out if the target or next user is not trusted.
6. Close the app or browser.
7. Reopen once and verify that the private account is not exposed.

For a shared tablet, follow the fuller [sign-in and sign-out routine](/blog/a-shared-tablet-sign-in-and-sign-out-routine/).

## When not to resume

Do not use the target if you cannot hide credentials, cannot sign out reliably, see unknown device management, or would expose private history to people without consent. Continue on a trusted device instead.

Contact Norva support when the visible session controls are unclear, but remove sensitive information from any evidence.

## Common mistakes and limitations

Avoid treating profiles as passwords, trusting private browsing to erase every trace, leaving local items behind, showing account settings on a large display, and skipping the reopen test.

Device storage, browsers, operating systems, and authentication providers differ. This workflow reduces exposure but cannot guarantee privacy on a device controlled by someone else.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does using a separate profile hide the account from other users?

No. It separates viewing context when available; it does not secure the signed-in account from someone with access.

### Is a household TV always trusted?

Not automatically. Consider who can use it, whether it retains sessions, and whether account details are visible.

### Should I delete all history after a shared-screen session?

Do not remove unrelated data. End the session correctly and change only information that belongs to the current user through verified controls.
