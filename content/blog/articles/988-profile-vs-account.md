# Profile vs. Account: Understanding the Boundary

> **In short:** An account is the service-level identity and administrative relationship used for sign-in, recovery, billing, plan, source connections, and device management. A profile is a viewing context inside the account, commonly used for progress, favorites, preferences, and recommendations. A profile should not be assumed to have separate credentials or security isolation.

The distinction matters on shared screens. Selecting a profile can change what viewing state appears, but the household may still be operating inside one signed-in account controlled by one administrator.

The [media player glossary](/blog/media-player-glossary/) places profiles and accounts alongside sessions, trusted devices, authentication, and authorization.

## The account creates the service relationship

The account typically controls sign-in, recovery, current plan, billing relationship, terms acceptance, connected sources, support cases, and device records. Exact controls vary by service and should be confirmed in the official interface.

Account credentials should be protected and never placed in a profile label or shared troubleshooting document.

## A profile organizes viewing context

A profile can separate progress, favorites, history, language preferences, or recommendations for recurring viewing contexts. It may represent one person, a shared household use, or another clear purpose.

Use the [first household profile guide](/blog/create-first-household-profile/) to choose neutral labels and ownership rules.

## Profiles are not automatically security boundaries

A profile picker often appears after the account is already signed in. Unless the service explicitly provides and documents a separate lock or permission model, do not assume another household member cannot view or change profile state.

Review actual shared-screen visibility. A hidden profile row is not the same as access control.

## Billing and entitlements belong to the account

The subscription plan and its current capabilities apply through the account relationship. A profile does not normally hold a separate payment receipt or subscription entitlement.

Check current official pricing and terms for profile limits. Editorial product guidance currently describes up to two profiles for Norva and up to five for Norva Family, but those claims require human verification before publication.

## Sources and devices belong to administration

Connecting an authorized source and reviewing trusted devices are account-administration tasks. A profile selects a viewing context within the resulting experience; it does not prove source authorization or device ownership.

Do not name a profile after a source address, password hint, device token, or exact room.

## Viewing state can belong to profiles

Progress and favorites should be tested under the intended profile. Comparing one profile on a TV with another on a phone can falsely suggest synchronization failure. Record account and profile independently in every cross-screen trace.

Actual state scope is product-specific, so verify progress, favorites, and preferences separately rather than inferring all from one successful example.

## Deletion consequences differ

Removing a profile may affect its viewing context. Closing an account can affect the broader service relationship, sources, devices, plan, and data. Never treat the two actions as equivalent.

Read the current interface, privacy policy, and terms before a destructive decision. Preserve only necessary, sanitized evidence and verify the result.

## Household administration needs both layers

Assign an account administrator for security, billing, sources, devices, and support. Assign a profile owner or affected viewers for naming, preferences, and retirement decisions. The same person can hold both roles, but the responsibilities remain distinct.

The [two-profile household evaluation](/blog/norva-for-two-profile-households/) tests selection, state separation, shared-title collisions, and wrong-profile recovery.

## Common boundary mistakes

- Sharing account credentials to create profile independence.
- Treating a profile as a separate security account.
- Comparing synchronization across different profiles.
- Encoding source or device details in profile names.
- Assuming each profile has its own subscription.
- Confusing profile deletion with account closure.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does each profile need a password?

Do not assume so. A profile is generally a viewing context inside an account. Verify any lock or permission feature through current official guidance.

### Can profiles keep different language preferences?

They may where supported, but actual tracks depend on the source, media, version, device, and rights. Test each intended context.

### Who should remove an obsolete profile?

The account administrator should coordinate with affected viewers, understand the consequences, preserve necessary evidence, and use the current supported control.
