# Favorites, Watchlists, and Continue Watching: How They Differ

> **In short:** Favourites are deliberate “keep this” choices. A watchlist usually means “consider this later.” Continue Watching reflects unfinished activity based on playback history and progress. Labels and exact behaviour vary by product, so organise around the decision each view should support rather than assuming every app uses the terms identically.

When these views overlap, they become three long lists of the same titles. Giving each one a clear purpose keeps the library useful.

## A decision-based comparison

| View | Primary question | Usually added by | Best used for |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Favourites | “Which items matter to me?” | Explicit user action | durable personal choices |
| Watchlist | “What might I start later?” | Explicit user action | future viewing intent |
| Continue Watching | “What did I start and not finish?” | Playback activity | immediate resumption |
| History or Recent | “What did I interact with?” | Account activity | recall and diagnosis |

Not every player offers all four labels. Norva publicly documents favourites, history, playback progress, and continuity features. Treat “watchlist” here as a general organisational concept, not an unsupported Norva feature claim.

## Favourites should remain deliberate

A favourite can represent a title you value, revisit, or want to keep easy to find. It is durable compared with an active-progress row.

The list loses meaning if every mildly interesting item is added. A simple rule is to favourite only items that you would recognise as intentional a month later.

Favourites can be useful across supported screens when the product syncs them through an account. Norva states that favourites are among the preferences preserved on supported devices.

## A watchlist is about the future

A watchlist, where a product provides one, captures consideration before playback. It is closer to a reading queue than a record of activity.

Useful maintenance questions include:

- Do I still intend to start this?
- Is it still available from my authorised source?
- Does it belong in a temporary list or a durable favourite?
- Is the same title already active in Continue Watching?

If no distinct watchlist exists, do not force favourites to perform every role. Categories, search, and a short favourites list may be enough.

## Continue Watching is generated from behaviour

Continue Watching usually relies on playback progress. It should answer an immediate question without manual filing.

Briefly opening several items can pollute the row. Completion rules can also vary. A product may provide controls to remove an item, mark it complete, or hide unavailable entries.

The underlying relationship between history and progress is explained in [Why Watch History and Playback Progress Save Time](/blog/watch-history-playback-progress-benefits/).

## The keep, start, resume, review framework

Use four verbs to assign purpose:

- **Keep:** favourites for durable personal value.
- **Start:** a watchlist or equivalent future-intent view.
- **Resume:** Continue Watching for active progress.
- **Review:** history or recent activity for recall.

Take ten visible items and ask which verb applies. If one item appears in several views, that can be reasonable, but each view should still answer a different question.

This framework is an editorial organisation tool, not a claim that every app supports four separate controls.

## A ten-minute clean-up

1. Open Continue Watching and remove abandoned items only when the product offers a clear control.
2. Finish or mark genuinely completed items according to documented behaviour.
3. Review favourites and remove accidental additions.
4. Move future ideas into the product’s intended future-planning feature, if available.
5. Leave history alone unless there is a privacy or accuracy reason to change it.
6. Confirm one intentional favourite and one active item on another supported screen.

The more detailed [Continue Watching guide](/blog/manage-continue-watching/) focuses on keeping that row compact.

## Where “Recent” fits

Recent activity is descriptive rather than aspirational. It can include completed items, brief tests, or accidental starts. It is therefore useful for finding something seen lately, but not always a good replacement for favourites.

The article [Recent, Favorites, or All Titles](/blog/recent-favorites-all-titles/) compares the browsing situations each view handles well.

## Privacy and household considerations

History and progress can reveal viewing habits. Shared accounts should use documented profiles where appropriate and review the privacy policy for account controls. Norva’s public pricing page states that Norva supports up to two profiles and Norva Family up to five; profile count must not be interpreted as a device or simultaneous-playback limit.

Do not use a shared favourites list as proof of who viewed an item. A favourite records an account or profile action, not identity beyond the system’s documented model.

## Common mistakes and limitations

Avoid building dozens of micro-lists that require more maintenance than they save. Do not assume that removing an item from Continue Watching deletes its history; product behaviour varies. Do not infer current availability from an old favourite.

A source change can alter identity or availability, so list entries may need review after a major catalogue update.

## Frequently asked questions

### Should a favourite also appear in Continue Watching?

It can. One view records deliberate preference; the other records unfinished activity. The overlap is not automatically a duplicate.

### Why did an item enter Continue Watching after only a brief test?

The product may record progress once playback begins. Check its available remove or completion controls rather than assuming a defect.

### Is a watchlist required for good organisation?

No. Clear favourites, search, categories, and active-progress views can be sufficient. Use only the structures that support real decisions.
