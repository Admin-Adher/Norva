# How to Run a Quarterly Household Account Review

> **In short:** Once each quarter, confirm the account coordinator and backup, source ownership and authorisation, trusted-device custody, active profile owners, billing contact, privacy practices, and unresolved incidents. Review exceptions rather than personal viewing. Assign every action an owner and date, then delete evidence that no longer has a security, support, or rollback purpose.

A quarterly review is a maintenance checkpoint, not a performance report on household members. Its purpose is to keep shared responsibilities accurate before a move, broken device, expired source, or support incident exposes gaps.

## Prepare a privacy-safe agenda

Send the agenda in advance and ask only the relevant owners to confirm their areas. Do not export histories, favorites, searches, or title-level activity.

Use these sections:

1. account roles and backup contact;
2. subscription and billing awareness;
3. connected-source ownership and authorisation;
4. trusted-device custody;
5. profile ownership and lifecycle;
6. privacy and credential handling;
7. unresolved incidents and support cases;
8. follow-up actions.

Review current plan terms on the official pricing or terms pages instead of copying limits into a permanent household document.

## Confirm roles and continuity

Verify who coordinates the account, who can cover an absence, and who owns each source and device. The coordinator should not hold source credentials that belong to another person unless that owner has explicitly authorised the arrangement.

Ask whether the backup could complete a basic handoff using the existing documentation. Do not expose secrets merely to prove the backup exists.

## Reconcile sources and devices

For every connected source, record owner, purpose, current authorisation status as confirmed by that owner, and the safe reconnection path. Do not paste source addresses or credentials into the review.

Compare current household devices with the [trusted-device inventory](/blog/build-a-trusted-device-inventory-for-the-household/). Confirm physical custodian, location, last-known purpose, and whether a sold, returned, repaired, or guest device still needs action. Use only account and session controls that are currently documented and visible.

## Review profiles without inspecting people

Confirm that each active profile has a current owner, a clear shared or personal purpose, and no unresolved handoff. Profiles organise context; they are not passwords, parental controls, device limits, or content rights.

Ask profile owners whether they need help. Do not inspect their private history to decide whether a profile is “inactive.”

## Check privacy and credential practice

Review whether credentials stay with source owners, whether temporary screens receive a sign-out check, and whether screenshots or support notes contain private data. Apply the routine in [keeping source credentials out of household sharing](/blog/how-to-keep-source-credentials-out-of-household-sharing/).

Delete obsolete screenshots and redact retained incident records. Keep only what explains an unresolved case, a material change, or a necessary rollback.

## Original evidence: quarterly agenda

Use one row per review domain:

| Domain | Owner confirms | Exception | Action owner | Due date |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Account | Coordinator and backup | None or issue | Named person | Date |
| Sources | Ownership and authority | Expired access | Source owner | Date |
| Devices | Custody and purpose | Unknown label | Custodian | Date |
| Profiles | Owner and purpose | Handoff needed | Profile owner | Date |
| Privacy | Minimal evidence | Old screenshot | Coordinator | Date |

Record “confirmed by owner” rather than copying sensitive proof into the table.

## Close the review properly

Read back each exception, owner, and due date. Separate urgent access or credential issues from routine organisation. If an unknown device or unexplained access remains, follow the verified incident and support path rather than experimenting.

Schedule the next checkpoint, but allow event-driven reviews after moves, device disposal, source ownership changes, or serious incidents. Fold the agenda into the [shared household governance model](/blog/the-complete-guide-to-shared-household-media-governance/).

## Common mistakes and limitations

Avoid collecting personal activity, copying current plan limits into evergreen notes, forcing every owner to reveal credentials, and treating “no complaint” as verified health.

The review confirms household process. It does not audit Norva's infrastructure, guarantee account security, or establish rights to any source or media.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does the review have to happen exactly every three months?

No. Quarterly is a memorable cadence; use a schedule appropriate to the household and review sooner after material changes.

### Should viewing history be included?

No. Review ownership, responsibility, and exceptions. Title-level activity is generally unnecessary and may invade privacy.

### Who keeps the completed agenda?

Assign a coordinator, restrict access to those who need it, remove secrets, and delete the record when it no longer serves continuity or incident needs.
