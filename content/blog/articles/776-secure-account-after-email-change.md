# A Security Checklist After Changing Your Account Email

> **In short:** Confirm the email change through Norva's official account or support route, then test that the new inbox can receive legitimate account and recovery messages. Secure both the new and old mailboxes, review recovery methods, sessions, devices, password-manager records, and household roles, and update authorized-source contacts separately. Record the change as requested, pending, or confirmed; never place passwords, codes, or live links in the record.

An email address can be a sign-in identifier, a notification destination, and a recovery dependency. Changing the visible address does not prove that every one of those functions moved, nor that the old inbox stopped receiving sensitive messages.

## Confirm the Norva-side result

Open Norva through a known official route rather than an email link. Check the current account state or contact official support if the product does not expose a clear confirmation. Record whether the request was submitted, awaiting verification, or confirmed.

Do not infer product behavior. Whether an email change affects sign-in, active sessions, notifications, or recovery depends on current Norva documentation. A screenshot of the requested change is evidence of a request, not proof of completion.

## Secure the new mailbox first

Give the new mailbox a unique password, review its recovery methods, inspect active devices and sessions, and check for unfamiliar forwarding, filters, or delegated access under the provider's current instructions. The [recovery-email security guide](/blog/secure-recovery-email-media-account/) provides a boundary-by-boundary review.

Test only through an official account flow. Do not repeatedly trigger recovery messages or share a live verification link with another person. Confirm that expected messages arrive and that sender and destination details match the official process.

## Keep control of the old mailbox during transition

Do not abandon or delete the old mailbox immediately. Review whether it still receives Norva alerts, source notifications, password-manager notices, billing communications, or recovery messages. Remove unauthorized forwarding and secure its sessions.

Choose a transition period based on the services involved and current provider policies rather than a universal number of days. If the old address belongs to an employer, school, former provider, or expiring domain, document the loss-of-access date and escalate dependencies before it arrives.

## Review credentials, sessions, and devices

An email change is not automatically a password change. If the old address or mailbox was compromised, create a unique Norva password through the official destination and follow the [post-password session review](/blog/post-password-change-session-review/).

Inspect trusted devices and sessions using controls currently documented by each service. Mark unfamiliar access for investigation, and do not assume that updating an identifier closes existing sessions.

## Update each source independently

Norva and compatible sources the account owner is authorized to use are separate boundaries. An email change at Norva does not change a source account, and a source-email change does not update Norva.

Use the [credential-separation map](/blog/separate-account-and-source-credentials/) to update the correct owner, official destination, recovery dependency, and password-manager record without copying secret values into a household checklist.

## Communicate the change safely

Tell authorized household administrators which identifier should now be used and when the old one will retire. Do not broadcast the new password or forward a verification link. Update the responsibility matrix, emergency contact card, and support records that legitimately need the address.

If the change was unexpected, stop treating it as routine maintenance. Use a trusted device to secure recovery, preserve alerts, change affected credentials, and begin the credential-exposure response.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Assuming the displayed email proves recovery also changed.
- Deleting the old inbox before checking dependencies.
- Using the same password for the new inbox and Norva.
- Forwarding verification links to household members.
- Treating source accounts as if Norva changed them automatically.
- Marking the transition complete before checking official evidence.
- Following instructions from an unexpected email instead of opening Norva directly.

## Frequently asked questions

### Should I change my password when I change my email?

Not automatically for routine maintenance, but change any exposed or reused password through the official service and keep every boundary unique.

### When can I close the old mailbox?

Only after legitimate dependencies have moved, expected alerts reach the new inbox, and the old provider's retention and recovery implications are understood.

### What if I did not request the email change?

Use a known-good device, secure the recovery mailbox, preserve the alert, review sessions, and contact official Norva support immediately.
