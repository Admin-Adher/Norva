# Hide, Remove, or Delete: Choose the Right Cleanup Action

> **In short:** Hide usually changes visibility, remove usually detaches an item or reference from a particular catalogue view, and delete may destroy the underlying asset or data. Exact meanings vary by product and source. Choose the least destructive supported action that meets the verified goal, and confirm scope, personal context, recovery, and source effect before approving it.

The words sound interchangeable in everyday conversation, but their consequences can differ greatly. Never infer an action’s scope from its button label alone.

## Understand the three scopes

Use these as working definitions, then check the product and source documentation:

- **Hide:** keep the record or asset available but exclude it from a view, profile, recommendation, or normal browse route.
- **Remove:** detach a catalogue reference, category membership, source connection, or local entry without necessarily erasing the underlying asset.
- **Delete:** erase an asset, record, or associated data from the location named in the confirmation.

A control may act only inside an organiser, or it may send a change to a connected source. Norva organises compatible sources that the user is authorised to connect; available controls and their effects can vary. When the interface or documentation is ambiguous, stop and ask support.

## Begin with the outcome, not the button

Write one desired outcome:

- “Stop showing this inactive category in normal browsing.”
- “Detach an old source that has already been migrated and verified.”
- “Remove an exact duplicate reference while retaining the valid version.”
- “Permanently erase an asset from its storage location.”

The first three may not require deletion. A precise outcome prevents a visual annoyance from becoming an irreversible storage decision.

## Use the least-destructive action ladder

Answer these questions in order:

1. **Visibility:** Would filtering, hiding, or archiving solve the viewing problem?
2. **Reference:** If not, can a category membership or catalogue reference be removed without affecting the asset?
3. **Relationship:** Would the action break a series, version group, favourite, or progress link?
4. **Source:** Does the control act only in the organiser or also on the connected source?
5. **Recovery:** Is there a tested way to restore both the item and its relationships?
6. **Proof:** Have ownership, identity, scope, and duplicates been verified?

Escalate only when the preceding action cannot achieve the outcome. For any destructive proposal, follow [the reversible cleanup workflow](/blog/make-catalog-cleanup-reversible/).

## Test with a reversible example

Choose a low-risk item that behaves like the intended batch. Record where it appears, apply the weakest appropriate control, refresh normally, and check search, browse, favourites, history, versions, and source visibility where relevant.

If the result differs from the confirmation text or documentation, restore the item and stop. Do not assume the next item will behave differently.

## When deletion may be appropriate

Deletion can be a legitimate goal when the authorised owner intentionally wants to erase an identified asset or data and understands the scope. It still requires confirmation that the item is not the only copy needed, the target is correct, dependencies are understood, and recovery or deliberate non-recovery has been approved.

The Library of Congress recommends identifying where personal digital materials are stored and making copies as part of preservation planning. That does not mean every deletion needs permanent retention; it means recovery choices should be conscious rather than accidental.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Trusting a generic label without reading its scope.
- Testing only whether the poster disappeared.
- Removing a category before checking its intake mapping.
- Treating two similar titles as duplicates.
- Forgetting household profiles and progress.
- Assuming cloud, local, and source-side deletion are equivalent.

The action ladder cannot override source permissions or guarantee recovery. It helps expose the decision that must be confirmed.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is hiding always reversible?

Often, but not universally. Confirm where hidden items can be reviewed and whether the setting belongs to a profile, account, device, or source.

### Does removing a source delete its media?

Do not assume either outcome. Read the exact confirmation and current support guidance, then test with non-essential content if the workflow permits.

### What should I do if a warning is unclear?

Cancel. Record the screen, item, source, and intended outcome, then consult support before trying again.
