# How to Record Major Library Changes Without Micromanaging

> **In short:** Log only changes that affect several people, alter a connected source, restructure shared categories, change profile ownership, modify trusted devices, or matter for rollback. Record date, owner, reason, affected contexts, before/after state, verification, and reversal path. Exclude personal viewing, routine favourites, credentials, private URLs, and content-level surveillance.

A useful change log explains why the household library looks different after a material action. It should not become a diary of who watched what.

Set a clear retention rule as well. Keep an entry while it still helps someone explain, verify, or reverse a shared change. Once the household has confirmed the new baseline and the record has no recovery value, remove obsolete screenshots and notes. A smaller, current log is easier to trust than an archive filled with stale details.

## Define “major” by impact

A change deserves a record when it:

- affects multiple profiles or devices;
- connects, refreshes, or removes a source;
- changes shared category structure through verified controls;
- alters version grouping or item identity at scale;
- onboards or offboards a profile;
- changes the trusted-device inventory;
- follows an incident or support instruction;
- has a non-obvious rollback.

Routine personal progress, a favourite on one's own profile, and a one-item language choice usually do not need a shared log.

## Use a significance filter

Ask:

1. Will another household member notice the change?
2. Could it explain a later problem?
3. Would rollback require context?
4. Does it affect authorisation, privacy, or access?
5. Is the action broader than one person's profile?

Log when at least one answer creates real recovery value. Do not invent a numerical threshold.

## Capture minimum fields

Record:

- date, local time zone, and change owner;
- layer: account, source, profile, device, or library structure;
- reason;
- affected profiles and devices using neutral labels;
- before and after state;
- approval required and received;
- verification result;
- rollback or support reference.

Never record passwords, source credentials, account emails, payment data, or detailed private history.

## Link to source stewardship

Source changes should identify the authorised source steward and impact, not the secret. The [source responsibility guide](/blog/how-to-assign-source-connection-responsibilities-at-home/) explains the connection card.

## Keep troubleshooting separate but linked

A troubleshooting log records experiments; the household change log records the final material action. Link the support case or experiment summary without copying sensitive evidence.

Use [the household troubleshooting rule](/blog/how-to-set-a-household-rule-for-troubleshooting-changes/) for one-change-at-a-time work.

## Review the log for usefulness

After a later change, ask whether the prior record helped explain state or rollback. Remove fields nobody uses and stop logging actions that have no shared consequence.

Do not delete a still-relevant incident reference merely to make the log shorter. Retain decisions proportionately and review privacy.

## Communicate without broadcasting

Notify only affected members. A source refresh may need a household message; a private favourite does not.

The [shared governance guide](/blog/the-complete-guide-to-shared-household-media-governance/) defines personal, shared reversible, and shared disruptive levels.

## Common mistakes and limitations

Avoid logging every click, recording private titles by default, storing screenshots indefinitely, omitting rollback, and creating parallel logs with conflicting versions.

A change log supports organisational memory. It does not guarantee recovery, prove causation, or replace official source and Norva support records.

## Test whether the log changes a decision

At review time, take each entry and name the decision it supports: explain a missing category, identify a source refresh, restore an agreed label, or route a support case. Delete fields and old entries that no longer change any action.

## Frequently asked questions

### Should every source refresh be logged?

Log it when it materially changes shared state, follows an incident, or matters for rollback. Routine no-impact actions may not need an entry.

### Can personal favourites appear in the log?

Usually not. Include only a privacy-safe reference when a favourite problem is the material incident.

### Who edits the log?

Assign one coordinator, while change owners supply and verify their entries.
