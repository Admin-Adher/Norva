# How to Review Profile Activity Without Invading Privacy

> **In short:** Start with a specific organisational question, obtain agreement from the profile owner, and inspect only the minimum visible state needed to answer it. Review naming, ownership, accidental mixing, and stale shared contexts rather than analysing private viewing choices. Keep notes purpose-bound, avoid credentials and sensitive titles, share conclusions, and delete temporary records afterward.

A profile-health review should improve household organisation. It should not become surveillance of what another person watches, when they watch, or why.

Agree in advance who will be present, how long the review will remain open, and which decision will close it. If the question can be answered from profile names and ownership alone, do not enter history or recommendation screens.

## Define the legitimate question

Use a narrow prompt such as:

- Is this profile still owned and used?
- Are two profiles being confused?
- Is a shared profile mixing progress unintentionally?
- Does the name expose unnecessary personal information?
- Is a device retaining the wrong account context?

“See everything this person watched” is not an organisational need.

## Ask for consent and participation

Invite the profile owner to review their own context. Explain which screens or fields will be viewed, what decision is being made, and how notes will be handled.

A household account holder may manage the account, but that does not make every private viewing detail necessary for routine maintenance. Review Norva's privacy policy and current household agreement.

## Prefer structural signals

Appropriate signals include:

- profile label and intended owner;
- whether the profile is shared or personal;
- clear evidence of wrong-profile progress;
- duplicate or confusing names;
- devices that intentionally retain sign-in;
- whether language preferences fit the owner;
- whether an offboarding or onboarding action is incomplete.

Avoid copying full history, detailed viewing times, or recommendations unless the owner says they are directly relevant.

## Review on the smallest screen necessary

A shared TV can expose profile history to a room. Use a private supported device when the owner agrees and the task requires detail. Turn off unrelated notification previews through documented controls if appropriate.

The [shared-screen privacy review](/blog/how-to-review-viewing-privacy-on-shared-screens/) covers physical and interface exposure.

## Record conclusions, not a dossier

Write “profile name is confused with shared context” rather than a list of titles. Do not record account email, source URLs, passwords, or recovery codes.

Set a deletion point for temporary notes. A profile-quality decision rarely requires permanent content-level logs.

## Make a proportionate change

Rename a confusing profile, clarify ownership, update an agreed preference, or begin offboarding only after the owner confirms. Do not clear history or favourites merely to make a profile look tidy.

The [household profile quality checklist](/blog/a-household-profile-quality-checklist/) focuses on structure rather than content judgement.

## Communicate the outcome

Tell the profile owner what was observed, what changed, and what did not change. Correct misunderstandings before closing the review.

Use the [complete household profile guide](/blog/the-complete-guide-to-household-media-profiles/) to explain that profiles organise progress, history, favourites, and preferences but are not passwords.

## Common mistakes and limitations

Avoid silent review, collecting full history by default, using a large shared display, retaining screenshots indefinitely, and judging personal viewing choices.

This workflow is a household organisational practice, not legal advice or a technical privacy guarantee. Anyone with access to a signed-in account may still reach profile information.

## Use a stop question

Before opening another screen, ask whether the current structural evidence already answers the legitimate household question. If it does, stop. Additional history adds privacy exposure without improving the decision and should not be collected “just in case.”

## Frequently asked questions

### Can an account holder review every profile?

Account management capability does not make every detail necessary. Use purpose, consent, and minimum evidence.

### What if a profile owner refuses the review?

Address the specific shared-account risk without secretly inspecting unrelated activity. Seek an agreed household or support path.

### Should review notes be permanent?

Usually not. Retain only decisions that help future organisation and remove private temporary evidence.
