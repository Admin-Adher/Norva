# Seven Practical Ways to Secure Your Media Player Account

> **In short:** Use a unique password, secure the email account used for recovery, enable stronger authentication wherever it is actually offered, pair televisions only when you initiated the request, review trusted devices, keep software current, and sign out before giving away a device. These habits reduce avoidable exposure without promising perfect security.

A cross-device account is convenient because it can connect viewing context across phones, browsers, and televisions. The same convenience makes account hygiene important. Norva’s Privacy Policy describes account information, device and pairing records, preferences, and subscription status used to provide the service.

## 1. Use a unique password

Do not reuse the password from email, shopping, or another media service. CISA recommends long, random, unique passwords and a password manager to create and store them.

Norva states that passwords are stored in hashed form by its authentication provider. That server-side practice does not remove the user’s need for a unique secret.

**Observable result:** your Norva password is not used by any other account.

## 2. Protect the recovery email

Someone who controls your email may be able to intercept account messages or recovery steps. Give the email account its own unique password and enable its strongest available authentication option.

**Observable result:** the recovery mailbox has current recovery information and no unrecognised sessions.

## 3. Enable stronger authentication where offered

CISA recommends multifactor authentication because an additional factor can protect an account when a password is compromised. Norva’s public pages do not currently confirm a Norva multifactor control, so do not claim that one exists. Apply the guidance to your email and to any service that actually exposes the option.

**Observable result:** stronger authentication is active on each relevant service that supports it.

## 4. Treat pairing as an approval event

Only approve a TV pairing request that you initiated, while you can see the television. Check that the code and on-screen context match. Do not send pairing codes through chat, email, public posts, or screenshots.

NIST authentication guidance discusses pairing codes and protected authentication channels. A code is a temporary approval mechanism, not something to keep or reuse.

Read [what users should check about TV pairing codes](https://norva.tv/blog/tv-pairing-code-security/) for the full sequence.

## 5. Review trusted devices

Norva’s privacy information confirms that trusted-device records, device tokens, and pairing codes are used so TV and phone can share an account and sync playback. Review the account’s current device controls where available. Remove or sign out devices that are sold, lost, shared permanently, or no longer recognised.

**Observable result:** every retained device has a current owner and purpose.

Use [the trusted-device review guide](https://norva.tv/blog/review-trusted-device-list/) for a repeatable audit.

## 6. Keep devices and applications current

Install operating-system, browser, and application updates from official channels. Updates can contain security and reliability fixes. Avoid unverified downloads, modified installers, and unsolicited “support” links.

**Observable result:** each supported device reports no pending critical update through its official update control.

## 7. Sign out before a device changes hands

Before selling, recycling, returning, or permanently lending a device:

1. sign out of Norva;
2. review trusted-device controls where available;
3. remove locally stored offline media through the supported controls;
4. follow the device maker’s secure erase instructions when ownership changes.

Norva’s Privacy Policy states that offline media is encrypted and stored on the device. Encryption reduces exposure but does not replace responsible device disposal.

## Common mistakes and limitations

- Reusing the same password across services.
- Assuming a profile is a security boundary.
- Approving a pairing code you did not request.
- Claiming Norva offers a security feature not listed publicly.
- Keeping an old television trusted after it changes owners.
- Treating encryption as protection against every unlocked-device risk.

No checklist guarantees security. If you suspect unauthorised access, change relevant credentials through official pages, secure the recovery email, review devices, and contact support.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does Norva offer multifactor authentication?

The current public pages reviewed for this draft do not confirm that feature. Do not claim it is available. Enable stronger authentication on your email and other services wherever it is actually offered.

### Is a household profile password-protected?

Do not assume so. Profiles organise viewer context. Protect access at the account and device level.

### Should I keep a pairing code for later?

No. Treat it as a temporary approval value and never share or archive it in an insecure place.

### What if I no longer recognise a device?

Use the current account controls to remove or sign it out where available, secure your credentials, and contact support if the situation remains unclear.
